## 1. The Onset of Post-Main-Sequence Evolution

### 1.1 Point 2: Core Hydrogen Exhaustion

The post-main-sequence (post-MS) stage begins at **Point 2** on the Hertzsprung–Russell diagram (HRD), defined as the moment of complete exhaustion of hydrogen burning in the stellar core. At this point the star has two chemically distinct regions created naturally by the thermonuclear reactions operating throughout the main sequence (MS):

- **The envelope** retains the initial chemical composition: hydrogen mass fraction $X = 0.70$, helium $Y = 0.28$, metals $Z = 0.02$, giving a mean molecular weight
$$\mu_e = \frac{1}{2X + \frac{3}{4}Y + \frac{1}{2}Z} = 0.63$$
- **The core** has been fully processed by hydrogen burning: $X \approx 0$, $Y = 0.98$, $Z = 0.02$, giving $\mu_i = 4/3 \approx 1.33$.

This **chemical dichotomy** — a hydrogen-rich envelope sitting atop a helium-rich core — is the single most important structural change the star undergoes at the end of the MS. Every subsequent evolutionary feature, from envelope expansion to the Red Giant Branch, flows directly from the consequences of this chemical contrast and the mean-molecular-weight jump it creates. A schematic cross-section of the star at Point 2 shows the helium-rich inner sphere surrounded by a nascent burning shell and a hydrogen-rich outer envelope.

### 1.2 The Chemical Dichotomy and Mean Molecular Weight

The mean molecular weight $\mu$ directly governs the relationship between gas density, temperature, and pressure through the ideal gas equation of state $P = k\rho T/(\mu H)$, where $k$ is Boltzmann's constant and $H$ is the atomic mass unit. Because $\mu_i/\mu_e = (4/3)/0.63 \approx 2.11$, the core gas is roughly twice as "heavy" per particle as the envelope gas at the same temperature and pressure. This difference drives the density gradient mismatch between the two regions, which in turn forces the envelope to expand — as derived in detail in Section 2.

## 2. Why the Envelope Expands: The Density Gradient Analysis

### 2.1 Setting Up the Problem

At the core–envelope boundary (radius $r = r_0$), continuity of the stellar structure requires:
$$P_e = P_i, \quad T_e = T_i, \quad M_e = M_i$$

There is no discontinuity in pressure, temperature, or enclosed mass at this interface. The luminosity condition is more subtle:
- When the shell is **off**: $L_e = L_i$
- When the shell is **active**: $L_e = L_i(1 + u)$, where $u L_i$ is the energy generated by the burning shell and $u > 0$.

From the continuity of pressure and the ideal gas equation of state applied on both sides of the boundary (with $T_e = T_i$):

$$P_i = \frac{k \rho_i T_i}{\mu_i H} = P_e = \frac{k \rho_e T_e}{\mu_e H}$$

This immediately gives the first key relation:

$$\frac{\rho_i}{\rho_e} = \frac{\mu_i}{\mu_e}$$

The core is denser than the envelope by the ratio of their mean molecular weights. For our numbers $\rho_i/\rho_e \approx 2.11$.

### 2.2 Deriving the Density Gradient Ratio

The density gradient is obtained by differentiating the ideal gas equation of state with respect to radius $r$:

$$T \frac{d\rho}{dr} + \rho \frac{dT}{dr} = \frac{\mu H}{k} \frac{dP}{dr}$$

$$\frac{d\rho}{dr} = \frac{1}{T}\left(\frac{\mu H}{k}\frac{dP}{dr} - \rho\frac{dT}{dr}\right)$$

Applying this to both the envelope ($e$) and the core interior ($i$), and using $T_e = T_i$ at the boundary, the ratio of density gradients is:

$$\frac{\left(\frac{d\rho}{dr}\right)_e}{\left(\frac{d\rho}{dr}\right)_i} = \frac{\frac{\mu_e H}{k}\left(\frac{dP}{dr}\right)_e - \rho_e\left(\frac{dT}{dr}\right)_e}{\frac{\mu_i H}{k}\left(\frac{dP}{dr}\right)_i - \rho_i\left(\frac{dT}{dr}\right)_i} \tag{1}$$

This ratio tells us how the density must fall across the envelope relative to how it falls across the core, in order to maintain hydrostatic equilibrium. The analysis proceeds by finding separate expressions for the pressure and temperature gradient ratios.

### 2.3 The Pressure Gradient Ratio

From hydrostatic equilibrium $dP/dr = -GM(r)\rho/r^2$, at the boundary $r_e = r_i$ and $M_e = M_i$:

$$\frac{\left(\frac{dP}{dr}\right)_e}{\left(\frac{dP}{dr}\right)_i} = \frac{\rho_e}{\rho_i} = \frac{\mu_e}{\mu_i}$$

Therefore:
$$\left(\frac{dP}{dr}\right)_e = \left(\frac{dP}{dr}\right)_i \frac{\mu_e}{\mu_i}$$

The pressure gradient in the envelope is smaller than in the core by exactly the mean-molecular-weight ratio, because the envelope is less dense.

### 2.4 The Temperature Gradient Ratio

The radiative temperature gradient from the equation of radiative transport is:
$$\frac{dT}{dr} \propto \frac{\kappa \rho}{T^3}\frac{L}{r^2}$$

The opacity follows a power law: $\kappa \propto (1 + X)\rho^n$, where $n = 0$ corresponds to electron-scattering (Thomson) opacity and $n = 1$ corresponds to Kramers free-free opacity. Taking the ratio, using $T_e = T_i$, $r_e = r_i$, and $L_e = L_i(1+u)$:

$$\frac{\left(\frac{dT}{dr}\right)_e}{\left(\frac{dT}{dr}\right)_i} = (1+u)\frac{(1+X_e)}{(1+X_i)}\left(\frac{\mu_e}{\mu_i}\right)^{n+1}$$

The three factors arise as follows: $(1+u)$ is the shell luminosity enhancement; $(1+X_e)/(1+X_i)$ accounts for the opacity dependence on hydrogen content ($X_i \approx 0$, $X_e = 0.7$, so this ratio equals $1.7$); and $(\mu_e/\mu_i)^{n+1}$ comes from the density-dependence of the opacity factored through the mean molecular weight relation $\rho_e/\rho_i = \mu_e/\mu_i$.

### 2.5 The Master Formula

Substituting all relations into Equation (1) and collecting terms, one defines:
$$\alpha = \frac{\mu_i H}{k}\left(\frac{dP}{dr}\right)_i, \quad \beta = \rho_i\left(\frac{dT}{dr}\right)_i, \quad \gamma = (1+u)\frac{(1+X_e)}{(1+X_i)}\left(\frac{\mu_e}{\mu_i}\right)^n$$

and the density gradient ratio reduces to:

$$\frac{\left(\frac{d\rho}{dr}\right)_e}{\left(\frac{d\rho}{dr}\right)_i} = \left(\frac{\mu_e}{\mu_i}\right)^2 \frac{\alpha - \gamma\beta}{\alpha - \beta}$$

The factor $(\mu_e/\mu_i)^2 = (0.63/1.33)^2 \approx 0.22$ is always less than 1 and provides an irreducible suppression of the envelope density gradient. The second factor $(\alpha - \gamma\beta)/(\alpha - \beta)$ is additionally less than 1 when $\gamma > 1$, making the overall ratio even smaller.

### 2.6 Physical Meaning of the $\gamma$ Parameter

$$\gamma = (1+u)\frac{(1+X_e)}{(1+X_i)}\left(\frac{\mu_e}{\mu_i}\right)^n$$

The parameter $\gamma$ encapsulates two required ingredients for envelope expansion:
- **Shell efficiency factor** $(1+u)$: $u = 0$ means the shell is off; $u > 0$ means it contributes to luminosity. Larger $u$ increases $\gamma$.
- **Chemistry factor** $\frac{(1+X_e)}{(1+X_i)}\left(\frac{\mu_e}{\mu_i}\right)^n$: depends on the hydrogen content difference between envelope and core and on the opacity law exponent $n$.

**ZAMS case:** $X_e = X_i$, $\mu_e = \mu_i$, $u = 0$, so $\gamma = 1$, and the density gradient ratio equals exactly 1. The star has a single smooth density profile — consistent with a homogeneous structure on the ZAMS.

**End-of-MS case:** $X_i \approx 0$, $X_e = 0.7$, $\mu_e = 0.63$, $\mu_i = 4/3$. The chemistry factor evaluates to 1.70 for $n = 0$ and 0.80 for $n = 1$. For $\gamma > 1$ regardless of opacity law, the shell efficiency must satisfy $u > 0.3$ — the shell must contribute at least 30% of the total luminosity. When the shell provides the entire luminosity ($u = 1$, as it does during most of the RGB), $\gamma$ easily exceeds 1.

When $\gamma > 1$:
$$\left(\frac{\mu_e}{\mu_i}\right)^2 \approx 0.22 \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{\alpha - \gamma\beta}{\alpha - \beta} < 1$$

The density gradient ratio therefore falls well below 1 — in fact below $(\mu_e/\mu_i)^2 \approx 0.22$. The envelope density gradient must decrease by at least a factor of 4 relative to the core to maintain hydrostatic equilibrium. The only way to accomplish this physically is to **expand the envelope by at least a factor of 4** in radius. This is the fundamental physical origin of the giant branch.

### 2.7 Two Required Ingredients and Timing

The analysis shows clearly that **both ingredients must be simultaneously present**:
1. **Chemical dichotomy** ($\mu_e \ne \mu_i$, $X_e \ne X_i$): established at Point 2
2. **Active H-burning shell** ($u > 0$): activated at Point 3

Between Points 2 and 3 — chemical dichotomy exists but the shell is not yet active — the star does not yet undergo significant envelope expansion. The HR tracks confirm: it is only at Point 3, when the shell ignites, that the tracks begin their rightward march. Moreover, the effect is proportional to $u$: **as the shell becomes more efficient ($u$ increases), $R$ increases**. At the full RGB stage ($u \approx 1$, shell provides all luminosity), the solicitation on the envelope is maximum.

## 3. Segment 3–4–5: The Sub-Giant Branch and the Hertzsprung Gap

### 3.1 Observational Properties

Starting from Point 3 through Points 4 and 5, all evolutionary tracks move rightward (lower $T_\text{eff}$) in the HRD. The three simultaneous observational signatures are:
- **Surface temperature decreases**
- **Radius increases**
- **Luminosity shows negligible variation (approximately constant)**

From the Stefan–Boltzmann relation $L = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T_e^4$: if $R$ increases while $L$ stays nearly constant, then $T_e$ must decrease — fully consistent with the observed horizontal or slightly declining tracks.

### 3.2 Why Luminosity Stays Nearly Constant

The expanding envelope absorbs some of the shell's energy as mechanical work, acting as a heat sink. The luminosity $L_r$ decreases from the shell outward through the envelope because energy is stored in expansion. This keeps the surface luminosity from rising significantly. In internal structure diagrams of both $1\,M_\odot$ and $5\,M_\odot$ models, $L_r$ shows a gentle downward slope outward from the shell. Additionally, in massive-star models, core contraction contributes a small but non-zero $L_r$ at the stellar center, reflecting gravitational (Kelvin-Helmholtz) energy release supplementing the thermonuclear shell luminosity.

A structural note for the $5\,M_\odot$ model: the outermost 50% of the stellar radius contains only 17% of the total stellar mass — the star is becoming extremely centrally concentrated, with a dense compact core and a vastly extended, diffuse envelope.

### 3.3 The Hertzsprung Gap in Young Stellar Systems

For intermediate and high mass stars ($M \gtrsim 2\,M_\odot$), the crossing of Segment 4–5 is extremely rapid:
- $10^5$ yr for $10\,M_\odot$
- $10^7$ yr for $2\,M_\odot$

Since the number of stars observed in any evolutionary phase is proportional to the time spent in that phase, this rapid crossing produces a **very sparsely populated region** in the CMD of young stellar systems. This region is the **Hertzsprung gap** — a near-empty band running horizontally across the CMD between the main-sequence turnoff and the giant branch. The cluster NGC 1866 (age $\sim 150$ Myr, $M_\text{TO} \sim 4$–$5\,M_\odot$) shows a clear Hertzsprung gap with only a handful of stars crossing it.

In very massive stars ($15\,M_\odot$, $30\,M_\odot$), helium ignition (Point 6) occurs before the star even reaches the Hayashi track, because their cores exceeded the Schönberg–Chandrasekhar limit at birth, triggering almost immediate contraction after core H exhaustion.

### 3.4 The Sub-Giant Branch in Old Stellar Systems

When the crossing time of Segment 4–5 is long enough to populate it, it becomes the **Sub-Giant Branch (SGB)**. The timescale for $1\,M_\odot$ stars is $10^9$ yr — directly comparable to the stellar age itself. Observed examples:
- NGC 1978 ($\sim 2$ Gyr, $M_\text{TO} \sim 1.5\,M_\odot$): well-populated slanting SGB visible
- NGC 5466 ($\sim 13$ Gyr, $M_\text{TO} \sim 0.8\,M_\odot$): nearly horizontal SGB continuously connecting turnoff to the RGB

The existence and population of the SGB in a CMD is a direct age indicator.

## 4. Segment 5–6: The Red Giant Branch

### 4.1 Approach to the Hayashi Track and the RGB Ascent

When the star reaches Point 5, it arrives at its own Hayashi track (HT). The HT is the locus of fully convective stellar models — it runs nearly vertically at $T_\text{eff} \approx 3500$–$4000$ K, with its exact position depending on the stellar mass. The critical physical constraint is:

**No stable hydrostatic stellar models exist to the right of the Hayashi track.**

Once the star is at the HT, the continued solicitation to expand the radius cannot be accommodated by further decrease in $T_\text{eff}$. From Point 5 onward (Segment 5–6), the **luminosity must increase substantially** while the temperature decreases only modestly. The evolutionary track runs approximately parallel to the HT — nearly vertically upward in the HRD. This ascending track is the **Red Giant Branch (RGB)**.

### 4.2 Energy Architecture of the RGB

The RGB is powered **entirely by the H-burning shell**, which provides 100% of the stellar luminosity. The shell is an extremely thin layer (very narrow in mass coordinate) surrounding the helium core. As the shell burns hydrogen, it continuously deposits helium onto the growing core:

- The **helium core mass $M_c^\text{He}$ steadily increases** over time
- A **strong, nearly linear $M_c^\text{He}$–$L$ relation** connects the growing core mass to the rising luminosity
- Consequently, **time and increasing luminosity are directly linked** on the RGB

The shell luminosity scales extremely steeply with mean molecular weight: $L_H \propto \mu^7$. This explains why once the shell finds itself over a region of altered composition (as in the RGB Bump), even a small change in $\mu$ produces a large change in $L$.

From internal structure models of the $1\,M_\odot$ star at Point 5:
- The temperature profile is nearly flat (isothermal) in the core — a signature of electron degeneracy
- At the center, $P_g/P \approx 0.4$: ideal gas provides only 40% of the total pressure, with electron degeneracy providing the remaining 60%. The degree of degeneracy varies with position in the core.
- The luminosity $L_r$ rises from zero at the center with an extremely steep, near-vertical jump at the shell location — reflecting the extraordinary thinness of the shell in mass coordinate.

### 4.3 Development of the Convective Envelope

As the star ascends the RGB parallel to its HT, the outer envelope becomes progressively convective. The base of the convective envelope $M_{ce}$ deepens (moves inward in mass coordinate) as the luminosity increases. A Kippenhahn diagram for a $0.8\,M_\odot$ star shows:
- $M_{ce}$ dropping to a minimum depth early on the RGB, then slowly retreating outward
- $M_{c\text{He}}$ rising monotonically as the shell deposits He onto the core

The convection is important for two reasons: it drives the First Dredge-Up and it establishes the hydrogen abundance discontinuity responsible for the RGB Bump.

## 5. First Dredge-Up

### 5.1 The Process and Its Limits

When the convective envelope penetrates into layers that were previously modified by hydrogen-burning reactions, it dredges chemically processed material up to the stellar surface. This is the **First Dredge-Up** (1° Dredge-Up) — the first of up to three such events during a star's lifetime.

The convection never reaches the active H-burning shell itself. It penetrates only into layers where the chemistry is partially modified — specifically where the CN branch of the CNO cycle has operated. The key constraint: **convection reaches regions of partial CN processing, not the full CNO burning zone**.

### 5.2 Chemical Signatures: The CN Anticorrelation

The CN branch of the CNO cycle converts $^{12}$C into $^{14}$N. The CNO cycle proceeds:
$$^{12}\text{C} \to ^{13}\text{N} \to ^{13}\text{C} \to ^{14}\text{N} \to ^{15}\text{O} \to ^{15}\text{N} \to ^{12}\text{C}$$

The slowest step is $^{14}\text{N} + p$, which causes $^{14}$N to accumulate at equilibrium. The net effect on abundances in CN-processed layers:
- $^{12}$C: decreases by $\sim 30\%$ from initial value
- $^{14}$N: rises to roughly twice the original abundance
- $^{16}$O: barely changes at CN-branch temperatures (requires higher temperatures for the full CNO cycle)

When the convective envelope dredges up this material, the stellar surface shows the **CN anticorrelation**: C decreases while N increases. This is a direct, observable chemical signature of the CNO cycle operating in stellar interiors, detectable spectroscopically in RGB stars.

Primary dredge-up products: **helium** (surface $Y$ increases), depleted $^{12}$C, enhanced $^{14}$N.

### 5.3 The Hydrogen Discontinuity

The inward-penetrating convective envelope homogenizes the composition down to its inner boundary, creating a **sharp vertical step** in the hydrogen abundance profile $X_H(M_r)$ at the mass coordinate of maximum convection depth. Below this step, $X_H$ decreases smoothly inward toward the shell; above it, the convected region is uniform. This discontinuity is frozen into the stellar structure once convection retreats. The outward-moving H-burning shell will encounter this discontinuity at a later time, triggering the RGB Bump.

## 6. The RGB Bump

### 6.1 Mechanism: Shell Crossing the Hydrogen Discontinuity

As the star ascends the RGB, two processes operate simultaneously:
- The H-burning shell moves outward in mass coordinate
- The convective envelope first deepens (establishing the discontinuity), then retreats

When the H-burning shell reaches the hydrogen-abundance discontinuity left by the first dredge-up, the shell suddenly finds itself above a region of **higher hydrogen abundance** (the material previously mixed to a higher $X$ by convection). Because the shell luminosity scales as $L_H \propto \mu^7$, and a higher $X$ means lower $\mu$, the shell luminosity **drops suddenly**. The star temporarily decreases in luminosity — it moves slightly downward in the HRD. After the shell crosses the discontinuity and re-establishes its burning in the well-mixed region above, the luminosity resumes its rise.

The thin shell is especially sensitive to any change in the hydrogen profile because a thin shell has very little inertia and cannot buffer compositional perturbations.

### 6.2 The Three-Crossing of a Luminosity Interval

As a result of the temporary dip, each star **crosses the same narrow luminosity strip three times**:
1. While ascending the RGB before the bump
2. During the temporary luminosity decrease (moving downward)
3. While resuming the ascent after the bump

This triple crossing means stars accumulate near the bump luminosity, spending more time there than on the adjacent portions of the RGB.

### 6.3 Two Observational Consequences

**(1) Bump in the differential luminosity function:** The accumulation of stars at the bump luminosity produces a distinct **local peak** in a histogram of star counts vs. magnitude along the RGB. This "bump" in the differential luminosity function has been detected in many globular clusters (NGC 104, NGC 1261, NGC 1904, NGC 2808, NGC 5272, NGC 5904, NGC 6171, NGC 6397, NGC 6752, NGC 7006, and others).

**(2) Change of slope in the integrated luminosity function:** After crossing the discontinuity, the shell enters a region with more hydrogen (the former convective envelope material), so the fuel supply effectively increased. The evolutionary speed above the bump is **faster** than below. This change in speed manifests as a **change in slope** of the cumulative (integrated) luminosity function at the bump magnitude — detectable as a kink in the log-count vs. magnitude plot.

Both signatures have been confirmed observationally, validating the theoretical picture of the RGB Bump mechanism.

## 7. Electron Degeneracy in the He Core and the Transition Mass $M = 2.2\,M_\odot$

### 7.1 Which Stars Develop a Degenerate Core?

After H-burning ends, the helium core contracts and heats up. The boundary between ideal gas and non-relativistic degenerate electron gas is:
$$\log T = \frac{2}{3}\log\rho + 5$$

Stars whose core conditions cross to the right of this line (toward lower $T$ at fixed $\rho$, or higher $\rho$ at fixed $T$) become degenerate. Degenerate electrons exert pressure $P \propto \rho^{5/3}$ independent of temperature — this dramatically resists further compression and delays or permanently halts contraction.

### 7.2 The Transition Mass: $M = 2.2\,M_\odot$

The transition mass is determined computationally by evolving grids of models and finding which mass just touches the degeneracy boundary before helium ignition. **$M = 2.2\,M_\odot$** is that critical mass:

**Stars with $M < 2.2\,M_\odot$:**
- The He-core enters the degenerate regime after H-burning while on the RGB
- Degeneracy delays helium ignition substantially, prolonging the RGB
- When He ignites, it does so in semi-degenerate conditions — the Helium Flash
- These stars undergo the degenerate He-core phase **twice**: temporarily during the RGB, then permanently after He-burning (as a CO white dwarf precursor)

**Stars with $M > 2.2\,M_\odot$:**
- The He-core remains in the ideal gas regime throughout
- Smooth thermoregulation permits stable He ignition without instabilities (Point 6 at moderate luminosity)
- Evolution along the RGB is faster; the giant branch is shorter and more poorly populated

Stars with $2.2 < M/M_\odot < 8$ still develop a **degenerate CO-core** after He-burning (the second degeneracy event), which ultimately leads to CO white dwarfs. Only stars above $8\,M_\odot$ remain non-degenerate throughout all nuclear burning stages.

### 7.3 Consequences for Giant Branch Observability

| $M > 2.2\,M_\odot$ | $M < 2.2\,M_\odot$ |
|---|---|
| Non-degenerate He-core | Degenerate He-core |
| Rapid RGB evolution | Slow RGB evolution |
| Short, poorly populated giant branch | Extended, well-populated RGB |
| He ignition at moderate $L$ (not at the RGB tip) | He ignition at maximum luminosity = RGB Tip |

### 7.4 Summary Table: Mass-Dependent Histories and Final Fates

| Mass Range | He-core | CO-core | Final Fate |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| $0.08 < M/M_\odot < 0.5$ | Fully degenerate; no He ignition | — | He-WDs |
| $0.5 < M/M_\odot < 2.2$ | Degenerate; He-flash; then He burns | Degenerate | CO-WDs |
| $2.2 < M/M_\odot < 8$ | Non-degenerate; stable He ignition | Degenerate | CO-WDs |
| $M > 8\,M_\odot$ | Never degenerate | Never degenerate | Type II SN |

## 8. The Helium Flash

### 8.1 Thermonuclear Instability in a Degenerate Core

In an **ideal gas**, increased temperature → increased pressure → expansion → cooling: a negative feedback loop (thermoregulation) that stabilizes nuclear burning. In a **degenerate gas**, pressure is independent of temperature. No negative feedback exists:
- Temperature increases
- $\varepsilon_{3\alpha} \propto T^{25}$ rises dramatically
- More energy released → temperature rises further → runaway

This thermonuclear runaway in the degenerate He-core is the **Helium Flash**. The peak energy release within the core is roughly equivalent to $L \sim 10^{11}\,L_\odot$.

**Critical physical fact:** This enormous energy is entirely consumed within the core to remove the electron degeneracy (via core expansion). It **never propagates to the stellar surface** and produces **no observable increase in photospheric luminosity**. The star at the RGB tip is not visibly brighter during the Flash.

### 8.2 Why the Flash Occurs Off-Center: Neutrino Cooling

A degenerate He-core at temperatures approaching $\sim 10^8$ K generates neutrinos through plasma processes:
- Plasma process, photoneutrino process: $\gamma + e^- \to e^- + \nu + \bar\nu$
- Pair annihilation neutrinos

Neutrinos escape essentially freely (negligible interaction cross-section with stellar matter). This **neutrino cooling** selectively cools the innermost core region (which first reaches the neutrino-producing temperatures), creating a **temperature inversion**: the center is cooler than some intermediate radius. The temperature profile develops an **off-center maximum**.

The 3$\alpha$ reaction ignites where $T$ is highest — therefore **the Main Flash occurs off-center**, not at $r = 0$.

### 8.3 The Cascade of Sub-Flashes

The He-flash is a sequence of multiple events:

1. **Main Flash (off-center):** The enormous energy drives convection, which expands the shell of the core above the flash location. Density decreases, degeneracy is lifted in that shell.

2. **Second Flash:** The inner core (below the Main Flash location) remains degenerate. A second, smaller flash occurs at the new degenerate–non-degenerate boundary.

3. **Progressive cascade:** Each subsequent flash lifts degeneracy from the next innermost layer, working progressively toward the center until the entire core is in the ideal gas regime.

The full cascade spans $\sim 10^6$ yr. During this time, only $\sim 5\%$ of the helium is converted to carbon. The flashes are not the main helium-burning event; they are a degeneracy-removal mechanism that prepares the core for stable burning.

Condition to remove electron degeneracy:
$$\frac{T}{\rho^{2/3}} > 10^5$$

The energy of the flashes first raises $T$ at constant $P$ (degenerate EoS), until this condition is satisfied and the gas transitions to the ideal gas regime.

### 8.4 Post-Flash Adjustment and the ZAHB

After all degeneracy is removed:
- **At the Flash (A):** RGB Tip, maximum luminosity. Core conditions: $\rho_c \sim 10^6\,\text{g}\,\text{cm}^{-3}$, $T_c \sim 8 \times 10^7\,\text{K}$.
- **Adjustment Phase (A → B):** Core expansion cools the H-burning shell, reducing its efficiency. Total stellar luminosity drops. The star descends in the HRD.
- **After adjustment (B → C = ZAHB):** Stable 3$\alpha$ burning begins in the non-degenerate core ($\rho_c \sim 10^4\,\text{g}\,\text{cm}^{-3}$, $T_c \sim 10^8\,\text{K}$), and the H-shell reactivates. The star settles on the Zero Age Horizontal Branch.

### 8.5 Universality of the Flash Core Mass

Because the physics of the degenerate core sets the conditions for He ignition independently of the total stellar mass, **all stars with $M < 2.2\,M_\odot$ ignite the 3$\alpha$ process at the same helium core mass**:

$$M_c^\text{He} \approx 0.5\,M_\odot$$

This holds regardless of whether the star began as $0.8\,M_\odot$ or $2.0\,M_\odot$. Combined with the tight $M_c^\text{He}$–$L$ relation on the RGB, all these stars reach the **same maximum luminosity before the Flash** — they all terminate the RGB at the same luminosity. This universality is the foundation of the RGB Tip standard candle.

Stars with $M < 0.5\,M_\odot$ never accumulate enough helium core mass ($\approx 0.5\,M_\odot$ threshold) to ignite the 3$\alpha$ process and are destined to become He-WDs without reaching the HB.

## 9. The RGB Tip as a Standard Candle

### 9.1 Constant RGB Tip Luminosity

For all stars with $M < 2.2\,M_\odot$ (stellar populations older than $\sim 1$–$2$ Gyr), the terminal point of the RGB occurs at the same luminosity:
$$\log(L/L_\odot) \approx 3.45$$

In the $I$-band (preferred because cool red giants emit most flux in the red/near-IR, minimizing differential extinction):
$$M_I \approx -4.0$$

### 9.2 Using the TRGB as a Distance Indicator

The **Tip of the RGB (TRGB)** is a powerful primary distance indicator for extragalactic stellar systems:
$$(m - M)_I = m_I - (-4.0) = m_I + 4.0$$

Example calculation (null extinction):
- Observed TRGB: $m_I = 22.5$
- Distance modulus: $(m-M)_0 = 22.5 - (-4.0) = 26.5$
- $5\log d - 5 = 26.5 \implies \log d = 6.3 \implies d = 2\,\text{Mpc}$

**Requirements:**
- Stellar population must be older than $\sim 1$–$2$ Gyr (to have stars in the low-mass, degenerate-flash regime)
- Must correct for interstellar extinction (use $I$-band or near-IR where extinction is minimized)
- There is a weak metallicity dependence that must be accounted for

**Detection:** The TRGB appears as a sharp, abrupt edge in the RGB luminosity function — a dramatic drop in star counts at the tip magnitude. This has been detected in resolved stellar populations of many nearby galaxies (Karachentsev et al. 2006: ESO 349-031, KKH 6, NGC 247; and many others).

### 9.3 The RGB Phase Transition

When the turnoff mass crosses below $2.2\,M_\odot$ (occurring at ages $\sim 1$–$2$ Gyr), a well-populated, extended RGB suddenly appears in the CMD. This is the **RGB Phase Transition (RGB Ph-T)**, one of the most important events in the evolution of a stellar population's integrated light.

Before the RGB Ph-T (younger populations):
- The giant branch is short and poorly populated
- Integrated light dominated by hot, luminous MS stars
- Integrated colors are blue

After the RGB Ph-T (populations older than $\sim 1$–$2$ Gyr):
- A luminous, well-populated RGB dominates the evolved population
- Red giants contribute the majority of the integrated bolometric luminosity
- Integrated colors become red

In population synthesis models (Renzini & Buzzoni 1986; Maraston 1998), the fractional RGB contribution to total bolometric luminosity shows a sharp, steep rise at $\log t \approx 9.0$ (1 Gyr), crossing and overwhelming the MS contribution at older ages.

**Diagnostic power:** A pronounced, well-populated RGB in a stellar aggregate definitively indicates the presence of a sub-population **older than 1–2 Gyr**. A blue plume of hot MS stars indicates a young population. The two features in a CMD can be "read" directly in terms of the star formation history of the stellar system.

## 10. Mass Loss on the RGB

### 10.1 Physical Basis

Mass loss occurs preferentially when the **surface gravity is low** — i.e., when the stellar radius is large:
$$g = \frac{GM}{R^2}$$

For comparison: $\log g = 4.4$ on the MS (solar values), $\log g = -0.5$ for a red giant. Surface material is far less tightly bound in the giant phase. The general scaling of mass loss rate expected from stellar physics:
$$\dot{M} \propto \frac{LR}{M}$$

Both $L$ and $R$ are large and increasing on the upper RGB, while $M$ provides gravitational binding. Mass loss is therefore most significant in the brightest part of the RGB.

### 10.2 The Reimers Law

The most widely used empirical prescription is the **Reimers (1975) law**:
$$\dot{M} = -4 \times 10^{-13}\,\eta\,\frac{L}{gR}\,M_\odot\,\text{yr}^{-1}$$

where $L$ is in $L_\odot$, $g$ is the surface gravity, $R$ in $R_\odot$, and $\eta$ is the **mass loss efficiency parameter** — a dimensionless free parameter calibrated from observations. Typical calibrated values: $\eta \sim 0.2$–$0.5$ for RGB mass loss. There is no first-principles theory for mass loss; $\eta$ represents fundamental uncertainty in stellar evolution models.

We do not yet have a solid theoretical understanding of the mass loss mechanism itself. In low-mass stars, the **total RGB mass loss is estimated at $\sim 30$–$40\%$ of the initial mass**. In high-mass stars it can be even larger.

### 10.3 Consequences for Subsequent Evolution

The mass remaining after RGB mass loss is the **evolutionary mass** $M_E = M_\text{ZAMS} - M_\text{ML}$. This critically determines:
- Position on the ZAHB (through the parameter $q = M_c/M_E$)
- Whether the star evolves as a normal HB star or an EHB star

If mass loss is very severe (the star loses too much mass), it may leave the RGB before the He-core reaches $0.5\,M_\odot$ → evolves directly into a **He-WD** without He ignition. If it loses enough mass to leave only a very thin envelope at the ZAHB, it becomes an **EHB / AGB-manqué star**.

## 11. The Zero Age Horizontal Branch (ZAHB) and He-Burning Phase

### 11.1 The ZAHB as an Analog of the ZAMS

The **Zero Age Horizontal Branch (ZAHB)** is the locus in the HRD where stars have just established stable helium burning in a non-degenerate core. It is directly analogous to the ZAMS:

| ZAMS | ZAHB |
|---|---|
| Core H-burning just ignited | Core He-burning just ignited |
| Stars of different mass arranged along a sequence | Stars of different evolutionary mass arranged along a sequence |
| Starting line for H-burning evolution | Starting line for He-burning evolution |
| Position determined by total mass | Position determined by ratio $q = M_c/M_E$ |

The ZAHB is not an evolutionary track — it is a reference sequence from which stars evolve as helium is consumed.

### 11.2 Structure of a ZAHB Star

The stellar structure from center outward:
1. **Inner He-burning convective core**: $^4\text{He} \to ^{12}\text{C}$ (with eventual $^{16}\text{O}$ production)
2. **He-rich layer**: helium deposited by past H-burning
3. **H-burning shell**: $\text{H} \to \text{He}$, still active
4. **H-rich envelope**: hydrogen-rich outer layers

Both energy sources (He core + H shell) contribute to the total luminosity. The H-shell contribution dominates for intermediate-mass stars throughout essentially the entire He-burning phase: the ratio $L_H/L_\text{He} \gg 1$ for most of the evolution, especially for $7\,M_\odot$ models. Only when He is almost entirely exhausted does the He-shell burning contribution approach the H-shell contribution.

### 11.3 The Parameter $q$ and HB Position

For all stars with $0.5 < M/M_\odot < 2.2$ (which suffered the He-flash), the core mass is fixed:
$$M_c \approx 0.5\,M_\odot$$

The evolutionary mass $M_E$ varies due to different ZAMS masses and different RGB mass loss histories. Therefore $q = M_c/M_E$ varies, and:
- **Higher $M_E$ → smaller $q$ → thicker H-envelope → lower $T_\text{eff}$ → redder, cooler HB position**
- **Lower $M_E$ → higher $q$ → thinner H-envelope → higher $T_\text{eff}$ → bluer, hotter HB position**

This is the fundamental mechanism linking mass loss history and initial mass to HB color.

### 11.4 ZAHB Luminosity as a Distance Indicator

The luminosity of the ZAHB depends on $M_c^\text{He}$. Since $M_c \approx 0.5\,M_\odot$ for all low-mass stars, $L_\text{ZAHB}$ is the same for all these stars (at fixed metallicity). The ZAHB can therefore be used as a distance indicator:

$$M_V(\text{ZAHB}) \approx +0.5 \quad \text{(metal-poor globular clusters)}$$

with a metallicity correction applied. Distance determined by comparing observed apparent magnitude of the ZAHB with this absolute magnitude. This method requires that metallicity effects are properly modeled.

### 11.5 He-Burning Timescales

The He-burning timescale is much shorter than H-burning because: (a) the star is $\sim 100\times$ more luminous; (b) the energy yield per unit mass from He-burning ($\approx 5.9 \times 10^{17}$ erg/g) is roughly 10 times less than H-burning ($\approx 6.3 \times 10^{18}$ erg/g); (c) neutrino losses carry away some energy.

$$t_\text{He} \approx \begin{cases} 4\,\text{Myr} & 10\,M_\odot \\ 22\,\text{Myr} & 5\,M_\odot \\ 100\,\text{Myr} & 1\,M_\odot \end{cases}$$

For comparison, the $1\,M_\odot$ MS lifetime is $\sim 10\,\text{Gyr}$ — two orders of magnitude longer. Most of the He-burning time is spent near the ZAHB, analogously to how most MS time is spent near the ZAMS.

## 12. Horizontal Branch Morphology

### 12.1 The Diversity of HB Morphologies

The He-burning phase shows the most diverse observational appearance of any major stellar evolutionary stage. Different stellar populations display:
- **Red Clump / Helium Clump**: compact group at cool temperatures (e.g., 47 Tuc, NGC 1978). Typical of intermediate-age, high-metallicity populations.
- **Classical Horizontal Branch**: stars distributed from red to moderately blue (e.g., M5, M13, many metal-poor GCs)
- **Extended Blue Tail / Extreme HB**: stars at very high $T_\text{eff}$, sometimes called EHB stars (e.g., $\omega$ Cen, NGC 2808, M80)

In bolometric magnitudes, all HB stars have approximately the same luminosity. The diversity appears in visual photometry because cool stars (low $T_\text{eff}$) and hot stars (high $T_\text{eff}$) emit their energy at very different wavelengths. A cool red giant emits primarily in the optical; a hot EHB star emits primarily in the UV. In an $R$-band CMD, the hot blue EHB stars appear very faint (most of their flux is in UV, not sampled by $R$), creating the appearance of a near-vertical blue tail descending to fainter magnitudes — even though their bolometric luminosities are comparable to the cooler HB stars.

### 12.2 Parameters Controlling HB Morphology

The HB color distribution depends on all factors affecting $M_E$ (the evolutionary mass at the ZAHB):

| Parameter (increasing) | Effect on $M_E$ / envelope | Effect on $T_\text{eff}$ | HB morphology |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Age** | Lower $M_\text{ZAMS}$ at fixed turnoff → lower $M_E$ → thinner envelope | Higher $T_\text{eff}$ | **Bluer HB** |
| **Metallicity** | Higher $Z$ → opacity effects → effectively thicker envelope dominates | Lower $T_\text{eff}$ | **Redder HB** |
| **Helium abundance $Y$** | Higher $Y$ → lower $M_\text{ZAMS}$ at same evolutionary age → smaller $M_E$ → thinner envelope | Higher $T_\text{eff}$ | **Bluer HB** |
| **Mass loss** | More mass lost on RGB → smaller $M_E$ → thinner envelope | Higher $T_\text{eff}$ | **Bluer HB** |

The **second parameter problem**: Two clusters with the same age and comparable metallicity (e.g., NGC 6388 vs. NGC 0104 = 47 Tuc) can have completely different HB morphologies — NGC 6388 has an extended blue HB while 47 Tuc has a red clump only. This demonstrates that at least one parameter beyond age and metallicity governs HB morphology. The leading candidates are helium abundance spread and mass loss efficiency, but the problem remains a subject of active research.

### 12.3 EHB Stars, the AGB-Manqué, and the UV Upturn

**EHB stars** have so thin a hydrogen envelope that the H-shell cannot operate efficiently during the subsequent AGB phase. As a result, EHB stars **bypass the AGB entirely**:
$$\text{EHB} \to \text{AGB-manqué} \to \text{Post-HB blue subdwarf} \to \text{WD cooling track}$$

In old elliptical galaxies and spiral bulges, essentially all MS stars have evolved off by ages $>10$ Gyr. The UV flux of the integrated SED should be negligible — yet observations of the M31 nucleus and other elliptical galaxies reveal a significant **UV upturn**: an unexpected rise in flux at wavelengths $< 1500$ Å. EHB stars (and their post-HB descendants) have been proposed as the responsible population (Greggio & Renzini 1990). Understanding the UV upturn thus requires understanding what drives EHB star formation in old populations — a problem directly connected to HB morphology, mass loss, and helium enhancement.

## 13. Convective Core Growth During He-Burning: Overshooting and Semi-Convection

### 13.1 The Convective Core and the Schwarzschild Criterion

The steep temperature dependence of the 3$\alpha$ process ($\varepsilon_{3\alpha} \propto T^{25}$) naturally generates a **fully convective core**. The Schwarzschild criterion for convection is:
$$\nabla_\text{rad} > \nabla_\text{ad} \implies \text{Convection}$$
$$\nabla_\text{rad} < \nabla_\text{ad} \implies \text{Radiative (no convection)}$$

The Schwarzschild criterion sets the formal **convective border** where the acceleration of convective cells is zero:
$$\nabla_\text{rad} = \nabla_\text{ad} \implies a = 0, \quad v \ne 0$$

The velocity of convective cells at the border is **not** zero — it only becomes zero some distance beyond the formal border. Cells with non-zero velocity carry their momentum across the formal Schwarzschild boundary, penetrating into the nominally radiative zone. This creates an **extra-mixing region** beyond the formal convective border: **overshooting**.

### 13.2 The Self-Driving Mechanism: Growing Convective Core

During H-burning in massive MS stars, as H decreases and He increases, the opacity drops (because He is more transparent than H under Kramers opacity: $\kappa_\text{FF} \propto (X+Y)(1+X)\rho/T^{3.5}$, and He-dominated plasma has lower opacity). Lower $\kappa$ → lower $\nabla_\text{rad}$ → the convective region **shrinks** over time.

During He-burning, the opposite occurs. As He is converted to C and O, the mean ionic charge $Z$ increases dramatically. Free-free opacity scales as:
$$\kappa_\text{FF} = D_\text{FF}(Z)\frac{1}{u^3}, \quad D_\text{FF}(Z) \propto Z^2$$

A CO plasma is far more opaque than a He plasma. As He depletes and C,O accumulate: $\kappa \nearrow \implies \nabla_\text{rad} \nearrow \implies$ the convective region **expands** over time. This self-driving mechanism causes the convective core to grow continuously during He-burning.

### 13.3 The Overshooting Problem and Its Parametrization

The Schwarzschild criterion alone is insufficient to describe the convective boundary because:
1. The cell velocity at the formal border is non-zero → cells overshoot
2. The convective core is growing → overshooting must be tracked dynamically

The overshooting is parametrized by the **free parameter $\Lambda_\text{ov}$**:
$$\text{Example: } \Lambda_\text{ov} = 1.25 \implies 25\% \text{ extra mixing beyond the Schwarzschild border}$$

$\Lambda_\text{ov}$ must be specified for every convective region at every evolutionary stage. It is a free parameter whose value must be constrained by comparison with observations (e.g., the location of the MS width, the morphology of the helium burning loop).

**Effect of including overshooting:**
- More extended mixed region → more helium fuel available → **longer He-burning phase**
- More pronounced loop toward higher surface temperatures in the HRD
- More realistic, physically continuous transition in $\nabla_\text{rad}$ across the convective border (avoiding the unphysical sharp discontinuity that appears without overshooting)

### 13.4 Semi-Convection: The Three-Zone Structure

If overshooting is not included, the radiative temperature gradient $\nabla_\text{rad}$ develops a sharp, unphysical discontinuity at the convective core boundary as C and O accumulate — the gradient becomes increasingly steep with time due to the increasing opacity. Including overshooting mixes C and O outward into the He-rich zone while bringing fresh He inward:

**Step 1:** Overshooting extends the mixed region, bringing opaque C,O outward and fresh He inward into the core, temporarily increasing He in the core and decreasing $\nabla_\text{rad}$.

**Step 2:** The systematic decrease in $\nabla_\text{rad}$ causes it to develop a **minimum** in the gradient profile. When this minimum touches $\nabla_\text{ad}$, the convective region splits into two non-communicating sub-regions: (a) an inner fully convective region and (b) an outer region where $\nabla_\text{rad} \approx \nabla_\text{ad}$.

**Step 3:** In region (b), the convection is marginally stable — partially mixed but not fully convective. This is the **semi-convective zone** ($\nabla_\text{rad} \sim \nabla_\text{ad}$, partial mixing maintains the gradient equality).

The resulting three-zone structure:
1. **Fully convective core**: extends to the position of the minimum in $\nabla_\text{rad}$
2. **Semi-convective region**: $\nabla_\text{rad} \approx \nabla_\text{ad}$; partially mixed He+C+O material
3. **Radiative region**: He-rich, outside the semi-convective zone

Helium abundance profiles in $5\,M_\odot$ models show this structure directly: completely flat $X_4$ (He mass fraction) in the fully convective core, then a gradual slope through the semi-convective zone, then a flat profile in the radiative He envelope.

## 14. He-Burning Evolution in the HRD: Loop Morphology

### 14.1 The HB/He-Burning Loop

After leaving the ZAHB, stars undergo a characteristic loop in the HRD during the He-burning phase. This loop results from competition between the He-core burning and the H-shell burning:

**(A) First phase — He-core expansion dominates:** The convection generated by the 3$\alpha$ process drives expansion of the He-burning core. Because the H-burning shell sits above the expanding core, it is pushed outward to higher radii where the temperature is slightly lower — reducing the shell's efficiency. The envelope, losing some of its energy source from below, **contracts**. Surface temperature **increases** (star moves blueward in the HRD). This is segment A of the HB loop.

**(B) Second phase — core begins to contract:** When the He core expansion slows and the core begins to contract (as He depletes), the 3$\alpha$ rate increases, and the core's growing energy output drives the **envelope to expand**. Surface temperature **decreases** (star moves redward in the HRD). This is segment B of the loop.

**For EHB stars (negligible H envelope):** Segment A is essentially absent because there is no significant H-shell to interact with the core expansion. EHB stars evolve almost directly without a pronounced blue loop.

### 14.2 Return to the Asymptotic Giant Branch

When helium in the convective core is exhausted, the 3$\alpha$ energy production ceases. The inert CO core contracts, the envelope expands again, and the star moves toward the Hayashi track for the second time. This second ascent of the giant branch is the **Asymptotic Giant Branch (AGB)**:

$$\text{Sequence I: MS} \to \text{RGB} \quad \text{(H-shell driven)}$$
$$\text{Sequence II: HB} \to \text{AGB} \quad \text{(He-shell driven, then double-shell)}$$

## 15. He-Burning in Massive Stars ($M > 2.2\,M_\odot$)

### 15.1 Stable, Non-Degenerate He Ignition

For stars more massive than $2.2\,M_\odot$, the absence of electron degeneracy in the He-core allows **stable, thermoregulated He ignition** without any instability. The required conditions at ignition: $T \sim 10^8$ K, $\rho \sim 10^4\,\text{g}\,\text{cm}^{-3}$.

Even without a flash, He-ignition terminates the RGB evolution and brings the star into the He-burning region (Segment F–G–H–K–I for a $5\,M_\odot$ model):
- **Point E → G:** Shell luminosity first decreases (core expansion by convection reduces shell efficiency), then increases
- **Point G:** Maximum efficiency of the H-shell
- **G → I:** $L_H \searrow$ and $L_\text{He} \nearrow$
- **Point K:** $L_\text{He} \approx 60\%$ of $L_\text{tot}$

### 15.2 The CO Core Composition

The 3$\alpha$ process produces $^{12}$C, and the reaction $^{12}\text{C} + \alpha \to ^{16}\text{O} + \gamma$ then produces $^{16}$O. The relative rates are:
- $^{3}\alpha$ rate $\propto \rho^2 Y^3$ (three-body)
- $^{12}\text{C} + \alpha$ rate $\propto \rho Y$ (two-body)

Early in He-burning ($Y$ large), $^{12}$C production dominates. As $Y$ decreases, the $^{12}\text{C} + \alpha$ rate becomes relatively more important. The final CO core contains **more $^{16}$O than $^{12}$C** — oxygen dominates by the end of He-burning. The exact C/O ratio is uncertain due to uncertainties in the $^{12}\text{C} + \alpha$ reaction cross-section at stellar energies.

## 16. The Asymptotic Giant Branch (AGB)

### 16.1 Structure of the AGB Star

At the onset of the AGB, the stellar interior consists (from center outward):
1. **CO core**: inert, contracting; all He has been exhausted here
2. **He-burning shell**: $^4\text{He} \to ^{12}\text{C},^{16}\text{O}$; first to ignite at the base of the He layer
3. **He-rich intershell region**: helium left over from the H-burning shell phase
4. **H-burning shell**: $\text{H} \to \text{He}$; will alternate with the He-shell
5. **H-He envelope**: hydrogen-rich outer layers

The AGB is a **double-shell burning phase**: two burning shells alternate in activity, each one's ashes serving as the other's fuel.

### 16.2 The M$^\text{up} = 8\,M_\odot$ Transition Mass

After He-burning, the CO-core contracts. Its fate depends on whether it becomes degenerate:

**$M < 8\,M_\odot$:** The CO-core becomes electron-degenerate. Degeneracy slows and eventually halts further nuclear burning. The star undergoes the AGB + Thermal Pulse phase and eventually ejects its envelope as a **Planetary Nebula**, leaving a **CO White Dwarf**.

**$M > 8\,M_\odot$:** The CO-core remains non-degenerate. Carbon ignites stably ($T \sim 8 \times 10^8$ K), initiating further nuclear burning all the way to iron. The star eventually undergoes **Type II (Core Collapse) Supernova**.

The mass $M^\text{up} = 8\,M_\odot$ is the largest mass at which electron degeneracy in the CO-core is sufficient to **prevent carbon ignition**. Stars with $7$–$11\,M_\odot$ occupy a fuzzy transition zone where the outcome (O-Mg WD, electron-capture SN, or Type II SN) depends sensitively on the exact mass and uncertain physics.

## 17. Early AGB (E-AGB) and the Second Dredge-Up

### 17.1 E-AGB Structure

The **Early AGB (E-AGB)** begins when the He-shell first ignites. The ignition of the He-shell (innermost) drives the expansion of the layers above it. This expansion cools the H-shell, which is then **switched off**. The star moves toward the Hayashi track. The outer envelope, now receiving less energy from below, becomes increasingly convective from outside.

### 17.2 The Second Dredge-Up

The inward penetration of the convective envelope during the E-AGB is the **Second Dredge-Up** (2° Dredge-Up). Unlike the First Dredge-Up, the second event is significant mainly in more massive stars:

**Models show: only in stars with $M > 4$–$5\,M_\odot$ does convection penetrate deep enough to bring H-burning processed material to the surface.**

Chemistry of the dredged-up material is similar to the First Dredge-Up:
- Enriched in **helium** and $^{14}$N (CN-processed material)
- Depleted in $^{12}$C
- The surface chemistry is further modified toward the CN-cycle equilibrium composition

The He-shell burning phase then continues quiescently, converting all the He in the intershell region to C. As the He-shell approaches the H-He discontinuity (the H-shell is not active at this point), it extinguishes. The intershell contraction allows the H-shell to reignite. This sets the stage for the thermal pulse phase.

## 18. The Thermal Pulse AGB (TP-AGB)

### 18.1 The Basic Cycle

The **Thermal Pulse AGB (TP-AGB)** is the advanced, high-luminosity stage of AGB evolution characterized by the **alternating ignition of the H- and He-burning shells**. It lasts until the envelope is ejected in the Planetary Nebula phase.

The cycle:
1. **H-shell active, He-shell dormant**: H-shell burns hydrogen, depositing fresh He into the intershell region. As the He layer accumulates, its mass grows.

2. **He-shell ignites (Thermal Pulse)**: When the mass of the He-rich intershell region reaches a critical value ($\sim 10^{-2}\,M_\odot$ for a $0.6\,M_\odot$ CO core), the He-shell re-ignites. The ignition is a thermonuclear runaway (minor flash, analogous to the He-flash but much less intense): $L_\text{He} \sim 10^6$–$10^7\,L_\odot$ is reached briefly.

3. **He-shell burning drives convection and expansion**: The energy from the He-shell pulse drives **powerful convection throughout the intershell region**, thoroughly mixing it. The intershell region expands, cooling and pushing the H-shell to lower temperatures — the H-shell is **switched off**.

4. **He-shell fades, H-shell reignites**: The He-shell consumes its fuel (the He deposited by the H-shell), gradually fades, and the intershell contracts. The H-shell reignites, and the cycle repeats.

### 18.2 Why the He-Shell Produces a Runaway

The He-shell thermonuclear runaway occurs despite the gas being only **poorly degenerate**, for a different reason than the main He-flash. For a burning shell of thickness $s$ at radius $r$:
$$\frac{dP}{P} = 4\frac{s}{r}\frac{d\rho}{\rho}$$

In a stable burning process, extra energy causes expansion (decrease in $\rho$) and cooling — normal thermoregulation. But if the shell is very thin ($s/r \ll 1$), the pressure changes little with density. In this case:
$$\frac{d\rho}{\rho}\left(4\frac{s}{r} - \alpha\right) = \beta\frac{dT}{T}$$

When $4s/r < \alpha$, a decrease in density can produce an **increase in temperature** — the opposite of normal behavior. Combined with the extreme temperature sensitivity of the 3$\alpha$ process ($\varepsilon \propto T^{25}$), this thin-shell instability produces a thermonuclear runaway in the He-shell.

### 18.3 Timescales of Thermal Pulses

The inter-pulse period (time between successive thermal pulses) depends strongly on the CO-core mass:
$$t_\text{TP} \approx 2 \times 10^5\,\text{yr} \quad \text{for } 0.6\,M_\odot \text{ CO core}$$
$$t_\text{TP} \approx 10^3\,\text{yr} \quad \text{for } 5\,M_\odot \text{ CO core}$$

More massive cores have shorter inter-pulse periods. In luminosity–time diagrams, the H-shell luminosity ($L_H$) shows a relatively steady, high value with brief dips during each pulse, while the He-shell luminosity ($L_\text{He}$) shows a nearly flat baseline punctuated by sharp, massive spikes (each pulse). Every spike in $L_\text{He}$ corresponds to a dip in $L_H$ — the anti-phased alternation is a hallmark of the TP-AGB.

## 19. The Third Dredge-Up and the Formation of Carbon Stars

### 19.1 The Third Dredge-Up Mechanism

The **Third Dredge-Up** (3° Dredge-Up) is the most spectacular and chemically important mixing event in stellar evolution. It requires the **combination of two separate convective processes**:

1. **Intershell convection** (driven by the He-shell pulse): During each thermal pulse, the powerful convective motions generated by the He-shell completely mix the intershell region, homogenizing it and **enriching it with C and O** (the products of He-burning). This is the "inner convection" event.

2. **Envelope convection** (driven by envelope expansion): As the AGB star approaches the Hayashi track, the convective envelope deepens. During and after each thermal pulse — particularly when the H-shell is switched off and the intershell is still enriched and mixed — the envelope convection can **penetrate deeply enough to reach the intershell region**.

These two events are **not simultaneous**: the inner convection (He-shell pulse) occurs first and enriches the intershell; then, after the inner convection ceases, the outer convective envelope deepens and contacts the enriched intershell region. The material from the intershell (rich in C and O from He-burning) is then carried to the stellar surface.

### 19.2 Carbon Stars

Repeated Third Dredge-Up events progressively enrich the stellar surface in carbon. Initially, stellar surfaces are oxygen-rich (C/O < 1 by number). Each dredge-up episode adds C to the surface. After enough episodes, the surface can reach C/O > 1 — at which point the star becomes a **Carbon Star**: one of the chemically most distinctive types of evolved star. Carbon stars show strong C$_2$, CN, and CH molecular bands in their spectra.

The Third Dredge-Up is thus the mechanism connecting the deep interior nuclear burning to the observable surface chemistry of AGB stars, and ultimately to the chemical enrichment of the interstellar medium.

## 20. The s-Process: Heavy Element Nucleosynthesis in AGB Stars

### 20.1 What Are s-Elements?

**s-elements** are chemical elements produced by **slow neutron capture** — the s-process. "Slow" means the timescale between successive neutron captures is longer than the $\beta$-decay timescale of unstable intermediate isotopes. Therefore each capture is followed by $\beta$-decay before the next neutron is added:
$$A + n \to (A+1) + \gamma \quad \text{(neutron capture)}$$
$$^{A+1}Z \to ^{A+1}(Z+1) + e^- + \bar\nu \quad \text{(beta decay, if unstable)}$$

The s-process path runs along the valley of nuclear stability, building up elements heavier than iron (e.g., Ba, Ce, La, Sr, Y, Zr). Barium is a classic example of an s-process element. AGB stars are among the most important astrophysical sites for s-process nucleosynthesis, injecting enriched material into the ISM via stellar winds and planetary nebula ejection.

### 20.2 The Neutron Source Problem

Free neutrons have a half-life of only $\sim 15$ minutes (free neutron $\beta$-decay: $n \to p^+ + e^- + \bar\nu$). They must be created **locally** (in the same stellar region) and **nearly simultaneously** with their capture — there is no time to transport them. Two neutron-producing reactions are relevant in AGB interiors:

**Reaction 1 — $^{22}$Ne source (requires $T \sim 3.5 \times 10^8$ K, accessible in $M > 3\,M_\odot$ AGB):**
$$^{14}\text{N} + ^4\text{He} \to ^{18}\text{F} + \gamma$$
$$^{18}\text{F} \to ^{18}\text{O} + e^+ + \nu$$
$$^{18}\text{O} + ^4\text{He} \to ^{22}\text{Ne} + \gamma$$
$$^{22}\text{Ne} + \alpha \to ^{25}\text{Mg} + n$$

The $^{14}$N fuel is provided by the CN branch of the CNO cycle operating in the intershell. The $^{22}$Ne neutron source requires high temperatures reached at the base of the intershell in more massive ($M > 3\,M_\odot$) AGB stars.

**Reaction 2 — $^{13}$C source (requires $T \sim 9 \times 10^7$ K, viable in lower-mass AGB):**
$$^{13}\text{C} + \alpha \to ^{16}\text{O} + n$$

This requires $^{13}$C to be present. After the Third Dredge-Up, when the H-shell reactivates above the C-enriched intershell, some protons from the H-envelope are mixed into the C-rich intershell. These react via:
$$^{12}\text{C} + H \to ^{13}\text{N} + \gamma$$
$$^{13}\text{N} \to ^{13}\text{C} + e^+ + \nu$$

producing $^{13}$C in the intershell. When the He-shell later ignites, the $^{13}$C reacts with alpha particles to produce neutrons.

### 20.3 The N$^{14}$ Poison Problem

$^{14}$N is a severe **neutron poison** — it absorbs neutrons very efficiently:
$$^{14}\text{N} + n \to ^{15}\text{N} + \gamma$$

This prevents neutrons from reaching the heavier target nuclei needed for s-process. The ideal environment for s-process must be: (a) rich in protons (to form $^{13}$C) AND (b) rich in helium (to convert $^{14}$N into $^{18}$F → $^{18}$O → $^{22}$Ne, removing the N$^{14}$ poison). These conditions are precisely those of the intershell region after the Third Dredge-Up, making the TP-AGB phase the prime s-process site.

## 21. Hot Bottom Burning (HBB) in Massive AGB Stars

### 21.1 The HBB Phenomenon

In **massive AGB stars ($M > 6$–$7\,M_\odot$)**, the temperature at the **base of the convective envelope** reaches $\sim 8 \times 10^7$ K. At such temperatures, hydrogen burning (CNO-cycle reactions) operates directly at the base of the envelope — this is called **Hot Bottom Burning (HBB)**. Because the convective envelope thoroughly mixes the material, the products of this burning are carried efficiently to the surface.

### 21.2 Consequences of HBB

1. **Luminosity increase**: The additional nuclear energy source at the envelope base increases the stellar luminosity beyond the prediction of standard core-mass–luminosity relations.

2. **Carbon destruction → prevents carbon star formation**: The CNO cycle at the base of the envelope converts $^{12}$C into $^{14}$N. Even if the Third Dredge-Up deposits $^{12}$C into the intershell, HBB converts it back to N before it can accumulate at the surface. This is why **massive AGB stars do not become carbon stars** despite having active Third Dredge-Up events.

3. **Lithium production via the Cameron-Fowler mechanism**: At $T \sim 10^8$ K, the chain operates:
$$^3\text{He} + ^4\text{He} \to ^7\text{Be} + \gamma$$
$$^7\text{Be} + e^- \to ^7\text{Li} + \nu$$

However, at HBB temperatures, $^7$Li is rapidly destroyed by proton capture: $^7\text{Li} + p \to 2\,^4\text{He}$. **Without convection**, all Li is destroyed. But the convective envelope efficiently transports newly formed $^7$Li to cooler surface layers (below $T \sim 2 \times 10^6$ K) **before** it can be destroyed. This is the Cameron-Fowler mechanism, which can produce Li overabundances of $\sim 3$ orders of magnitude relative to normal stellar values. This explains the anomalously high Li abundances observed in the atmospheres of bright Long Period Variables (Mira-type AGB stars).

4. **High-temperature p-capture cycles**: HBB activates additional proton-capture cycles beyond the standard CNO cycle.

## 22. Chemical Anomalies: NeNa and MgAl Cycles

### 22.1 Chemical Anomalies in Stellar Populations

Hot Bottom Burning in AGB stars activates **high-temperature proton-capture cycles** that can explain chemical anomalies observed in old stellar populations, particularly globular clusters. These anomalies manifest as **anti-correlations** between abundances of specific element pairs.

### 22.2 The NeNa Cycle

At **$T > 4 \times 10^7$ K**: the NeNa cycle operates. In regions where oxygen is depleted, $^{20}$Ne undergoes proton captures producing $^{23}$Na:
$$^{20}\text{Ne} + p \to \dots \to ^{23}\text{Na}$$

This produces the observed **O-Na anticorrelation**: stars with lower oxygen abundance show higher sodium abundance. This anti-correlation is observed across many globular cluster systems (Carretta et al. 2009 measured it in 19 Galactic Globular Clusters).

### 22.3 The MgAl Cycle

At **$T > 7 \times 10^7$ K**: the MgAl cycle operates. Proton captures on magnesium isotopes ($^{24}$Mg, $^{25}$Mg, $^{26}$Mg) produce $^{27}$Al:
$$^{24,25,26}\text{Mg} + p \to \dots \to ^{27}\text{Al}$$

This produces the **Mg-Al anticorrelation**: stars with depleted Mg show enhanced Al.

Both cycles occur in the **Hot Bottom Burning region** at the base of the convective envelope in massive ($3$–$8\,M_\odot$) AGB stars. The O-Na and Mg-Al anticorrelations are among the most important chemical diagnostic tools for understanding AGB nucleosynthesis and the chemical enrichment history of globular clusters. The detection of these patterns across many clusters provides strong observational evidence for HBB in massive AGB stars.

## 23. The AGB Phase Transition

### 23.1 Definition

Analogous to the RGB Phase Transition, the **AGB Phase Transition** occurs when the turnoff mass crosses $M^\text{up} = 8\,M_\odot$, corresponding to ages of $\sim 200$–$300$ Myr.

**For populations younger than $\sim 200$–$300$ Myr ($M_\text{TO} > 8\,M_\odot$):**
- Stars evolve through all nuclear burning stages without developing degenerate CO cores
- The AGB phase (double-shell burning) does not develop as a distinct, extended phase
- The AGB is poorly populated

**For populations older than $\sim 200$–$300$ Myr ($M_\text{TO} < 8\,M_\odot$):**
- Stars develop degenerate CO cores after He-burning
- Degeneracy slows evolution, producing an extended TP-AGB phase
- The AGB becomes well populated and luminous
- AGB stars make a significant contribution to the integrated bolometric luminosity of the population

In population synthesis models (Renzini & Buzzoni 1986), the AGB fractional contribution to integrated bolometric luminosity shows a clear onset corresponding to this transition. The RGB Phase Transition and the AGB Phase Transition bracket the main events in how an aging stellar population's integrated light is shaped by evolving stellar types.

## 24. Summary: Critical Mass Boundaries and Stellar Evolution

### 24.1 The Three Critical Masses

Three mass boundaries define the qualitative character of stellar evolution:

| Mass Boundary | Physical Meaning | Age Corresponding to Turnoff |
|---|---|---|
| $0.5\,M_\odot$ | Minimum mass to ignite He (reach He-WD threshold) | $\gg t_\text{Hubble}$ |
| $M^\text{He} = 2.2\,M_\odot$ | Transition from degenerate to non-degenerate He-core ignition; RGB Phase Transition | $\sim 1$–$2$ Gyr |
| $M^\text{up} = 8\,M_\odot$ | Transition from degenerate to non-degenerate CO-core; WD vs. SN fate; AGB Phase Transition | $\sim 200$–$300$ Myr |

### 24.2 The Complete Mass-Fate Table

| Mass Range | He-core | CO-core | Final Fate |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| $0.08 < M/M_\odot < 0.5$ | Fully degenerate; He-flash never reached | — | He-WDs |
| $0.5 < M/M_\odot < 2.2$ | Degenerate; He-flash at $M_c \approx 0.5\,M_\odot$ | Degenerate after He-burning | CO-WDs |
| $2.2 < M/M_\odot < 8$ | Non-degenerate; stable He ignition | Degenerate after He-burning | CO-WDs |
| $M > 8\,M_\odot$ | Non-degenerate throughout | Non-degenerate; C ignites stably | Type II (Core Collapse) SN |

### 24.3 The Full Evolutionary Sequence of a Low-Mass Star ($0.5 < M/M_\odot < 2.2$)

1. **ZAMS**: Core H-burning (MS phase, timescale $\sim 10$ Gyr for $1\,M_\odot$)
2. **SGB (Segment 4–5)**: Shell H-burning ignites; envelope expansion at nearly constant $L$ (timescale $\sim 10^9$ yr for $1\,M_\odot$)
3. **RGB (Segment 5–6)**: Shell H-burning; ascending the giant branch. First Dredge-Up, RGB Bump, core degeneracy grows. Luminosity driven by $M_c^\text{He}$–$L$ relation. Mass loss on upper RGB.
4. **He Flash (Point 6 / RGB Tip)**: Degenerate He-core ignites off-center; cascade of sub-flashes removes degeneracy. $M_c^\text{He} \approx 0.5\,M_\odot$ (universal). No change in surface luminosity.
5. **ZAHB**: Stable core He-burning + H-shell. Duration $\sim 100$ Myr for $1\,M_\odot$.
6. **HB evolution**: Loop in HRD as He is consumed. Core: $3\alpha$ + $^{12}\text{C} + \alpha$ → CO core forms.
7. **E-AGB**: He-shell ignites; CO core inert; H-shell extinguishes. Second Dredge-Up (in $M > 4$–$5\,M_\odot$).
8. **TP-AGB**: Alternating H- and He-shell burning; thermal pulses; Third Dredge-Up; s-process; HBB in massive AGB. Mass loss intensifies.
9. **Planetary Nebula ejection**: Envelope ejected; CO-core exposed.
10. **CO White Dwarf**: Inert, slowly cooling degenerate remnant.

## 7. Gravitational Waves: Theoretical Foundation

### 7.1 What Are Gravitational Waves?

**Gravitational waves (GWs)** are ripples or perturbations in the fabric of spacetime itself, generated whenever mass or energy is accelerated. The fundamental statement is:

> **The accelerated motion of any object endowed with mass or energy generates gravitational waves.**

In Newtonian gravity there is no radiation associated with gravitational interactions â€” orbiting objects simply orbit forever. In general relativity, however, accelerating masses curve spacetime dynamically, and these curvature fluctuations propagate outward at the speed of light as gravitational waves, carrying energy away from the source. The analogy with electromagnetic radiation (generated by accelerating electric charges) is instructive but imperfect: GWs are transverse, quadrupolar waves (the lowest multipole for GW radiation is the quadrupole, because the GW field has spin-2 and GW energy cannot be radiated by a spherically symmetric or axially symmetric collapse alone).

A binary system â€” two massive objects (BHs, NSs, or ordinary stars) orbiting their common centre of mass â€” is the ideal GW source: continuously accelerating masses that radiate GWs, lose orbital energy, and gradually spiral inward until merger.

### 7.2 Binary System Orbital Mechanics

Consider a binary system with masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ separated by distance $R = r_1 + r_2$ (where $r_1$ and $r_2$ are the distances of each mass from the centre of mass). The **angular orbital velocity** is given by Kepler's third law:

$$\omega_s^2 = \frac{G(m_1 + m_2)}{R^3} = \frac{GM}{R^3}$$

where $M = m_1 + m_2$ is the total mass. This relation is foundational: it connects the orbital separation to the orbital frequency, and since GW frequency is twice the orbital frequency, it connects $R$ to the observable GW frequency. As the orbit shrinks ($R \searrow$), the orbital velocity increases ($\omega_s \nearrow$), and everything evolves faster.

### 7.3 Strain and the GW Observable

The primary observable of a GW is the **strain** $h(t)$, which quantifies the fractional distortion of spacetime:

$$\frac{\Delta L}{L} = h_0$$

where $\Delta L$ is the change in the separation $L$ between two free-falling test masses and $L$ is their initial separation. As a GW passes, the distance between objects alternately increases and decreases â€” a ring of test masses is deformed into an ellipse during one half-cycle and then into a perpendicular ellipse during the next half-cycle. The amplitude of this periodic deformation is $h_0$.

The time-varying strain signal is:

$$h(t) = h_0 \cos(\omega_\mathrm{gw}\,t)$$

The **GW frequency** is exactly **twice the orbital frequency**:

$$\omega_\mathrm{gw} = 2\omega_s$$

This factor of 2 arises because the quadrupole moment of the binary completes a full cycle every half orbit (as the mass distribution repeats itself twice per orbit). A measurement of $\omega_\mathrm{gw}$ immediately gives $\omega_s$, and through Kepler's third law, the orbital separation $R$.

### 7.4 The GW Amplitude $h_0$ and Its Physical Origin

The amplitude of the strain is:

$$h_0 = 2\left(\frac{2Gm_1}{c^2\,r}\right)\left(\frac{2Gm_2}{c^2\,R}\right) = 2\left(\frac{R_{S1}}{r}\right)\left(\frac{R_{S2}}{R}\right)$$

where:
- $R_{S1} = 2Gm_1/c^2$ is the Schwarzschild radius of mass $m_1$,
- $R_{S2} = 2Gm_2/c^2$ is the Schwarzschild radius of mass $m_2$,
- $r$ is the **distance from the binary to the observer** (Earth),
- $R$ is the **current orbital separation** of the binary.

This expression has a beautiful physical interpretation: $h_0$ is essentially the product of two Schwarzschild radius ratios â€” the compactness of each body ($R_{Si}/R$, how compact each mass is relative to the orbital separation) divided by the distance to the observer ($R_{Si}/r$). The amplitude is the product of both factors; it decreases with distance as $1/r$ (as expected for a wave in 3D) and increases as the orbit shrinks (as $R \searrow$, $R_{Si}/R$ grows).

Equivalently, $h_0$ can be written in terms of the total and reduced masses:

$$h_0 = 2\left(\frac{2GM}{c^2 r}\right)\left(\frac{2G\mu}{c^2 R}\right)$$

where $M = m_1 + m_2$ and $\mu = m_1 m_2 / (m_1 + m_2)$ is the **reduced mass**.

As the orbital separation $R$ decreases, both $h_0$ and $\omega_\mathrm{gw}$ increase:

$$R \searrow \quad \Rightarrow \quad h_0 \nearrow \quad \omega_\mathrm{gw} \nearrow\!\!\nearrow$$

The simultaneous increase in amplitude and frequency is the defining characteristic of a binary inspiral and produces the distinctive **"chirp"** signal shape in time-frequency representations.

### 7.5 Order of Magnitude of the GW Strain

The strain $h_0$ is predicted to be extraordinarily small. Consider two neutron stars with $m_1 = m_2 = 1.4\,M_\odot$:

$$R_{S1} = R_{S2} = \frac{2G \cdot 1.4 \cdot 2 \times 10^{33}}{9 \times 10^{20}} \approx 4 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{cm}$$

For a binary system with orbital separation $R = 10 \times R_\star = 10^7\ \mathrm{cm}$ (ten times the NS radius), at a distance of 10 Mpc $= 3 \times 10^{25}\ \mathrm{cm}$:

$$h_0 = \frac{(4 \times 10^5)^2}{3 \times 10^{25} \times 10^7} \times 2 \approx 10^{-22}$$

This means $\Delta L / L = 10^{-22}$. Taking $L = 1.5 \times 10^{13}\ \mathrm{cm}$ (the Earthâ€“Sun distance):

$$\Delta L \approx 10^{-22} \times 1.5 \times 10^{13}\ \mathrm{cm} = 1.5 \times 10^{-9}\ \mathrm{cm}$$

**This is the size of one atom.** A passing gravitational wave from a NSâ€“NS merger at 10 Mpc changes the Earthâ€“Sun distance by one atomic diameter. This extraordinary smallness is why detecting GWs required decades of technological development and is one of the greatest experimental achievements in science.

## 8. GW Energy, Luminosity, and the Inspiral Dynamics

### 8.1 Total Orbital Energy of a Binary System

The total mechanical energy of a binary BH system in a circular orbit is:

$$E_\mathrm{tot} = K + U = \frac{1}{2}\mu\omega_s^2 R^2 - \frac{Gm_1 m_2}{R} = -\frac{GM\mu}{2R} = -\frac{Gm_1 m_2}{2R}$$

The negative sign indicates a bound system. Note that as the orbit shrinks ($R$ decreases), $E_\mathrm{tot}$ becomes more negative â€” the total energy decreases (the system loses energy). This energy is carried away by gravitational radiation.

In classical mechanics with no GW emission, the orbit would persist forever (no energy loss). In general relativity, the orbiting BHs radiate continuously, losing orbital energy and spiralling inward. This is the **GW-driven inspiral**.

### 8.2 Gravitational Wave Luminosity

The power emitted by the binary system as gravitational radiation (the GW luminosity) is:

$$L = E = \frac{32}{5}\frac{G}{c^5}\left(\mu R^2 \omega_s^3\right)^2$$

The physical content of this formula is important:
- The prefactor $G/c^5 \sim 10^{-60}$ is extremely small. This is why GW power is negligible for ordinary binary stars (e.g., two Sun-like stars orbiting each other lose energy so slowly that the orbit will not shrink appreciably in the age of the Universe).
- The luminosity scales as $(\mu R^2 \omega_s^3)^2$. Since $\omega_s \propto R^{-3/2}$ (from Kepler's law), we get $L \propto R^{4} \times R^{-9} = R^{-5}$: as the separation decreases, the luminosity increases steeply. In the final stages of the inspiral (small $R$, large $\omega_s$), the GW luminosity becomes enormous.
- Correspondingly, the energy flux (power per unit area) at distance $r$ from the source is:

$$F = \frac{1}{32\pi}\frac{c^3}{G}h_0^2\,\omega_\mathrm{gw}^2$$

This connects the observable quantities ($h_0$, $\omega_\mathrm{gw}$) to the radiated power, and is used later to estimate the distance to the source.

### 8.3 The Inspiral: Connecting Energy Loss to Orbital Evolution

The energy conservation equation $L = -dE_\mathrm{tot}/dt$ drives the inspiral. Starting from $E_\mathrm{tot} = -GM\mu/(2R)$:

$$\frac{dE_\mathrm{tot}}{dt} = \frac{GM\mu}{2R^2}\dot{R} = \frac{GM\mu}{2R}\frac{\dot{R}}{R}$$

From Kepler's third law $\omega_s = (GM)^{1/2}/R^{3/2}$, differentiating gives:

$$\dot{\omega}_s = -\frac{3}{2}\left(\frac{GM}{R^5}\right)^{1/2}\dot{R} = -\frac{3}{2}\omega_s\frac{\dot{R}}{R}$$

so $\dot{R}/R = -(2/3)\dot{\omega}_s/\omega_s$. Substituting:

$$\frac{dE_\mathrm{tot}}{dt} = -\frac{GM\mu}{3R}\frac{\dot{\omega}_s}{\omega_s}$$

Setting this equal to $-L$:

$$\frac{32G}{5c^5}\mu^2 R^4\omega_s^6 = \frac{GM\mu}{3R}\frac{\dot{\omega}_s}{\omega_s}$$

Using $R = (GM/\omega_s^2)^{1/3}$ (from Kepler's law), after algebraic manipulation:

$$\dot{\omega}_s = \frac{96G}{5c^5}\frac{\mu}{M}R^5\omega_s^7 = \frac{96}{5}\frac{G^{5/3}\omega_s^7}{c^5\omega_s^{10/3}}(\mu M^{2/3})$$

This is the **fundamental equation of the inspiral**: it connects the rate of change of orbital frequency to the masses and current frequency. Crucially, the mass dependence enters through the specific combination $\mu^3 M^2$, which defines the chirp mass.

## 9. The Chirp Mass: The Primary Observable

### 9.1 Derivation of the Chirp Mass

Cubing the inspiral equation:

$$\dot{\omega}_s^3 = \left(\frac{96}{5}\right)^3\frac{G^5\omega_s^{11}}{c^{15}}(\mu^3 M^2)$$

Rearranging to isolate the mass combination:

$$(\mu^3 M^2) = \left(\frac{5}{96}\right)^3\frac{c^{15}}{G^5}\omega_s^{-11}\dot{\omega}_s^3$$

This combination $(\mu^3 M^2)^{1/5}$ defines the **chirp mass**:

$$\boxed{M_\mathrm{chirp} = (\mu^3 M^2)^{1/5} = \frac{(m_1 m_2)^{3/5}}{(m_1 + m_2)^{1/5}}}$$

The chirp mass can be written explicitly in terms of observable quantities (the GW frequency $\omega_\mathrm{gw}$ and its time derivative):

$$M_\mathrm{chirp} = \frac{c^3}{G}\left[\left(\frac{5}{96}\right)^3\omega_s^{-11}\dot{\omega}_s^3\right]^{1/5}$$

Converting to the directly observed GW frequency $\nu_\mathrm{gw}$ using $\omega_\mathrm{gw} = 2\omega_s$ and $\omega_s = \pi\nu_\mathrm{gw}$:

$$\boxed{M_\mathrm{chirp} = \frac{c^3}{G}\left[\frac{5}{96}\pi^{-8/3}\nu_\mathrm{gw}^{-11/3}\dot{\nu}_\mathrm{gw}\right]^{3/5}}$$

### 9.2 Physical Meaning and Why It Is the Primary Observable

The chirp mass is the most important quantity derivable from a GW inspiral signal. It is called the "chirp mass" because it controls the "chirp" â€” the characteristic increase in frequency that gives the signal its bird-like name in audio representations.

Crucially, **$M_\mathrm{chirp}$ is determined solely by measurable quantities**: the GW frequency $\nu_\mathrm{gw}$ and its rate of change $\dot{\nu}_\mathrm{gw}$, both of which are directly read from the time-frequency spectrogram of the detected signal. No prior knowledge of the source distance or individual masses is needed.

The product $[\nu_\mathrm{gw}^{-11}\dot{\nu}_\mathrm{gw}^3]$ remains **constant** throughout different stages of the inspiral:

$$[\nu_\mathrm{gw}^{-11}\dot{\nu}_\mathrm{gw}^3] = \left[\frac{G^5}{c^{15}}\left(\frac{96}{5}\right)^3\pi^8\right]M_\mathrm{chirp}^5$$

This constancy â€” the fact that the same combination of observables at different moments of the inspiral yields the same $M_\mathrm{chirp}$ â€” serves both as a consistency check and as the means by which the chirp mass is measured. From the GW150914 signal, successive time intervals between wave minima ($\Delta t$) give frequencies $\nu_\mathrm{gw}$ and rates $\dot{\nu}_\mathrm{gw}$ at different stages. Applying the formula consistently yields:

$$M_\mathrm{chirp}(\mathrm{GW150914}) \approx 30\,M_\odot$$

This is a direct, model-independent measurement of the mass scale of the system from the waveform alone.

### 9.3 From Chirp Mass to Total Mass: The Massâ€“Ratio Degeneracy

While the chirp mass is precisely determined, individual masses are not. The chirp mass is a specific combination of $m_1$ and $m_2$, and without additional information, one cannot solve for both masses separately from $M_\mathrm{chirp}$ alone.

Parameterise the masses as $m_1 = \alpha M$ and $m_2 = (1 - \alpha)M$ with $M = m_1 + m_2$ and $0 < \alpha < 1$. Then:

$$M_\mathrm{chirp} = [\alpha(1-\alpha)]^{3/5}\,M^{6/5}/M^{1/5} = [\alpha(1-\alpha)]^{3/5}M$$

$$M_\mathrm{tot} = \frac{M_\mathrm{chirp}}{[\alpha(1-\alpha)]^{3/5}}$$

The denominator $[\alpha(1-\alpha)]^{3/5}$ has its **maximum** at $\alpha = 1/2$ (equal masses), where it equals $(1/4)^{3/5} \approx 0.435$. For any other mass ratio, the denominator is smaller, so $M_\mathrm{tot}$ is larger. Therefore:

$$M_\mathrm{tot} \geq \frac{M_\mathrm{chirp}}{(0.25)^{3/5}} = 2.3\,M_\mathrm{chirp}$$

The minimum total mass corresponds to the equal-mass case:

$$M_\mathrm{tot}^\mathrm{min} = 2.3\,M_\mathrm{chirp}$$

For GW150914 with $M_\mathrm{chirp} = 30\,M_\odot$:

$$M_\mathrm{tot}^\mathrm{min} = 2.3 \times 30 = 69\,M_\odot$$

The equal-mass assumption thus provides a **lower bound on the total system mass**.

## 10. Determining the Total Mass from Maximum GW Frequency

### 10.1 The Coalescence Condition

The inspiral description (Newtonian approximation) breaks down near merger. A physically motivated criterion for the onset of **coalescence** is the moment when the separation of the two BHs equals the sum of their Schwarzschild radii:

$$R_S = \frac{2G(m_1 + m_2)}{c^2} = \frac{2GM}{c^2}$$

At this separation, the Keplerian angular velocity is:

$$\omega_\mathrm{Schw} = \left(\frac{GM}{R_S^3}\right)^{1/2} = \left(\frac{GMc^6}{8G^3M^3}\right)^{1/2} = \frac{c^3}{GM\sqrt{8}}$$

### 10.2 Total Mass from Maximum Observed Frequency

The GW frequency at coalescence is $\omega_\mathrm{gw} = 2\omega_\mathrm{Schw}$. Using $\omega_s = \pi\nu_\mathrm{gw}$:

$$M = \frac{1}{\sqrt{8}}\frac{c^3}{G\omega_\mathrm{Schw}} = \frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{8}}\frac{c^3}{G\nu_\mathrm{gw}^\mathrm{max}}$$

This is a **key formula**: the total mass of the merging system is inversely proportional to the maximum GW frequency observed. The higher the frequency at merger, the lighter the system. This is because more massive BHs have larger Schwarzschild radii and therefore merge at lower orbital frequencies (the orbit is larger before the BHs touch).

From the GW150914 spectrogram, the maximum visible GW frequency is $\nu_\mathrm{gw}^\mathrm{max} \approx 330\ \mathrm{Hz}$. Substituting:

$$M_\mathrm{TOT} = \frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{8}}\frac{c^3}{G \times 330\ \mathrm{Hz}} = 1.4 \times 10^{35}\ \mathrm{g} = 70\,M_\odot$$

This independent estimate of $M_\mathrm{TOT} = 70\,M_\odot$ is consistent with (and confirms) the lower bound derived from the chirp mass ($\geq 69\,M_\odot$). For $M = 70\,M_\odot$, the corresponding Schwarzschild radius is:

$$R_S = 3 \times 70\ \mathrm{km} = 210\ \mathrm{km}$$

This agrees with the minimum orbital separation found from the Table 1 analysis of the GW signal â€” an important internal consistency check.

## 11. Individual Masses, Distance, and the Complete GW150914 Analysis

### 11.1 Individual BH Masses

With both $M_\mathrm{chirp} = 30\,M_\odot$ and $M_\mathrm{tot} = 70\,M_\odot$ determined, we can solve for the mass ratio parameter $\alpha$:

$$M_\mathrm{tot} = \frac{M_\mathrm{chirp}}{[\alpha(1-\alpha)]^{3/5}} \quad \Rightarrow \quad [\alpha(1-\alpha)]^{3/5} = \frac{30}{70}$$

Solving numerically gives $\alpha \approx 0.6$, from which the individual masses are:

$$m_1 = \alpha\,M_\mathrm{tot} = 0.6 \times 70\,M_\odot = 42\,M_\odot$$
$$m_2 = (1-\alpha)\,M_\mathrm{tot} = 0.4 \times 70\,M_\odot = 28\,M_\odot$$

The merger of two such massive BHs would have been undetectable to any pre-LIGO instrument. This was the first direct observation of a BHâ€“BH merger, and the individual BH masses â€” $42\,M_\odot$ and $28\,M_\odot$ â€” were significantly more massive than the typical BHs known from X-ray binaries before this detection.

### 11.2 Summary of GW150914 Analysis Chain

The complete derivation from raw signal to physical parameters proceeds as follows:

1. **From time-frequency diagram:** read $\Delta t$ between successive wave minima â†’ $\nu_\mathrm{gw}$ at each stage â†’ $\dot{\nu}_\mathrm{gw}$.
2. **Chirp mass:** $M_\mathrm{chirp} = (c^3/G)[5\pi^{-8/3}\nu_\mathrm{gw}^{-11/3}\dot{\nu}_\mathrm{gw}/96]^{3/5} \approx 30\,M_\odot$.
3. **Minimum total mass:** $M_\mathrm{tot}^\mathrm{min} = 2.3\,M_\mathrm{chirp} = 69\,M_\odot$.
4. **Maximum GW frequency:** $\nu_\mathrm{gw}^\mathrm{max} = 330\ \mathrm{Hz}$ â†’ $M_\mathrm{TOT} = 70\,M_\odot$.
5. **Mass ratio:** $\alpha \approx 0.6$ â†’ $m_1 = 42\,M_\odot$, $m_2 = 28\,M_\odot$.

### 11.3 Estimating the Distance

Using the fluxâ€“luminosity relationship $F = L/(4\pi r^2)$ and the expressions for $L$ and $F$ in terms of GW observables:

$$L = \frac{32}{5}\frac{G}{c^5}(\mu R^2\omega_s^3)^2 \qquad F = \frac{1}{32\pi}\frac{c^3}{G}h_0^2\omega_\mathrm{gw}^2$$

Setting $L = 4\pi r^2 F$ and solving for $r$:

$$r = \frac{16}{\sqrt{5}}\frac{G}{c^4}\frac{1}{h_0}\mu R^2\omega_s^2$$

For GW150914 at $\Delta t = 16\ \mathrm{ms}$:
- $\mu = m_1 m_2 / M = (42 \times 28)/70 \approx 17\,M_\odot$
- $h_0 = 0.8 \times 10^{-21}$ (read directly from the amplitude of the strain signal)
- $\nu_\mathrm{gw} \approx 63\ \mathrm{Hz}$ â†’ $\omega_s = \pi\nu_\mathrm{gw} \approx 200\ \mathrm{s^{-1}}$
- $R = (GM/\omega_s^2)^{1/3} \approx 4.7 \times 10^7\ \mathrm{cm}$

Substituting:

$$r \approx 1.1 \times 10^{27}\ \mathrm{cm} = 4 \times 10^8\ \mathrm{pc} = 400\ \mathrm{Mpc}$$

GW150914 originated from a source approximately **400 Mpc** away â€” well within the "local" Universe in cosmological terms, but at an enormous distance by any ordinary astronomical scale.

## 12. LIGO: The Detection Instrument

### 12.1 Michelson Interferometer Design

Ground-based GW detectors such as LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory) are arranged in the **Michelson interferometer** configuration: an L-shaped structure with two perpendicular arms, each 4 km long. The operating principle exploits the fact that a passing GW stretches space in one direction and compresses it in the perpendicular direction â€” exactly what a Michelson interferometer is sensitive to.

The basic components are:
1. **Laser:** produces highly coherent monochromatic light.
2. **Beam splitter:** divides the laser beam into two equal beams, sending one down each arm at 90Â° to each other.
3. **Mirrors:** at the far end of each arm, mirrors reflect the beams back toward the beam splitter.
4. **Beam recombination:** the returning beams recombine at the beam splitter, where they **interfere**.
5. **Photodetector:** measures the intensity of the combined beam.

The interferometer is tuned so that in its undisturbed state, the two beams return with a path length difference of exactly half a wavelength, producing **completely destructive interference** at the detector â€” zero light output. When a GW passes, it differentially changes the lengths of the two arms: one arm is stretched by $\Delta L$ and the other is compressed by $\Delta L$ simultaneously. This introduces a path length difference of $2\Delta L$ between the beams, breaking the perfect destructive interference and allowing some light to reach the detector. Measuring this change in light intensity directly measures the strain $h = \Delta L / L$.

The required sensitivity is $h \sim 10^{-22}$, meaning $\Delta L \sim 10^{-16}\ \mathrm{cm} = 10^{-18}\ \mathrm{m}$ â€” a thousand times smaller than a proton. Achieving this requires Fabry-PĂ©rot cavities to increase the effective optical path length, high-power lasers, seismic isolation, and a host of noise-reduction technologies.

### 12.2 The LIGO Network

The LIGO network consists of two observatories:
- **Hanford, Washington (H1)**
- **Livingston, Louisiana (L1)**

The two sites are separated by approximately **3,002 km**. Using two (or more) geographically separated detectors is essential for several reasons: (1) a genuine GW signal must appear in both detectors with a consistent time delay of at most $3002/3 \times 10^5\ \mathrm{km} \approx 10\ \mathrm{ms}$ (the light travel time between the sites); (2) local noise sources (seismic events, acoustic noise, etc.) affect only one site at a time and can thus be distinguished from a true GW; (3) the relative timing between sites provides directional information about the source on the sky. The GW150914 signal was detected with a time delay of $\sim 7\ \mathrm{ms}$ between Hanford and Livingston, consistent with a source arriving from a specific direction on the sky.

The standard four-panel LIGO data product for a GW event shows:
- **Row 1:** Observed strain $h(t)$ at each detector.
- **Row 2:** Reconstructed numerical relativity waveform (theoretical prediction from best-fit masses and spins).
- **Row 3:** Residuals (observed minus reconstructed) â€” ideally pure noise.
- **Row 4:** Timeâ€“frequency spectrogram (frequency vs. time), showing the characteristic upward-sweeping "chirp" signal.

## 13. Cosmological Corrections: Redshift Effects on GW Observables

### 13.1 Redshift of GW Frequency and the Apparent Chirp Mass

GWs, like electromagnetic radiation, are affected by the expansion of the Universe. For a source at redshift $z$, the detected frequency and frequency derivative are redshifted relative to the source-frame values:

$$\nu_\mathrm{gw}^\mathrm{det} = \frac{\nu_\mathrm{gw}^s}{(1+z)}$$

Time intervals at the observer are stretched:
$$\Delta t^\mathrm{det} = (1+z)\,\Delta t^s$$

The frequency derivative transforms as:
$$\dot{\nu}_\mathrm{gw}^\mathrm{det} = \frac{\Delta\nu^\mathrm{det}}{\Delta t^\mathrm{det}} = \frac{\Delta\nu^s/(1+z)}{\Delta t^s\,(1+z)} = \frac{\dot{\nu}_\mathrm{gw}^s}{(1+z)^2}$$

### 13.2 Effect on Derived Masses

Substituting the redshifted observables into the chirp mass formula, which scales as $\nu_\mathrm{gw}^{-11/5}\dot{\nu}_\mathrm{gw}^{3/5}$:

$$M_\mathrm{chirp}^\mathrm{det} \propto \frac{(\nu_\mathrm{gw}^s)^{-11/5}}{(1+z)^{-11/5}} \cdot \frac{(\dot{\nu}_\mathrm{gw}^s)^{3/5}}{(1+z)^{6/5}} = (1+z)^{11/5 - 6/5}(\nu_\mathrm{gw}^s)^{-11/5}(\dot{\nu}_\mathrm{gw}^s)^{3/5}$$

$$M_\mathrm{chirp}^\mathrm{det} = (1+z)\,M_\mathrm{chirp}^s$$

The detected (observer-frame) chirp mass is **systematically over-estimated** by a factor $(1+z)$ relative to the true source-frame chirp mass. The same scaling applies to individual masses:

$$m_1^\mathrm{det} = (1+z)\,m_1^s \qquad m_2^\mathrm{det} = (1+z)\,m_2^s$$

### 13.3 Redshift Correction for GW150914

The redshift of a GW source cannot be directly determined from the GW signal alone â€” GWs carry no spectral lines that shift with redshift. Instead, the redshift must be obtained from:
- An electromagnetic counterpart (e.g., the host galaxy's known redshift, as was done for GW170817).
- Standard cosmology, using the luminosity distanceâ€“redshift relation with Planck cosmological parameters.

For GW150914, the luminosity distance was estimated to be $\sim 400$ Mpc. Using standard cosmology (Planck parameters), this corresponds to a redshift of $z \approx 0.1$. Accordingly, the source-frame masses are approximately 10% smaller than the detector-frame values derived:

$$m_1^s \approx 38\,M_\odot \qquad m_2^s \approx 25\,M_\odot$$

For events at low redshift (as all confirmed LIGO events in O1 and O2 runs were), this correction is modest but not negligible. For future high-redshift detections, this effect will be much more significant.

## 15. GW170817: The Kilonova and NSâ€“NS Merger

### 15.1 Multi-Messenger Astronomy from NSâ€“NS Merger

**GW170817** was the first gravitational wave event detected from the merger of two **neutron stars** (NSâ€“NS), and it opened the era of **multi-messenger astronomy** with gravitational waves. For a NSâ€“NS merger sufficiently close to Earth, three simultaneous observable phenomena are predicted:

1. **Gravitational waves:** from the inspiral, merger, and possibly post-merger remnant. GW170817 was detected by Advanced LIGO (both sites) and Virgo, with a long inspiral phase visible in the spectrogram for $\sim 10$ seconds before merger.
2. **Short gamma-ray burst (GRB):** produced by a relativistic jet launched along the polar axis (perpendicular to the orbital plane) of the merger. GRB 170817A was detected by the Fermi-GBM instrument approximately **1.7 seconds** after the GW merger time ($T_0$), confirming the long-postulated connection between NSâ€“NS mergers and short GRBs. The short time delay is consistent with a compact remnant launching a relativistic jet. The Fermi-GBM light curve showed a peak at $\sim +2$ s above background.
3. **Kilonova (opticalâ€“infrared transient):** powered by the radioactive decay of heavy r-process elements produced in the merger ejecta.

The source was the galaxy **NGC 4993** at a distance of $\sim 40$ Mpc. The optical transient was named **AT 2017gfo**.

### 15.2 The Kilonova and r-Process Nucleosynthesis

The term **kilonova** refers to the transient opticalâ€“near-infrared source powered by the **radioactive decay** of very heavy elements synthesised during and after the merger. The physical mechanism is:

1. During the NSâ€“NS merger, large quantities of neutron-rich material are ejected. The ratio of neutrons to protons is enormously high â€” ideal conditions for the **rapid neutron capture process (r-process)**.
2. Free neutrons are captured by seed nuclei (iron-peak elements) much faster than those nuclei can beta-decay, building up nuclei far to the neutron-rich side of the valley of stability.
3. This process can synthesise elements up to and beyond uranium ($A \gtrsim 200$), including **gold (Au), platinum (Pt), europium (Eu), iridium (Ir)**, and the lanthanide elements.
4. These freshly synthesised unstable nuclei then decay radioactively on timescales of seconds to days, depositing energy into the surrounding ejecta and causing it to glow â€” the kilonova.

The spectroscopic evolution of AT 2017gfo (observed with the X-Shooter instrument on ESO's Very Large Telescope over 11 days) is diagnostic:
- **Early spectra (day 1.5):** smooth, blue continuum â€” dominated by lighter r-process elements with lower opacity.
- **Later spectra (days 3â€“11):** progressively redder, with broad absorption-like features â€” characteristic of lanthanide-rich ejecta with high opacity. Lanthanides (elements $57 \le Z \le 71$) have enormously complex electron shell structures that produce extremely high opacity in the optical, driving the emission to redder wavelengths.

This spectral evolution constitutes direct observational proof that NSâ€“NS mergers are a primary site of r-process nucleosynthesis and are responsible for producing much of the gold, platinum, and other heavy elements in the Universe.

## 16. Nucleosynthesis and the Origin of the Elements

### 16.1 Origin of Heavy Elements: The Complete Picture

Gravitational wave astronomy places NSâ€“NS mergers in their proper context within the broader framework of cosmic chemical evolution. The solar system abundance pattern (number of atoms per $10^6$ Si atoms vs. mass number) reveals the sites of origin of the elements:

- **Hydrogen and Helium (lightest elements):** produced in the **Big Bang nucleosynthesis** ($\sim 3$ minutes after the Big Bang).
- **Carbon through Silicon, and lighter elements beyond H/He:** produced primarily in **Type II (CC) SNe** â€” the explosive oxygen- and silicon-burning layers enrich the interstellar medium with O, Ne, Mg, Si, S, Ar.
- **Iron peak elements (Fe, Ni, Co, Mn, Cr):** approximately 70% from **Type Ia SNe** (thermonuclear) + 30% from CC SNe. Type Ia SNe are uniquely efficient at synthesising $^{56}$Ni (which decays to $^{56}$Fe).
- **Elements beyond iron (s-process peaks and r-process peaks, $A > 60$):** produced by two neutron-capture channels:
  - **s-process (slow neutron capture):** in AGB stars (low-to-intermediate mass stars on the thermally pulsing AGB), producing the Ba, La, Ce, Pb peaks.
  - **r-process (rapid neutron capture):** in **Kilonovae (NSâ€“NS and NSâ€“BH mergers)** and in some **CC SNe**, producing the heavy r-process elements: Au, Pt, Eu, Ir, U, Th, and the entire lanthanide chain.

The confirmation from GW170817 that NSâ€“NS mergers produce r-process elements is one of the most significant results in nuclear astrophysics of the last decade. It directly answers a question that had been debated for 60 years since the BÂ˛FH paper (1957): where are the r-process elements made?

## 17. Key Formulae Summary and Conceptual Connections

### 17.1 Black Hole Key Results

This section consolidates the most important formulas from the BH portion of the session for rapid exam reference.

**Schwarzschild radius** â€” the radius within which an object becomes a BH:
$$R_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2} = 3\left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right)\ \mathrm{km}$$

**Schwarzschild metric** â€” describes curved spacetime outside any spherically symmetric mass:
$$(ds)^2 = c^2 dt^2\left(1 - \frac{R_s}{r}\right) - \frac{dr^2}{1 - R_s/r} - r^2 d\Omega^2$$

**Proper radial distance** (physical distance between coordinate points):
$$dL = \frac{dr}{\sqrt{1 - R_s/r}} > dr$$

**Gravitational time dilation** (proper time at radius $r$):
$$d\tau = dt\sqrt{1 - R_s/r} < dt$$

**Coordinate speed of light** near a BH:
$$\frac{dr}{dt} = c\left(1 - \frac{R_s}{r}\right) \to 0 \text{ as } r \to R_s$$

**Compactness parameter** (determines SN vs. failed SN):
$$\xi_{2.5} = \frac{M_{2.5}/M_\odot}{R(M_{2.5})/1000\ \mathrm{km}} \qquad \xi_{2.5} \gtrless 0.2 \Leftrightarrow \text{SN vs. failed SN}$$

**Mass-loss rate metallicity dependence:**
$$\dot{M} \propto Z^\alpha, \quad \alpha \approx 0.5\text{â€“}0.9 \qquad \Rightarrow \text{metal-poor stars} \to \text{more massive BHs}$$

**Sphere of influence of IMBH:**
$$r_\mathrm{BH} = \frac{GM_\mathrm{BH}}{\sigma^2} = 4.32 \times 10^{-3}\frac{M_\mathrm{BH}/M_\odot}{(\sigma/\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}})^2}\ \mathrm{pc}$$

### 17.2 Gravitational Wave Key Results

**GW strain amplitude:**
$$h_0 = 2\frac{R_{S1}}{r}\frac{R_{S2}}{R}$$

**GW frequency (twice orbital frequency):**
$$\omega_\mathrm{gw} = 2\omega_s = 2\sqrt{\frac{GM}{R^3}}$$

**GW strain signal:**
$$h(t) = h_0\cos(\omega_\mathrm{gw}\,t)$$

**GW luminosity:**
$$L = \frac{32}{5}\frac{G}{c^5}(\mu R^2\omega_s^3)^2$$

**GW energy flux:**
$$F = \frac{1}{32\pi}\frac{c^3}{G}h_0^2\omega_\mathrm{gw}^2$$

**Total orbital energy:**
$$E_\mathrm{tot} = -\frac{GM\mu}{2R} = -\frac{Gm_1 m_2}{2R}$$

**Chirp mass (definition):**
$$M_\mathrm{chirp} = \frac{(m_1 m_2)^{3/5}}{(m_1 + m_2)^{1/5}}$$

**Chirp mass (from observables):**
$$M_\mathrm{chirp} = \frac{c^3}{G}\left[\frac{5}{96}\pi^{-8/3}\nu_\mathrm{gw}^{-11/3}\dot{\nu}_\mathrm{gw}\right]^{3/5}$$

**Total mass from maximum frequency:**
$$M_\mathrm{tot} = \frac{1}{\pi\sqrt{8}}\frac{c^3}{G\nu_\mathrm{gw}^\mathrm{max}}$$

**Total mass from mass ratio:**
$$M_\mathrm{tot} = \frac{M_\mathrm{chirp}}{[\alpha(1-\alpha)]^{3/5}} \geq 2.3\,M_\mathrm{chirp}$$

**Distance estimate:**
$$r = \frac{16}{\sqrt{5}}\frac{G}{c^4}\frac{\mu R^2\omega_s^2}{h_0}$$

**Redshift corrections:**
$$\nu^\mathrm{det} = \frac{\nu^s}{1+z}, \quad \dot{\nu}^\mathrm{det} = \frac{\dot{\nu}^s}{(1+z)^2}, \quad M_\mathrm{chirp}^\mathrm{det} = (1+z)M_\mathrm{chirp}^s$$

### 17.3 Conceptual Connections: The Big Picture

The session weaves together several major themes of stellar evolution. Understanding their connections is essential for the exam.

**Star â†’ BH:** Massive stars ($M_\mathrm{ZAMS} \gtrsim 25\,M_\odot$) produce iron cores that exceed the OV limit. Whether they explode (SN â†’ NS) or collapse directly (failed SN â†’ BH) depends on the compactness $\xi_{2.5}$ of the pre-collapse iron core. The final BH mass depends on both the explosion mechanism and the wind mass loss history, which is sensitive to metallicity.

**Metallicity â†’ BH mass distribution:** Metal-poor environments (early Universe, metal-poor galaxies) produce the most massive BHs. This is the reason LIGO detects BHâ€“BH mergers with $m_1, m_2 \sim 30$â€“$60\,M_\odot$, while X-ray binaries in the Milky Way (solar metallicity) show $m \sim 5$â€“$15\,M_\odot$.

**BH binary inspiral â†’ GW signal:** The orbital mechanics of the binary are described by Newtonian gravity (adequate for the inspiral stage), with GW emission providing the energy-loss mechanism that drives the spiral-in. The waveform directly encodes the chirp mass (from $\nu_\mathrm{gw}(t)$) and the total mass (from $\nu_\mathrm{gw}^\mathrm{max}$), enabling mass determination from the signal alone.

**NS merger â†’ heavy elements:** The extreme neutron-richness of NS merger ejecta makes them the premier r-process site. Confirmation from GW170817 closes the loop between nuclear physics (BÂ˛FH 1957), GW detection, and multi-messenger astronomy: we can literally weigh the gold produced in a NS merger from the kilonova light curve.

**IMBHs â†’ galaxy formation:** The Magorrian relation connects BH mass to host galaxy mass across ten orders of magnitude. IMBHs in globular clusters, if confirmed, would extend this relation to the lowest mass scales and potentially reveal the seeds from which SMBHs grew in the early Universe.

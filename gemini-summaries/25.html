01

AGB Termination, Severe Mass Loss, & Planetary Nebulae

For stars with initial masses below $M < M^{\text{up}} \approx 8 M_\odot$, the central temperature never reaches the threshold required to ignite stable Carbon burning ($\approx 5 \times 10^8 \text{ K}$). Therefore, the development of a highly degenerate Carbon-Oxygen (C-O) core marks the absolute ending point of their nuclear life.

The final transition from the Asymptotic Giant Branch (AGB) is driven by a catastrophic, high-efficiency mass-loss event known as the Superwind Phase:

AGB Radiative Dust-Driven Wind

The stellar envelope expands dramatically, dropping surface gravity to loose levels:

$$\log g \approx -0.5 \quad \text{[cgs]}$$

At temperatures around $T \approx 1000\text{--}1500 \text{ K}$, elements like Carbon and Silicates condense into dust grains. Radiation pressure pushes these grains outward, and gas-drag coupling ejects the entire convective envelope into space.

Superwind Mass-Loss Rates

During the final thermal pulse cycles, mass loss reaches astronomical scales:

$$\dot{M}_{\text{superwind}} \approx 10^{-4} \text{ to } 10^{-5} M_\odot / \text{year}$$

This rate is over nine orders of magnitude larger than the Solar wind ($\dot{M}_\odot \approx 4 \times 10^{-14} M_\odot / \text{yr}$), stripping the stellar envelope in less than $10^5 \text{ years}$.

Planetary Nebulae Phase (PN)

As the hot, degenerate C-O core is exposed ($T_{\text{eff}} > 30,000\text{ K}$), it emits intense ultraviolet radiation that ionizes the expanding, previously ejected envelope. The fast stellar wind ($\approx 1000\text{--}3000\text{ km/s}$) collides with the slower AGB wind envelope ($\approx 10\text{--}30\text{ km/s}$), producing the shock-heated, glowing gas structures observed as Planetary Nebulae.

02

Microphysics of Electron Degeneracy

White Dwarfs are held up against gravitational collapse entirely by electron degeneracy pressure, a quantum mechanical phenomenon described by Fermi-Dirac statistics and the Pauli Exclusion Principle.

Derivation of degenerate Equations of State

According to quantum mechanics, the phase space volume occupied by a single particle is limited to $h^3$. The density of available quantum states in momentum space $p$ is: $$g(p) dp = \frac{2}{h^3} 4\pi p^2 dp$$ where the factor of $2$ accounts for the two possible electron spin states ($s = \pm 1/2$).

In a completely degenerate state ($T \to 0$), electrons pack tightly into the lowest available energy levels, filling all states up to a maximum momentum known as the Fermi Momentum ($p_F$): $$n_e = \int_{0}^{p_F} \frac{8\pi p^2}{h^3} dp = \frac{8\pi p_F^3}{3h^3} \implies p_F = \left(\frac{3h^3 n_e}{8\pi}\right)^{1/3} = (3\pi^2 \hbar^3 n_e)^{1/3}$$ where the electron number density $n_e$ is related to mass density $\rho$ by: $$n_e = \frac{\rho}{\mu_e m_H}$$

1. Non-Relativistic Degeneracy

For moderate densities, electron velocity is far below $c$ ($v = p/m_e$):

$$P = \frac{1}{3}\int_0^{p_F} v p \, g(p) dp = \frac{1}{3}\int_0^{p_F} \frac{p^2}{m_e} \frac{8\pi p^2}{h^3} dp$$

Integrating yields the non-relativistic equation of state:

$$P_{\text{NR}} = \frac{h^2}{5m_e}\left(\frac{3}{8\pi}\right)^{2/3} n_e^{5/3} \implies P_{\text{NR}} \propto \rho^{5/3}$$
2. Relativistic Degeneracy

At extreme densities, electron velocities approach the speed of light ($v \approx c$):

$$P = \frac{1}{3}\int_0^{p_F} c p \, g(p) dp = \frac{1}{3}\int_0^{p_F} c p \frac{8\pi p^2}{h^3} dp$$

Integrating yields the relativistic equation of state:

$$P_{\text{ER}} = \frac{hc}{4}\left(\frac{3}{8\pi}\right)^{1/3} n_e^{4/3} \implies P_{\text{ER}} \propto \rho^{4/3}$$
03

The Mass-Radius Scaling Relation

Using hydrostatic equilibrium and the degenerate equation of state, we can derive the fundamental mass-radius relation for White Dwarfs. This relation reveals a striking, counter-intuitive result: more massive White Dwarfs are physically smaller.

Scaling Derivation

From the equation of hydrostatic equilibrium:

$$\frac{dP}{dr} = -\frac{GM_r\rho}{r^2} \implies \frac{P}{R} \sim \frac{G M^2}{R^5} \implies P \sim \frac{G M^2}{R^4}$$

The average density of the star scales with its mass and radius as:

$$\rho \sim \frac{M}{R^3}$$

Substituting this density scaling into the non-relativistic equation of state ($P \propto \rho^{5/3}$):

$$\left(\frac{M}{R^3}\right)^{5/3} \sim \frac{G M^2}{R^4} \implies \frac{M^{5/3}}{R^5} \sim \frac{G M^2}{R^4}$$

Solving for the radius $R$ as a function of mass $M$ yields:

$$R \propto M^{-1/3}$$
Fig 1: White Dwarf Mass-Radius relation showing critical drop at 1.4 M☉.
Core Physical Consequence: Density vs. Mass

Combining the relations $R \propto M^{-1/3}$ and $\rho \propto M R^{-3}$ reveals how density scales with mass:

$$\rho \propto M \cdot (M^{-1/3})^{-3} \implies \rho \propto M^2$$

This shows that **adding mass to a White Dwarf significantly compresses it, dramatically increasing its central density**. This compression pushes the stellar gas into relativistic territory.

04

The Chandrasekhar Mass Limit Derivation

As a White Dwarf becomes more massive, its internal density rises. This forces its electrons into relativistic speeds, shifting the equation of state from $P \propto \rho^{5/3}$ toward $P \propto \rho^{4/3}$.

Derivation of the Limit

Substituting the extreme relativistic equation of state ($P \propto \rho^{4/3}$) into our hydrostatic balance relation:

$$\left(\frac{M}{R^3}\right)^{4/3} \sim \frac{G M^2}{R^4} \implies \frac{M^{4/3}}{R^4} \sim \frac{G M^2}{R^4}$$

Notice that the radius $R$ cancels out completely on both sides of the equation:

$$M^{4/3} \sim G M^2 \implies M^{2/3} \sim \frac{1}{G}$$

This reveals that under relativistic conditions, hydrostatic equilibrium can only be maintained at a single, unique mass value, independent of radius: the Chandrasekhar Mass ($M_{\text{Ch}}$).

Performing a rigorous integration using the Lane-Emden equation for a polytrope of index $n=3$ yields:

$$M_{\text{Ch}} = \frac{\omega_3^0}{4\pi}\left( \frac{hc}{G} \right)^{3/2} \left( \frac{1}{\mu_e m_H} \right)^2 \approx 1.4 M_\odot$$
The Physical Limit of Stellar Equilibrium

If a degenerate C-O core exceeds this $1.4 M_\odot$ limit, degeneracy pressure can no longer support the star.

This triggers an immediate, catastrophic gravitational collapse. In binary systems, mass transfer that pushes a White Dwarf past this limit triggers a carbon-detonation explosion, observed as a Type Ia Supernova.

05

White Dwarf Internal Structure

White Dwarfs possess a highly stratified, layered internal structure, reflecting their evolutionary history and the physics of degenerate matter:

1. Degenerate Core

Contains $\approx 99\%$ of the star's total mass, composed of a mixture of Carbon and Oxygen ions immersed in a sea of degenerate electrons.

2. Helium Layer

A thin, non-degenerate layer of Helium directly surrounding the core, typically containing $\approx 10^{-2} M_\odot$.

3. Hydrogen Atmosphere

The outermost insulating boundary layer, consisting of a very thin sheet of Hydrogen with a mass of $\approx 10^{-4} M_\odot$.

06

Thermodynamics of White Dwarf Cooling

White Dwarfs have no internal sources of nuclear fusion. They cool by radiating away their stored thermal energy into the cold vacuum of space.

The Split Role of Electrons and Ions

The thermodynamic cooling of a White Dwarf is governed by a distinct division of labor between its electrons and ions:

  • The Degenerate Electrons (Heat Conductors): Because degenerate electrons have incredibly long mean free paths, their thermal conductivity is extremely high ($K_{\text{cond}} \gg K_{\text{rad}}$). This makes the entire degenerate core virtually isothermal ($T(r) = T_c \approx \text{const}$).
  • The Non-Degenerate Ions (Heat Reservoir): Since the degenerate electrons are locked in their lowest quantum states, they cannot transfer heat or cool down. Instead, the star's thermal energy reservoir is stored entirely in the kinetic energy of its non-degenerate Carbon and Oxygen ions: $$U_i = \frac{3}{2} N_i k_B T_c = \frac{3}{2} \frac{M}{\mu_{\text{ion}} m_H} k_B T_c$$
  • The Outer Atmosphere (Insulating Blanket): The thin, non-degenerate outermost layers of Hydrogen and Helium have high opacity, acting as a thick insulating blanket that controls the rate at which heat can escape from the core.
07

Analytical Proof of Mestel's Cooling Theory

Mestel's cooling theory describes how a White Dwarf's luminosity decays over time by modeling the rate of heat transport through its thin outer radiative envelope.

1. Modeling the Radiative Envelope

We assume the outer envelope is thin, radiative, and non-degenerate, with opacity described by Kramers' law:

$$\kappa = \kappa_0 \rho T^{-3.5}$$

Integrating the equations of radiative transport and hydrostatic equilibrium yields the pressure and temperature profiles of the envelope:

$$P(r) = \left( \frac{2}{8.5} \frac{g k_B}{\kappa_0 \mu m_H} \frac{M}{L} \right)^{1/2} T(r)^{4.25} \implies \rho(r) \propto \left(\frac{M}{L}\right)^{1/2} T(r)^{3.25}$$

2. Defining the Degeneracy Boundary

At the boundary between the non-degenerate envelope and the degenerate core, the ideal gas pressure must equal the non-relativistic degenerate electron pressure:

$$\frac{k_B \rho_{\text{bd}} T_{\text{bd}}}{\mu_e m_H} \approx C_{\text{NR}} \rho_{\text{bd}}^{5/3} \implies \rho_{\text{bd}} \propto T_{\text{bd}}^{1.5}$$

Equating this boundary density $\rho_{\text{bd}}$ to our envelope density profile at the boundary radius ($T_{\text{bd}} \approx T_c$, since the core is isothermal):

$$T_c^{1.5} \propto \left(\frac{M}{L}\right)^{1/2} T_c^{3.25} \implies L \propto M T_c^{3.5}$$

3. Deriving Luminosity Decay Over Time

Since the thermal energy is stored in the ions ($U \propto M T_c$), the stellar luminosity is equal to the rate of energy loss:

$$L = -\frac{dU}{dt} \propto -M \frac{dT_c}{dt}$$

Substituting our relation for $L$ ($L \propto M T_c^{3.5}$) into the cooling equation:

$$-M \frac{dT_c}{dt} \propto M T_c^{3.5} \implies \frac{dT_c}{dt} \propto -T_c^{3.5}$$

Integrating this differential equation from $t = 0$ to $t$:

$$\int T_c^{-3.5} dT_c \propto -\int dt \implies T_c^{-2.5} \propto t \implies T_c(t) \propto t^{-0.4}$$

Finally, substituting $T_c(t)$ back into our luminosity equation ($L \propto T_c^{3.5}$) gives Mestel's Cooling Law:

$$L(t) \propto t^{-7/5} = t^{-1.4} \implies t_{\text{cool}} \propto L^{-5/7}$$

Mestel's Law shows that as a White Dwarf cools, its luminosity drops rapidly at first ($L \propto t^{-1.4}$), but its cooling rate slows down over time as the core's thermal energy is depleted.

08

The Microphysics of Core Crystallization

As a White Dwarf's core cools and its temperature drops below $T_c < 10^7\text{ K}$, the electrostatic potential energy between its ions begins to exceed their thermal kinetic energy. This triggers a phase transition called crystallization.

The Plasma Parameter ($\Gamma$)

The state of the ion plasma is characterized by the dimensionless parameter $\Gamma$, defined as the ratio of Coulomb energy to thermal kinetic energy:

$$\Gamma = \frac{E_{\text{Coulomb}}}{E_{\text{Thermal}}} = \frac{Z^2 e^2}{a_i k_B T_c}$$

where $Z$ is the atomic number of the ions ($Z = 6$ for Carbon, $Z = 8$ for Oxygen), and $a_i = \left(\frac{3}{4\pi n_i}\right)^{1/3}$ is the average spacing between ions.

  • $\Gamma \ll 1$: Weakly coupled, ideal gaseous plasma.
  • $\Gamma \approx 1$: Strongly coupled liquid state.
  • $\Gamma \ge 178$: The electrostatic forces freeze the ions into a solid, body-centered cubic (BCC) crystal lattice.

Thermodynamic Cooling Delay: Core Fractionation

Crystallization slows the cooling of a White Dwarf through two primary mechanisms, keeping it brighter for longer:

  1. Latent Heat: The phase transition from liquid to solid releases latent heat, providing an additional energy source of $\approx 0.77 k_B$ per ion.
  2. Gravitational Settling (Fractionation): Because Oxygen has a higher charge-to-mass ratio than Carbon, Oxygen freezes first and sinks to the center of the core, displacing lighter Carbon upward. This chemical separation releases gravitational potential energy, slowing down the star's cooling rate by several gigayears.
09

Spectral Classification & Atmospheric Composition

Although White Dwarf cores are composed of Carbon and Oxygen, their strong surface gravity ($\log g \approx 8$) drives rapid chemical settlement. Heavier elements sink to the bottom, leaving only the lightest elements visible at the surface.

White Dwarfs are classified into distinct spectral types based on their atmospheric composition:

Spectral Class Atmospheric Composition Key Spectral Signatures Approximate Fraction
DA Pure Hydrogen Envelope Strong, broad Lyman & Balmer absorption lines ~ 80%
DB Pure Helium Envelope ($1.5 \times 10^4\text{ K} < T < 3 \times 10^4\text{ K}$) Neutral Helium (He I) absorption features ~ 15%
DO Hot Helium Envelope ($T > 4.5 \times 10^4\text{ K}$) Ionized Helium (He II) spectral lines ~ 1%
DQ Carbon-rich Atmosphere Atomic or molecular Carbon lines (Swan Bands) ~ Rare
DC Featureless / Continuous ($T_{\text{eff}}$ too cool, or line strengths below detection threshold) No absorption lines resolvable at any wavelength — a smooth, "blank" continuum ~ 14%
The DC Class: Absence of Evidence, Not Evidence of Absence

A DC white dwarf is not a distinct atmospheric composition — it is simply a WD, of either H- or He-rich makeup, whose surface is too cool ($T_{\text{eff}} \lesssim 5{,}000\text{ K}$) or whose residual line strengths have dropped below spectroscopic detectability. Roughly 14% of the observed WD population falls into this featureless class, meaning the true DA:DB split is somewhat obscured at the faint, cool end of the cooling sequence — precisely where WD cosmochronology (Section 11) does its most important work.

10

Observational CMD cooling Diagnostics

In theoretical Hertzsprung-Russell diagrams ($\log T_{\text{eff}} - \log L$), the cooling track of a White Dwarf is a simple straight line because its radius remains nearly constant: $$\log L = 4 \log T_{\text{eff}} + \text{const}$$

However, in observational Color-Magnitude Diagrams (such as $V$ vs $B-V$), this cooling track curves significantly. This curvature is driven by changes in the atmosphere's opacity:

  • As the surface temperature cools, the dominant opacity sources in the atmosphere change. This alters the bolometric corrections, bending the cooling track.
  • Hydrogen-line blanketing and Collision-Induced Absorption (CIA) from molecular hydrogen ($\text{H}_2$) suppress the red and infrared flux of very cool WDs, shifting their colors back toward the blue at late times.
11

The White Dwarf Luminosity Function & The Slow-Cooling Anomaly

Because Mestel's cooling law gives a one-to-one map between a White Dwarf's luminosity and its elapsed cooling time, a whole population of WDs can be turned into a clock. This observational application is called the White Dwarf Luminosity Function (WDLF) — the number density of WDs as a function of luminosity (or absolute magnitude) in a cluster or field population.

The WDLF as a Cosmic Chronometer

As a stellar population ages, its faintest, coolest, longest-cooling White Dwarfs pile up at progressively lower luminosities. Because no WD can be older than the population itself, the WDLF has a sharp faint-end cutoff — the luminosity of the very first WDs the population ever produced. Converting that cutoff luminosity into a cooling time via the same scaling that falls out of Mestel's Law:

$$t_{\text{cool}} = 8.8 \times 10^6 \left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right)^{5/7} \left(\frac{L}{L_\odot}\right)^{-5/7} \ \text{yr}$$

gives a direct age estimate for the population — a genuine "cosmic chronometer," independent of main-sequence stellar-evolution models entirely.

Fig 3: Schematic WDLF — the faint-end cutoff marks the coolest (oldest) White Dwarfs the population has produced.
Caveat: WD Chronometry vs. Main-Sequence Turnoff Dating

Despite its elegance, WDLF dating is systematically less precise than dating a cluster from its main-sequence turnoff (MS-TO): the WDLF cutoff is shallow and depends sensitively on the cooling models used, whereas MS-TO age depends on the much steeper, better-calibrated main-sequence evolutionary timescale.

The Chen et al. (2021) Slowly-Cooling White Dwarf Anomaly

A direct challenge to naive WD chronometry came from comparing "twin" globular clusters — pairs matched in age and metallicity but differing in Horizontal Branch (HB) morphology (i.e. how many stars populate the hot, blue end of the HB). Clusters with a pronounced blue-HB population were found to host an excess of anomalously bright, slowly-cooling White Dwarfs relative to the standard Mestel prediction.

The Progenitor Link: E-HB Stars

Blue/extreme Horizontal Branch (E-HB) stars lose almost their entire envelope on the RGB. As a result, they skip the AGB phase — and its Third Dredge-Up — entirely, jumping directly from the HB to the WD cooling track.

The Physical Consequence

Skipping the AGB lets these WDs retain a residual Hydrogen envelope thicker than $10^{-4}\,M_{\text{WD}}$ — thick enough to sustain stable residual H-burning at the surface, which acts as an extra heat source and slows the cooling rate below the canonical Mestel curve.

The evidence trail was cross-checked observationally: blue-HB clusters exhibit a measurably lower ratio of AGB stars to HB stars ($N_{\text{AGB}}/N_{\text{HB}}$) than red-HB clusters of the same age — direct confirmation that the missing AGB stars are the progenitors of the excess bright WDs. Cooling models that mix a standard population with a fraction of these H-burning "slow coolers" successfully reproduce the observed WDLF shape.

Exam Trap: Chronometry Bias

If a cluster's WD population secretly contains slow-cooling, thick-H-envelope White Dwarfs and this is not corrected for, the WDLF-derived age comes out systematically too young — the anomalously bright "old" WDs mimic the luminosity of a genuinely younger population. This is on top of WDLF dating already being intrinsically less precise than MS-TO dating even in the idealized, non-anomalous case.

Active Recall & Focus Quiz

Validate your retention of key physics concepts from the slides.

Question 1

What is the physical mechanism that drives dust-driven mass loss in stars at the tip of the Asymptotic Giant Branch (AGB)?

Correct! The combination of very low surface gravity, dust condensation, and radiation pressure drives the rapid mass loss of the superwind phase.

Question 2

What is the physical mass-radius relation for a non-relativistic electron degenerate White Dwarf?

Correct! The non-relativistic equation of state ($P \propto \rho^{5/3}$) demands that radius decreases as mass increases: $R \propto M^{-1/3}$.

Question 3

Why does the radius completely cancel out of the hydrostatic equation under extreme relativistic electron degeneracy?

Correct! Because the pressure ($P \propto M^{4/3}/R^4$) and hydrostatic weight ($W \propto M^2/R^4$) have the same $1/R^4$ dependence, the radius cancels out, establishing a single limiting mass.

Question 4

What is the physical division of labor between electrons and ions in a cooling White Dwarf?

Correct! Electrons are locked in their degenerate states and conduct heat efficiently, while the non-degenerate carbon-oxygen ions store the thermal energy reservoir.

Question 5

How does Mestel's cooling theory relate the cooling timescale ($t$) of a White Dwarf to its luminosity ($L$)?

Correct! Mestel's cooling derivation yields $t \propto L^{-5/7}$ (or $L \propto t^{-1.4}$), which describes how the cooling rate slows as the star's thermal energy is depleted.

Question 6

What threshold of the plasma parameter ($\Gamma$) triggers crystallization in a White Dwarf's core?

Correct! When $\Gamma = E_{\text{Coulomb}}/E_{\text{Thermal}} \ge 178$, the core ions freeze into a solid body-centered cubic (BCC) crystal lattice.

Question 7

How does core crystallization delay the cooling of a White Dwarf?

Correct! Both the release of latent heat and the sinking of heavier Oxygen (gravitational settling/fractionation) release energy, delaying cooling.

Question 8

Why are only Hydrogen (DA) or Helium (DB/DO) lines visible in most White Dwarf spectra?

Correct! High surface gravity separates the atmosphere by mass, sinking carbon and oxygen and leaving only hydrogen or helium visible at the surface.

Question 9

What is the primary core composition of a standard White Dwarf born from a star with an initial mass of $1 M_\odot$?

Correct! Stars with $M < 8 M_\odot$ end their nuclear lives after core Helium burning, leaving behind a degenerate Carbon-Oxygen (C-O) core.

Question 10

Why does a White Dwarf's cooling track curve in an observational CMD, unlike in a theoretical H-R diagram?

Correct! While a White Dwarf cools at a nearly constant radius (appearing as a straight line in theoretical HR diagrams), shifts in atmospheric opacity and molecular absorption bend its track in observational color-magnitude space.

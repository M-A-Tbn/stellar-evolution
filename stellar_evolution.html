1 M☉ — The Sun's Path

Ten billion quiet years burning hydrogen, a sudden identity crisis at core exhaustion, a violent but invisible flash, a second fire on the horizontal branch, a third double-shelled fire on the asymptotic giant branch, and a slow million-year fade into a crystallizing ember of carbon and oxygen.

◆The Whole Journey, at a Glance

The star's entire path through the Hertzsprung–Russell diagram: from arrival on the Zero Age Main Sequence (Point 1) to the final white dwarf cooling track. Faint gray curves hint at how other masses trace very different paths through the same diagram.

1Prelude — From Cloud to Fire

A 1 M☉ star begins as a cold, diffuse protostellar cloud that collapses under gravity, settles onto the fully-convective Hayashi track, then contracts on the Kelvin–Helmholtz timescale for tens of millions of years. Once the core reaches $T_c\sim10^7$ K hydrogen reactions begin, and the full pp chain activates at $T_c\approx1.4\times10^7$ K — Point 1, the Zero Age Main Sequence.

2The Long Burn — Main Sequence

The Sun burns hydrogen almost entirely via the proton-proton (pp) chain (~99% of luminosity, below the ~1.2 $M_\odot$ CNO-transition mass). Its gentle temperature sensitivity keeps burning diffuse, so the core stays radiative, wrapped in a convective envelope. This efficient, large fuel reservoir makes the main sequence by far the longest phase of the star's life.

$$t_{MS} \cong 10^{10}\,M^{-3}\ \text{yr}$$
Main-sequence lifetime scaling ($L\propto M^4$, fuel $\propto M$) — gives ~9–10 Gyr for 1 M☉.

3Point 2 — A Star Divided

The main sequence ends at Point 2 ($X_c=0.05$), transitioning seamlessly (no "hook," unlike CNO-burning stars) into shell burning. This leaves a sharp chemical split: an almost-pure-helium core ($\mu_i\approx1.33$) far "heavier" per particle than the envelope ($\mu_e\approx0.63$). Matching pressure and temperature at the boundary then forces the envelope to physically expand — the origin of the Red Giant Branch.

$$\mu = \frac{1}{2X + \tfrac{3}{4}Y + \tfrac{1}{2}Z}$$
Mean molecular weight; a much higher $\mu$ in the core than the envelope drives the required expansion.

4Climbing to the Tip — Sub-Giant and Red Giant Branch

As the envelope expands at roughly constant luminosity, $T_{eff}$ falls (Sub-Giant Branch), until the star hits its Hayashi track and luminosity itself must climb — the near-vertical Red Giant Branch, powered by a hydrogen shell around a growing helium core. Deepening convection dredges CNO material to the surface (First Dredge-Up), and the ascent halts at a universal RGB-tip luminosity ($\log L/L_\odot\approx3.45$), a reliable standard candle.

$$L = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T_{eff}^4$$
Stefan–Boltzmann law: a growing radius at near-fixed luminosity forces surface temperature down (SGB), until the star hits its Hayashi track — the cool boundary where no stable model exists further right — and luminosity itself must climb instead (RGB).

5The Helium Flash

The degenerate core's pressure doesn't depend on temperature, so when triple-alpha ignites at the RGB tip it runs away rather than self-regulating (peak $L\sim10^{11}\,L_\odot$ internally). All of that energy goes into lifting degeneracy — none escapes — so the star never visibly brightens. A cascade of off-centre flashes works inward until degeneracy is fully lifted and the star settles onto the stable Zero Age Horizontal Branch.

$$P \propto \rho^{5/3}$$
Degenerate electron pressure is temperature-independent — the reason there is no negative feedback to stop the runaway.

6Second Fire — The Horizontal Branch

On the ZAHB the star runs two fires at once: a convective helium-burning core (triple-alpha, then $^{12}\text{C}+\alpha\to{}^{16}\text{O}$) and the still-active hydrogen shell above it, which usually outshines the core. Helium burning yields far less energy per unit mass than hydrogen burning, so the phase is brief — ~100 Myr, about 1% of the main-sequence lifetime — with core/shell competition producing a small loop in the HR diagram before helium exhaustion ends it.

7The Asymptotic Giant Branch — Double Fire

A new helium shell ignites around the inert CO core while the old hydrogen shell fades; the two shells then alternately reignite in thermal pulses. Each pulse convectively mixes fresh carbon and oxygen into the intershell region, later dredged to the surface (Third Dredge-Up) and enriched further by the slow neutron-capture s-process. Dust-driven superwinds ($\dot M\sim10^{-4}\,M_\odot\,\text{yr}^{-1}$) then strip the envelope down to almost nothing, ending the AGB.

8Casting Off the Shroud — Planetary Nebula

The exposed remnant keeps contracting at nearly constant luminosity, so by Stefan–Boltzmann its surface temperature climbs; once it passes ~30,000 K it ionizes the previously ejected envelope into a glowing planetary nebula. This ejected material — CNO-processed gas, carbon, oxygen, and s-process elements — carries the star's nucleosynthetic history back into the interstellar medium.

9The Ember — Carbon-Oxygen White Dwarf

What's left is a naked, degenerate carbon-oxygen core (~0.55–0.6 $M_\odot$), held up purely by temperature-independent electron degeneracy pressure — more massive white dwarfs are actually smaller ($R\propto M^{-1/3}$), comfortably below the $1.44\,M_\odot$ Chandrasekhar limit here. With radius fixed, Stefan–Boltzmann forces the cooling track into the straight slope-4 line in Fig. 1 as leftover thermal energy slowly radiates away — the fate awaiting the Sun, and ~95% of all stars in the Galaxy.

$$L_{WD}(t) = L_0\left[1+\frac{5}{2}\frac{t}{\tau_0}\right]^{-7/5}$$
Mestel cooling law: fast fading while young, then progressively slower.

0.5 M☉ — The Star That Never Moves

A cool, dim ember burning pure hydrogen via the pp chain for roughly eighty billion years — six times the current age of the universe. Every 0.5 M☉ star ever formed, including the very first generation after the Big Bang, is still sitting exactly where it started: on the main sequence, with nowhere else to be for a very long time yet.

1Prelude — Contraction to Ignition

Like every star, a 0.5 M☉ protostar collapses onto the fully convective Hayashi track and contracts on the Kelvin–Helmholtz timescale. Because it sits comfortably above the $0.08\,M_\odot$ brown-dwarf cut-off, its core does reach the pp-chain ignition threshold ($T_c\approx1.4\times10^7$ K) — Point 1, the Zero Age Main Sequence — though the contraction takes longer than it did for the Sun, since less gravitational energy is available to release per unit time.

2Structure — Deep, But Not Quite Fully Convective

It is tempting to call a star this small "fully convective," but the course's own structural boundary puts that regime strictly below $0.3\,M_\odot$ (§5.4 of the Main Sequence notes). At $0.5\,M_\odot$ this star instead sits in the middle regime — a radiative core wrapped in a convective envelope — just one that runs unusually deep, because low $T_{eff}$ drives up Kramers opacity ($\kappa\propto T^{-3.5}$) and partial ionization suppresses $\nabla_{ad}$ across a much larger fraction of the star's radius than in the Sun.

3Why So Dim — the Lower Main-Sequence Relation

Below the CNO-transition mass, the course's mass-luminosity relation is steep: $L\propto M^4$. A star at half the Sun's mass is therefore not half as luminous but roughly a sixteenth as luminous.

$$L \approx M^4 \implies (0.5)^4 = 0.0625\ L_\odot$$
Lower main-sequence mass-luminosity relation ($M\lesssim1.2\,M_\odot$, pp-chain). Combined with $T_{eff}\approx3800$ K and $R\approx0.58\,R_\odot$, the Stefan–Boltzmann law $L=4\pi R^2\sigma T_{eff}^4$ reproduces this same 0.06 $L_\odot$ almost exactly — the three numbers are mutually consistent.

4The Longest Burn in the Course

The same steep mass dependence that makes this star faint also makes it patient: the fuel reservoir scales with $M$ while the burn rate scales with $L\propto M^4$, so lifetime scales as $M^{-3}$.

$$t_{MS} \cong 10^{10}\,M^{-3}\ \text{yr} \implies 10^{10}\times(0.5)^{-3} = 8\times10^{10}\ \text{yr}$$
Roughly six times the Hubble time (~13.8 Gyr). The course notes independently flag $\sim0.8\,M_\odot$ as the mass whose main-sequence lifetime equals the age of the universe — at $0.5\,M_\odot$ this star is comfortably inside that "never yet evolved" population.

5A Fate Written on Paper Only

The course's mass-boundary table places $0.5\,M_\odot$ as the minimum mass at which a helium core could ever reach the $M_c^\text{He}\approx0.5\,M_\odot$ threshold needed to trigger the degenerate helium flash; below it, stars are destined to become helium white dwarfs without ever reaching the flash, the Horizontal Branch, or the AGB. This star sits essentially on that line. In principle, over a timescale many multiples of the current age of the universe, it would slowly exhaust its core, contract, and settle into a He-WD. In practice, no star of this mass has ever had remotely enough time to do so — this is a theoretical projection from the physics, not an observed evolutionary path. Every real 0.5 M☉ star in the universe today is precisely where Point 1 left it.

◆The Only Point That Matters

There is no evolutionary track to draw. This star's entire known history is a single point on the ZAMS — cooler and fainter than the Sun's — with a barely perceptible creep in place as it slowly brightens over cosmic time.

◆How Long Does Each Phase Last?

There is only one phase to show, because only one phase has ever happened.

This is not a story with an ending yet — it is a star caught in the first and, so far, only chapter of its life. Everything past the main sequence for this mass — the eventual helium white dwarf — is an extrapolation from the same physics that governs the 1 and 1.5 M☉ tracks, not a phase any star has lived to reach.

1.5 M☉ — The pp → CNO Transition

A star straddling the line where the proton-proton chain gives way to the carbon-nitrogen-oxygen cycle — burning both at once, running a small convective core, and finishing its main sequence in a sixth of the Sun's time. Everything that follows — the degenerate flash, the horizontal branch, the double-shelled AGB, the crystallizing ember — is the same script the Sun will read, just performed on a faster clock from a hotter, brighter start.

1Prelude — Arrival on the ZAMS

Pre-main-sequence contraction proceeds as for any star, ending when the core reaches the pp-chain ignition temperature — but at $1.5\,M_\odot$ the core is already hot enough ($T_c\approx1.81\times10^7$ K, per the course's ZAMS data table) to run the CNO cycle alongside it. Point 1 is reached with both chains active from the start.

2The Long Burn — Mixed pp/CNO, Convective Core

At $1.5\,M_\odot$ this star sits just above the $\sim\!1.2\,M_\odot$ transition mass, so the extremely temperature-sensitive CNO cycle ($\epsilon_{CNO}\propto T^{15}$) already dominates energy production, concentrating it enough to drive a small convective core — unlike the Sun's fully radiative one.

$$L \approx M^{3.6} \implies (1.5)^{3.6} \approx 4.3\ L_\odot$$
Broken power-law mass-luminosity relation for $M\gtrsim1.2\,M_\odot$ (shallower than the lower main sequence's $M^4$, because CNO burning caps how steeply core temperature — and hence luminosity — needs to climb with mass).

3A Sixth of the Sun's Time

Higher luminosity burns through a larger fuel reservoir far faster than the extra fuel itself compensates for.

$$t_{MS} \cong 10^{10}\,M^{-3}\ \text{yr} \implies 1.7\times10^{9}\ \text{yr (table value)}$$
The course table's exact value is $1.7\times10^{9}$ yr (the simple $M^{-3}$ scaling law gives $2.96\times10^9$ yr), about six times shorter than the Sun's, for a ZAMS mass only 50% larger.

4Point 2 — A Hook, Not a Smooth Turn

Because the convective core keeps its hydrogen well-mixed, the whole core runs out of fuel simultaneously rather than gradually from the centre outward. With no fusion anywhere for a brief interval, the star contracts under gravity, heating up before the hydrogen shell ignites — the characteristic hook at the main-sequence turnoff, a direct fingerprint of a convective core. The course notes cite this exact feature observed at the turnoff of the globular cluster NGC 1978 ($M_{TO}\approx1.5\,M_\odot$), a direct observational confirmation of the theory.

5The RGB and the Flash — Same Script, Faster Reel

Once the hydrogen shell settles in, the sub-giant and red-giant ascent proceeds exactly as it does for the Sun: a growing degenerate helium core drives luminosity up the Hayashi track until it hits the universal RGB-tip luminosity ($\log L/L_\odot\approx3.45$) at the same $M_c^\text{He}\approx0.5\,M_\odot$ core mass that triggers every sub-$2.2\,M_\odot$ flash. The runaway triple-alpha ignition is again entirely absorbed in lifting electron degeneracy — no brightening reaches the surface — and the star settles onto the Zero Age Horizontal Branch.

6Horizontal Branch to AGB Tip

Core helium burning (triple-alpha, then $^{12}\text{C}+\alpha\to{}^{16}\text{O}$) runs alongside the reactivated hydrogen shell for roughly the same order of a hundred million years the Sun's HB phase will last — the physics setting this duration is essentially the same near-universal core mass and burning rate. Helium exhaustion then hands off to a double-shelled AGB, with alternating thermal pulses, third dredge-up, and s-process enrichment, before superwinds strip the envelope and expose a hot core.

7The Ember — Carbon-Oxygen White Dwarf

What remains is a degenerate CO core, comfortably under the $1.44\,M_\odot$ Chandrasekhar limit, cooling along the same Stefan–Boltzmann-mandated straight line in the HR diagram as every other CO white dwarf.

$$L_{WD}(t) = L_0\left[1+\frac{5}{2}\frac{t}{\tau_0}\right]^{-7/5}$$
Mestel cooling law — fast fading while young, then progressively slower, exactly as for the 1 M☉ track.

◆How Long Does Each Phase Last?

The main sequence still dominates, just less overwhelmingly than for the Sun — 1.7 Gyr is a much smaller multiple of the roughly $10^8$–$10^9$ yr giant-branch phases that follow. As with the 1 M☉ page, the bar below is compressed for legibility; read the printed durations as the true values.

A hotter, faster-burning sibling of the Sun's own story — different starting point, same ending. The main-sequence hook is this track's one genuinely distinct signature; everything downstream of the RGB tip is close to a rerun.

2The Long (but Brief) Burn — Convective-Core Main Sequence

At $2.25\,M_\odot$, CNO's steep temperature sensitivity concentrates energy production tightly enough to drive a genuinely convective core, homogenizing hydrogen within it and enlarging the effective fuel reservoir compared to a purely radiative core.

$$L \approx M^{3.6} \implies (2.25)^{3.6} \approx 18.5\ L_\odot$$
Same broken power-law relation used for the 1.5 M☉ track ($M\gtrsim1.2\,M_\odot$, CNO-dominated). Combined with $T_{eff}\approx8700$ K and $R\approx1.9\,R_\odot$, Stefan–Boltzmann reproduces this luminosity almost exactly.

3MS Lifetime — 5×10⁸ Years

The exact value from the course's own ZAMS lifetime table.

$$t_{MS} \cong 10^{10}\,M^{-3}\ \text{yr} \implies 5\times10^{8}\ \text{yr (table value)}$$
Twenty times shorter than the Sun's, for barely more than double the mass — the steep $M^{-3}$ scaling doing most of the work, though the table value ($5\times10^8$ yr) is slightly lower than the formula's $8.8\times10^8$ yr.

4Point 2 — The Hook, More Pronounced

As with the 1.5 M☉ track, the convective core exhausts hydrogen everywhere at once, forcing a brief gravitational-contraction phase before shell burning ignites — the same hook feature the course notes confirm observationally at the turnoff of NGC 1978. At this higher mass the core is more centrally concentrated still, so the hook is, if anything, a slightly sharper feature.

5The RGB and the MHeF Line

The course's three critical mass boundaries (§24.1) place $M^\text{He}=2.2\,M_\odot$ as the line between a degenerate helium core that ignites in a runaway flash at the RGB tip (below the line) and a non-degenerate core that ignites quietly at moderate luminosity, well before any RGB tip is reached (above the line). At $2.25\,M_\odot$ this star sits only 2% above that line — squarely inside the range where the course notes describe the transition as a genuine physical crossover rather than a sharp cliff, sensitive to the precise treatment of convective overshoot and composition.

6A Marginal Flash

Treated on the degenerate side of the line, this star's core still reaches the same $M_c^\text{He}\approx0.5\,M_\odot$ ignition mass and the same runaway triple-alpha instability described for the lower-mass tracks — but because it starts closer to the non-degenerate regime, the flash is milder and the RGB is shorter and less densely populated than for a $1$ or $1.5\,M_\odot$ star. Just above this mass, the same physical ingredients (a growing He core, rising $T_c$) instead produce quiet, self-regulating ignition with no flash at all — the qualitative difference is entirely about whether electron degeneracy pressure has taken hold in the core before $3\alpha$ conditions are reached.

7The RGB Phase Transition — A Population Signature

Because this mass sits at the turnoff for stellar populations roughly 1–2 Gyr old, it marks the RGB Phase Transition described in the course notes (§9.3): once a population's turnoff mass drops below $2.2\,M_\odot$, a well-populated, extended Red Giant Branch suddenly appears in its colour-magnitude diagram, and red giants — rather than hot main-sequence stars — come to dominate the integrated light. A star at exactly this mass is, in a population sense, the turnoff star marking that transition as it happens.

8Horizontal Branch, AGB, and the Ember

Past the flash, the story converges again with the lower-mass tracks: a ZAHB phase burning helium in the core and hydrogen in a shell, a double-shelled AGB with thermal pulses and dredge-up, superwind mass loss, and a slow contraction to a degenerate carbon-oxygen white dwarf — cooling along the same Mestel-law, slope-4 track in the HR diagram as every other CO white dwarf in this mass range.

$$L_{WD}(t) = L_0\left[1+\frac{5}{2}\frac{t}{\tau_0}\right]^{-7/5}$$
Mestel cooling law, unchanged from the lower-mass tracks — only the exact WD mass (and hence radius) differs slightly.

◆The Whole Journey, at a Glance

Above $M_\text{HeF}\approx2.2\,M_\odot$, helium ignites quietly — there is no flash, no luminosity drop. Instead, the interesting drama happens afterward: core helium burning drives the star through a pronounced blue loop, a real excursion to much hotter temperatures than the RGB tip, before it settles back onto the Hayashi track for the AGB. The shaded band marks the Cepheid instability strip the loop crosses.

1Prelude — A Convective Core Ignites

At 3 M☉ the core is well above the ~1.2 M☉ threshold where the CNO cycle takes over from the pp chain, and the star burns hydrogen in a convective core wrapped by a radiative envelope — the mirror image of the Sun's structure. The steep temperature sensitivity of CNO burning concentrates energy generation strongly toward the center, which is what forces convection there in the first place.

$$t_{MS} \cong 10^{10}\,M^{-3}\ \text{yr}$$
The course table's exact value is $2\times10^8$ yr (the $M^{-3}$ scaling law gives $3.7\times10^8$ yr), roughly 50× shorter than the Sun's main-sequence life.

2Point 2 — TAMS and the Hertzsprung Gap

Core hydrogen exhaustion at Point 2 shows the characteristic "hook" that CNO-burning stars trace on the HRD, absent in the Sun's smooth transition. The subsequent crossing of Segment 4–5 is fast for intermediate-mass stars — the notes quote $10^7$ yr at 2 M☉ and $10^5$ yr at 10 M☉, so 3 M☉ crosses on an intermediate timescale of order $10^6$–$10^7$ yr — sparsely populating the Hertzsprung gap in any young cluster's CMD.

3A Short, Poorly Populated Red Giant Branch

Because $M > M_\text{HeF} = 2.2\,M_\odot$, the helium core never crosses the electron-degeneracy boundary $\log T = \tfrac{2}{3}\log\rho + 5$ after H-burning ends — it stays ideal-gas throughout. Per the course's summary table, this means faster RGB evolution and a shorter, more poorly populated giant branch than a solar-mass star shows, with helium igniting at moderate luminosity rather than at a sharply defined tip.

4Quiet Helium Ignition — No Flash

Ignition proceeds at $T\sim10^8$ K, $\rho\sim10^4\,\text{g cm}^{-3}$, fully non-degenerate — thermoregulation self-stabilizes the 3$\alpha$ reaction just as it does on the main sequence. There is no runaway, no off-center flash cascade, and critically no drop in surface luminosity: the transition into core He-burning is smooth, in direct contrast to the Sun's invisible-but-violent helium flash at the RGB tip.

5The Blue Loop — Segments A and B

Leaving the ignition point, convective expansion of the helium core pushes the overlying H-burning shell to cooler layers, reducing its efficiency; starved from below, the envelope contracts and the surface heats up — Segment A, the blueward leg. Once core expansion slows and the core itself begins to contract, the rising 3$\alpha$ rate reverses the argument: the envelope re-expands and the surface cools again — Segment B, back toward the red. The loop's blueward reach — all the way to $T_\text{eff}\approx10{,}000$ K — is the whole reason this mass range matters observationally.

6Crossing the Instability Strip — Born a Cepheid

The Pulsating Stars notes place the instability strip at $\log T_\text{eff}\approx3.80$–$3.85$ (roughly 6300–7100 K), and Classical Cepheids — Population I, intermediate-mass He-burning supergiants — occupy $\log L/L_\odot\approx3.5$–$4.5$ within it. This star's loop luminosity ($\log L/L_\odot\approx2.6$–$2.9$) sits near the strip's lower-luminosity edge, but the geometry is the same: the slow, He-burning-phase crossing (the notes' "second crossing") is the dominant source of observed Cepheids, since the star lingers in the strip for a real fraction of its He-burning lifetime rather than flashing through it.

Why it mattersThe period–luminosity relation calibrated on exactly this crossing is the rung of the cosmic distance ladder that anchors extragalactic distances — the reason intermediate-mass blue loops are a cornerstone of observational cosmology, not just a curiosity of stellar structure.

7Second Fire — The CO Core Recipe

Core helium burning runs $3\alpha\to{}^{12}\text{C}$ followed by $^{12}\text{C}+\alpha\to{}^{16}\text{O}+\gamma$; because the two-body $^{12}\text{C}+\alpha$ channel gains relative importance as helium is depleted, the core ends up oxygen-rich. The exact final C/O ratio remains uncertain — the notes flag the $^{12}\text{C}+\alpha$ cross-section at stellar energies as an open nuclear-physics problem.

8The Asymptotic Giant Branch — a Muted Second Dredge-Up

Helium-shell ignition around the inert CO core starts the AGB and pushes the star back toward the Hayashi track. But the Second Dredge-Up only reaches the surface in stars above 4–5 M☉ — so at 3 M☉ this event is essentially absent or negligible, distinguishing this track from the 5 M☉ case. The later Thermal-Pulse AGB still runs its full course: alternating H/He shells, Third Dredge-Up, s-process enrichment, and a superwind ($\dot M\sim10^{-4}\,M_\odot\,\text{yr}^{-1}$) that strips the envelope.

9The Ember — Carbon-Oxygen White Dwarf

Because 3 M☉ sits comfortably below $M_\text{up}\approx8\,M_\odot$, the CO core becomes electron-degenerate after He-burning and never ignites carbon. The envelope disperses as a planetary nebula, leaving a degenerate carbon-oxygen remnant supported by $P\propto\rho^{5/3}$ electron pressure that cools along the slope-4 Stefan–Boltzmann track — the same fate as the Sun, just reached in a fraction of the time.

As with every mass, the main sequence dominates — just far less overwhelmingly than for the Sun, since post-MS phases scale up faster than $t_{MS}$ shrinks. Segment widths below are illustrative and non-linear; the printed durations are the physically meaningful numbers.

◆The Whole Journey, at a Glance

This is the mass the course uses as its own textbook illustration: a fast, hot main sequence; a sub-giant structure so centrally concentrated that half the star's radius holds barely a sixth of its mass; a quiet helium ignition tracked point by lettered point (E, G, K, I); and a blue loop hot enough to clear the Cepheid strip entirely before the double-shell AGB takes over.

1Prelude — Deep Convective Core, Fast Burn

At 5 M☉, CNO burning fully dominates the core, producing a large convective core and pushing luminosity up along the shallower upper-main-sequence relation $L\approx M^{3.6}$. The fuel is abundant but consumed at a punishing rate.

$$t_{MS} \cong 10^{10}\,M^{-3}\ \text{yr}$$
The course table's exact value is $7\times10^7$ yr (the $M^{-3}$ scaling law gives $8\times10^7$ yr), about 140× shorter than the Sun's.

2Point 2 to Point 3 — A Rapid Hertzsprung Gap Crossing

Core H-exhaustion again shows the CNO "hook," and the crossing of Segment 4–5 is rapid — between the notes' quoted $10^7$ yr (2 M☉) and $10^5$ yr (10 M☉) benchmarks, 5 M☉ crosses on a timescale of roughly $10^6$ yr, sparse enough that clusters near this turnoff mass (e.g. NGC 1866, $M_\text{TO}\sim4$–$5\,M_\odot$) show a visibly empty Hertzsprung gap.

3A Star Turned Inside Out

As the envelope expands at roughly constant luminosity (Stefan–Boltzmann forcing $T_\text{eff}$ down as $R$ grows), the expanding gas absorbs shell energy as mechanical work — a heat sink that keeps $L_r$ declining outward from the shell. The course's own structural note for this exact model is striking: the outermost 50% of the stellar radius contains only 17% of the total stellar mass — a dense compact core wrapped in a vastly extended, diffuse envelope.

4RGB Ascent and Quiet Ignition at Point E

With the He-core safely on the ideal-gas side of the degeneracy boundary, the RGB is short and helium ignites quietly at $T\sim10^8$ K, $\rho\sim10^4\,\text{g cm}^{-3}$ — no flash, no luminosity dip. This is Point E in the course's own notation for this exact model, the moment the star leaves the RGB and enters the He-burning region.

5Segment E–G–K–I — the Course's Own Worked Example

The notes trace this precise sequence for the 5 M☉ model: from Point E, the H-shell's luminosity first decreases (core expansion pushes the shell to cooler layers) then increases again; Point G marks the H-shell's maximum efficiency; from G to Point I, $L_H$ falls as $L_\text{He}$ climbs; and at Point K, helium burning supplies roughly 60% of the star's total luminosity. Geometrically this is the blue loop — Segment A blueward while the core expands, Segment B redward once the core itself begins contracting — carried far enough hot in this model to clear the Cepheid instability strip entirely.

6Core He-Burning: the CO Core Recipe

As in every star above $M_\text{HeF}$, $3\alpha\to{}^{12}\text{C}$ and $^{12}\text{C}+\alpha\to{}^{16}\text{O}$ run together, leaving an oxygen-rich CO core once helium is exhausted — the exact C/O split still limited by uncertainty in the $^{12}\text{C}+\alpha$ reaction rate.

7The AGB — Double-Shell Fire and Fast Thermal Pulses

The AGB interior stacks, from the center out: inert CO core, He-burning shell, He-rich intershell, H-burning shell, H-He envelope — a genuine double-shell furnace, with the two shells alternately reigniting as thermal pulses. Crucially, 5 M☉ clears the Second-Dredge-Up threshold ($M>4$–$5\,M_\odot$), so CN-processed material reaches the surface here in a way the lower-mass 3 M☉ track barely manages. The notes give the inter-pulse period explicitly for this core mass: $t_\text{TP}\approx10^3$ yr for a 5 M☉ CO core — two orders of magnitude faster than the $2\times10^5$ yr spacing typical of a $0.6\,M_\odot$ core.

8Third Dredge-Up and the s-Process

Each thermal pulse mixes fresh C and O into the intershell; when the deepening convective envelope later reaches that enriched layer, the Third Dredge-Up carries it to the surface, progressively raising C/O and — after enough episodes — potentially crossing into carbon-star territory, enriched further by slow neutron-capture s-process nucleosynthesis.

9The Ember — Carbon-Oxygen White Dwarf

Still below $M_\text{up}\approx8\,M_\odot$, the CO core degenerates after He-burning rather than igniting carbon. A superwind strips the envelope into a planetary nebula, leaving a degenerate carbon-oxygen white dwarf — more massive than the Sun's ~0.6 M☉ remnant, reflecting the larger core this model built up, but cooling along the same slope-4 track set by $L\propto R^2T^4$ at fixed radius.

1Prelude — Near the Top of the Intermediate-Mass Range

At 9 M☉, CNO burning drives a large convective core at the hot, luminous end of the mass–luminosity relation $L\approx M^{3.6}$. Core densities are already low here (the notes' ZAMS table shows $\rho_c$ falling from $\sim400\,\text{g cm}^{-3}$ at 0.1 M☉ to just $\sim13.5\,\text{g cm}^{-3}$ at 7 M☉) — a structural fact that will matter later, since low density makes electron degeneracy harder to trigger.

$$t_{MS} \cong 10^{10}\,M^{-3}\ \text{yr}$$
The course table's exact value is $2.2\times10^7$ yr (the $M^{-3}$ scaling law gives $1.4\times10^7$ yr), over 400× shorter than the Sun's.

2TAMS to the Hertzsprung Gap

The CNO "hook" at Point 2 gives way to a very fast Segment 4–5 crossing — approaching the notes' $10^5$ yr benchmark quoted for 10 M☉ — leaving almost no stars observable in this narrow transitional phase for a cluster at this turnoff mass.

3Quiet Ignition and the Widest Loop of the Three

Well above $M_\text{HeF}=2.2\,M_\odot$, the He-core stays non-degenerate and ignites smoothly at moderate luminosity, with no flash and no dip in brightness. The subsequent blue loop is the widest of the tracks on this page — Segment A's core-driven envelope contraction pushes the surface to its hottest point yet, before Segment B's core contraction sends the envelope back out toward the Hayashi track.

4Core He-Burning and the O-Ne-Mg Seed

$3\alpha\to{}^{12}\text{C}$ and $^{12}\text{C}+\alpha\to{}^{16}\text{O}$ leave an oxygen-rich CO core once helium is spent — the same recipe as every star above $M_\text{HeF}$, but here the core is massive enough that its subsequent fate is genuinely in question.

5The Mup Boundary — a Fuzzy Zone

The course notes are explicit: below $M_\text{up}\approx8\,M_\odot$ the CO core becomes electron-degenerate and carbon never ignites, guaranteeing a CO white dwarf; above it the core stays non-degenerate and carbon ignites stably, driving the star toward a Type II supernova. But "stars with 7–11 M☉ occupy a fuzzy transition zone where the outcome (O-Mg WD, electron-capture SN, or Type II SN) depends sensitively on the exact mass and uncertain physics." At 9 M☉, this star sits squarely inside that zone.

6The Carbon Flash and the Super-AGB Phase

Carbon ignites around $T\sim5$–$6\times10^8$ K in a weakly degenerate core — a flash directly analogous to the helium flash, igniting off-center (neutrino cooling preferentially chills the true center) before a convective flame propagates inward and erases the degeneracy. What follows is the Super-AGB phase: a red-supergiant envelope around an O-Ne-Mg core, with H- and He-burning shells and carbon-burning shell layered around it, running the Second- and Third-Dredge-Up analogs (including "dredge-out" events) just as a classical AGB star does around a CO core.

7Two Possible Endings — the Point of This Page

Everything now hinges on a race between the carbon-burning shell (growing the O-Ne-Mg core toward the Chandrasekhar mass, $M_\text{Ch}\approx1.4\,M_\odot$) and stellar winds stripping the envelope away. If winds win, the envelope is lost before the core reaches $M_\text{Ch}$, leaving an O-Ne white dwarf of $\sim1.0$–$1.2\,M_\odot$. If the envelope survives long enough, the core reaches $M_\text{Ch}$ and a qualitatively different collapse begins: electron capture onto $^{24}\text{Mg}$ and $^{20}\text{Ne}$ removes pressure support in a runaway with no negative feedback, oxygen ignites in a deflagration near $\rho\sim2.5\times10^{10}\,\text{g cm}^{-3}$, and the core collapses to a neutron star — an electron-capture supernova (EC-SN), thought to be weak and Ni-poor compared to a classical core-collapse event.

Honest answerThe course notes describe this boundary as sensitive to "uncertain physics" — chiefly mass-loss prescriptions the models cannot yet pin down. This page cannot tell you which fate 9 M☉ meets; it can only show you why nobody else can either, without a great deal more work on stellar-wind physics in this exact mass range.

8If It's a White Dwarf

An O-Ne WD differs from the CO WDs of lower-mass tracks: typical composition is $^{16}\text{O}\ 0.50$–$0.70$, $^{20}\text{Ne}\ 0.15$–$0.35$, with trace $^{23}\text{Na}$ and $^{22}\text{Ne}$. Because the mean atomic mass is higher than in a CO WD, the ionic heat capacity per unit mass is lower, so an O-Ne WD of similar mass actually cools faster than its carbon-oxygen cousin, despite starting from a more energetic collapse.

9If It's a Supernova

An EC-SN skips the long staircase of advanced burning stages that a truly massive star runs through — there is no need to reach iron, because electron capture itself triggers the collapse once $M_\text{Ch}$ is approached. The result is thought to be among the lowest-energy class of core-collapse explosions, possibly corresponding to a subset of low-nickel Type IIP supernovae, and it removes this star from the HR diagram entirely in favor of a neutron star remnant.

The main sequence still dominates by orders of magnitude, but the post-He-burning stages compress dramatically compared to lower-mass tracks — carbon burning and its aftermath run on timescales far shorter than anything on the 3 or 5 M☉ pages. 1Arrival — A Convective, CNO-Burning Core

At 15 M☉ the core is comfortably above the $1.2\,M_\odot$ CNO-transition mass, so hydrogen burns almost entirely via the CNO cycle at $T_c\approx3.4\times10^7$ K. The cycle's ferocious temperature sensitivity ($\epsilon_{CNO}\propto T_6^{15}$) concentrates burning into the innermost ~10% of the star, driving $\nabla_{rad}>\nabla_{ad}$ there and producing a convective core inside a radiative envelope — Point 1, the ZAMS.

2The Long Burn, Compressed — Main Sequence

The mass-luminosity relation is shallower on the upper main sequence than for the Sun, because the CNO cycle caps how much the core temperature needs to rise with mass.

$$L \approx M^{3.6}\ \Rightarrow\ 15^{3.6}\,L_\odot \approx 17{,}150\,L_\odot$$
Course mass-luminosity law for $M\gtrsim1.2\,M_\odot$; combined with $R\propto\sqrt{L}/T_{eff}^2$ this gives $R\approx5.6\,R_\odot$ at $T_{eff}\approx28{,}000$ K.

The course's own MS-lifetime table lists $t_{MS}=10^7$ yr for $15\,M_\odot$ exactly — a thousand times shorter than the Sun's, because fuel scales only with $M$ while luminosity scales with a much higher power of $M$.

3Point 2 — No Isothermal Detour

At $M>6\,M_\odot$ the convective core is already so large a fraction of the star's mass that, at H-exhaustion, contraction begins immediately — there is no stable isothermal helium core and no Schönberg-Chandrasekhar limit to wait for. The core's own $(\log\rho_c,\log T_c)$ trajectory stays entirely in ideal-gas territory, so helium ignites directly, without a helium flash and without the long Red-Giant-Branch ascent that defines lower-mass tracks.

4The Red Supergiant Disguise

Once hydrogen and helium shells take over, the envelope swells onto the Hayashi track and the star spends essentially all of its remaining, observable life as a cool, luminous red supergiant. This is where Point 3 in Fig. 1 sits — parked, not moving — because everything that happens afterward inside the star is too fast to leave any mark on the surface.

A star seen today as a red supergiant is therefore almost certainly still in ordinary H- or He-shell burning: the advanced burning stages that follow are so brief that catching one in the act, from the outside, is essentially a matter of luck.

5Building the Onion, Invisibly

Carbon ignites at $T\sim8\times10^8$ K in a fully non-degenerate core and burns in $\sim10^3$ yr; neon photodisintegrates at $T\sim1.6\times10^9$ K in a few years; oxygen burns at $T\sim2\times10^9$ K in $\sim200$ days; silicon burns at $T\sim3$–$4\times10^9$ K in about a week. Each stage is faster than the last because thermal-neutrino losses ($\propto T^9$-ish) drain energy straight out of the star, forcing the core to contract and reheat between stages rather than settle into a long-lived burning phase.

The end result is the onion-shell structure: H shell, He shell, C shell, O shell, a vestigial Ne layer, a Si-burning shell, and an inert iron core of roughly $1.3$–$2.0\,M_\odot$ at the centre. All of this — carbon through silicon — is built in a span of centuries to a few thousand years, while the star's outward appearance as a red supergiant barely changes.

6Why the Iron Core Collapses

Iron sits at the peak of the nuclear binding-energy curve: fusing it into anything heavier costs energy rather than releasing it, so once the core is iron there is no nuclear fuel left to burn. Electron captures on iron-group nuclei and the photo-disintegration of $^{56}$Fe back into helium and free neutrons both remove pressure support and absorb energy, and once the effective adiabatic index drops below $4/3$ the core is unstable and collapses.

$$^{56}\text{Fe} + \gamma \rightarrow 13\,^4\text{He} + 4n$$
Endothermic photo-disintegration — the nuclear equivalent of millions of years of fusion being undone in an instant, absorbing energy and accelerating the collapse.

7Collapse and Explosion — Tens of Milliseconds

Once collapse begins, the core falls from a radius of $\sim1000$ km to nuclear density in about $10$ ms — the fastest known stellar evolutionary transition. The inner core rebounds at nuclear density, launching a shock that stalls after photo-disintegrating the infalling iron; it is only revived by neutrinos diffusing out of the newly formed proto-neutron star and depositing energy just behind the shock, producing the delayed, neutrino-driven explosion recorded observationally as a Type II supernova.

1Arrival — The Hottest Track in This Atlas

At 25 M☉ the CNO cycle dominates hydrogen burning even more completely than at 15 M☉, and the same physics applies: concentrated energy generation in the innermost ~10% of the star drives $\nabla_{rad}>\nabla_{ad}$ there, producing a convective core under a radiative envelope. This is the hottest, most luminous ZAMS point among all nine tracks in this atlas.

2The Shortest Burn — Main Sequence

$$L \approx M^{3.6}\ \Rightarrow\ 25^{3.6}\,L_\odot \approx 108{,}000\,L_\odot$$

3Point 2 — Straight Into Contraction

As at 15 M☉, the core at $M>6\,M_\odot$ is already too large a fraction of the star's mass to sustain a stable isothermal helium core — contraction and helium ignition follow H-exhaustion directly, with no Schönberg-Chandrasekhar limit, no helium flash, and no extended Red Giant Branch.

4Red Supergiant, Then Possibly Wolf-Rayet

As shell burning takes over, the envelope again swells onto the Hayashi track and the star spends nearly all of its short remaining life as a red supergiant — parked at Point 3 in Fig. 1, its surface giving no hint of the nuclear staircase running underneath. At this mass, however, strong radiation-driven winds can be powerful enough to strip much of the hydrogen envelope before collapse, exposing the hot helium- or carbon-rich core beneath as a Wolf-Rayet star (Point 4) — the blueward hook back toward higher $T_{eff}$.

This stripping is exactly the mechanism behind Type Ib/Ic supernovae: some Ib/Ic progenitors have been directly identified as Wolf-Rayet stars that had already lost their envelopes by the time of collapse, in contrast to the hydrogen-rich Type II progenitors that retain theirs.

5The Onion-Shell Structure

Whatever the envelope's fate, the interior proceeds through the same catastrophic staircase as every star above $\sim11\,M_\odot$: carbon, neon, oxygen, and silicon burn in succession, each stage faster than the last as neutrino losses drain energy and force renewed gravitational contraction between stages. The canonical Kippenhahn diagram of this final structure — H, He, C, O, a vestigial Ne layer, and a Si-burning shell around an inert iron core — is drawn for a $25\,M_\odot$ star precisely because this mass shows the onion-shell layering with such clarity.

As before, all of this happens in centuries to a few thousand years, invisible at the surface; whatever the star looks like externally at this point — red supergiant or stripped Wolf-Rayet — that appearance is essentially frozen while the interior races toward collapse.

6Why the Iron Core Collapses

Iron sits at the peak of the binding-energy curve, so the core has no further nuclear energy to extract once silicon burning finishes. Electron capture and photo-disintegration of iron-group nuclei both strip pressure support and absorb energy, driving the effective adiabatic index below $4/3$ and triggering collapse on the free-fall timescale — tens of milliseconds, the fastest known stellar evolutionary transition.

7The Remnant — Black Hole, Not Neutron Star

This is the mass boundary that separates this track from the 15 M☉ one: for $M>11\,M_\odot$ the iron core exceeds the Chandrasekhar mass and collapses; if $1125\,M_\odot$ the remnant exceeds the maximum neutron-star mass and becomes a black hole. At exactly this progenitor mass the collapsing core produces a remnant at or above the Oppenheimer–Volkoff limit of roughly $2.5$–$3\,M_\odot$ — the point at which neutron degeneracy pressure can no longer hold the object up, and no stable configuration exists.

Two routes lead here: a prompt collapse, in which the stalled shock is never revived and the star simply vanishes with no visible supernova, or a fallback collapse, in which an initially successful explosion still leaves behind several solar masses of unescaped ejecta that rain back onto the proto-neutron star and push it over the limit. For stars above $\sim30\,M_\odot$, fallback of several solar masses is routine, and the same physics applies at 25 M☉ whenever the density gradient at the core boundary is too shallow for the shock to escape cleanly.

◆How Long Does Each Phase Last?

Even shorter-lived than the 15 M☉ track, this star's main sequence is measured in hundreds of thousands of years rather than millions, and its onion-shell finale is compressed into the same few thousand years — followed by the same near-instantaneous collapse, this time with no supernova guaranteed to be visible at all.

This is the most extreme stellar fate covered in this atlas: a star bright enough to outshine 100,000 Suns, living barely half a million years, possibly stripping its own envelope before it dies, and collapsing into an object so compact that not even neutron degeneracy pressure can hold it up. Whether the sky sees a supernova at all when this happens is not guaranteed.

◆A Track That Never Reaches the Main Sequence

There is no evolutionary track to draw here in the usual sense — only a contraction that runs out of time. The faint dashed line marks where the Zero-Age Main Sequence would continue if it could; the solid maroon path shows what an object below 0.08 M☉ does instead.

1Prelude — The Same Start as Every Star

A brown dwarf begins its life exactly like any protostar: a collapsing cloud settles onto the fully-convective Hayashi track and contracts on the Kelvin–Helmholtz timescale, converting gravitational energy into heat under the Virial Theorem's "negative heat capacity" — radiating energy away from the surface makes the interior hotter, not cooler. Up to this point, nothing in the physics distinguishes it from the star it might have become.

$$\tau_{KH}\sim\frac{GM^2}{RL}$$
Kelvin–Helmholtz timescale — how long contraction alone can power the luminosity while the core heats toward the ignition threshold.

2The Missing Ignition

Sustained hydrogen burning via the pp chain requires a core temperature near $10^7$ K, and reaching it requires enough mass to keep compressing the core as contraction proceeds. Below $M_{crit}\approx0.08\,M_\odot$, that temperature is never attained. The object is not a star in the astrophysical sense — the course notes call it precisely what it is: a "failed star," one that "simply radiates away their gravitational potential energy and cools down over time."

$$M_{crit}\approx 0.08\,M_\odot$$
The hydrogen-burning minimum mass. Below it, no self-regulating thermonuclear furnace ever switches on.

3Degeneracy Wins the Race

Contraction is a race between rising core temperature and rising core density. In a low-enough-mass object, the core becomes electron-degenerate before it becomes hot enough — degenerate pressure is set by density alone, not temperature, so it can halt further compression while $T_c$ is still short of ignition. Once that pressure takes over, the core stops heating, and the one process that could have started a main sequence never gets the chance.

$$P\propto\rho^{5/3}$$
Non-relativistic degenerate electron pressure — the same physics that later halts collapse in white dwarfs, arriving here decades before any fusion could begin.

4A Brief Consolation Fire

Some brown dwarfs briefly burn a trace deuterium reservoir, which ignites at a lower temperature than ordinary hydrogen fusion requires. The course notes do not detail this reaction specifically, so it is worth flagging honestly rather than overstating it: it is a minor, short-lived contribution that changes nothing about the object's ultimate fate. A spark, not a furnace.

5The Cooling Forever

With no sustained energy source, the object simply radiates away whatever heat contraction left behind. There is no dramatic "birth of the ember" moment the way there is for a true white dwarf — in a real sense, a brown dwarf is already most of the way to degenerate before it even finishes contracting. It has nowhere left to go but fainter and cooler, for longer than the present age of the universe.

Somewhere between a planet and a star, these objects are the quiet edge case of the whole atlas: everything that happens to them is pre-main-sequence physics, and nothing that happens to them is stellar physics.

◆A Point, Not a Path

There is no track to draw for this category — only a position. The faint dashed line is the Zero-Age Main Sequence across all stellar masses; the highlighted orange segment is the slice of it occupied by red dwarfs, and every star ever born in this range is still sitting exactly there.

1One Structure for the Whole Category

Red dwarfs are fully convective from centre to surface — not just in the core, the way the Sun's envelope is, but everywhere. Opacity stays high throughout the cool interior, keeping the radiative gradient above the adiabatic one at every layer, so the convective instability criterion is satisfied all the way down. Their pre-main-sequence Hayashi track sits almost exactly on top of their eventual ZAMS position: because the structure barely changes upon hydrogen ignition, these stars essentially arrive on the main sequence already looking like main-sequence stars.

$$L \propto M^{2.6}\ \text{(lowest masses)}\ \ \text{to}\ \ M^{4}\text{–}M^{4.5}\ \text{(near 0.5 } M_\odot\text{, pp-chain regime)}$$
The mass–luminosity relation steepens as mass rises through this category, toward the CNO transition at higher mass.

2One Fuel, No Alternative

Every star in this range is far below the ~1.2 M☉ threshold where the CNO cycle takes over, so the pp chain is the only hydrogen-burning process ever available to it. The pp chain's mild temperature sensitivity spreads energy production diffusely rather than concentrating it, which is exactly why these stars never develop the convective core that CNO burning would otherwise force — and exactly why they stay fully convective throughout their entire (extraordinarily long) main-sequence lives.

3A Lifetime Longer Than the Universe

The same $t_{MS}\propto M^{-3}$ scaling that gives the Sun ~10 Gyr gives red dwarfs numbers that dwarf the age of the universe. The course notes make the case directly for $0.8\,M_\odot$: its main-sequence lifetime is comparable to the Hubble time ($\sim13.8$ Gyr), which is why every star at or below that mass formed near the Big Bang is still burning hydrogen today. Push the same scaling down through this category and the numbers only grow more extreme.

$$t_{MS}\approx 10^{10}\,M^{-3}\ \text{yr}$$
At $0.5\,M_\odot$: roughly 80 Gyr. At $0.08\,M_\odot$, the hydrogen-burning limit: on the order of $10^{13}$ years — a lifetime measured in trillions of years, using the same relation that fixes the Sun's ten billion.

4A Category Frozen in Time

The central fact of this pageEvery red dwarf that has ever formed, at any epoch since the Big Bang, is still today exactly where it began: on the main sequence, burning hydrogen via the pp chain, inside a fully convective envelope. None has ever become a subgiant. None has ever climbed a red giant branch. None has ever produced a white dwarf. There simply has not been enough time in the entire history of the universe.

That makes this whole mass range unique among the six categories in this atlas: it is the only one with no post-main-sequence chapter to tell, not because the physics forbids it, but because the clock hasn't run out yet — and won't, for a very long time.

Every red dwarf visible in the night sky tonight is doing exactly what it was doing when it ignited, and will keep doing so long after every Sun-like and massive star in this atlas has finished its own story.

◆Three Starting Points, One Shape

Three representative tracks — 0.5, 1, and 2.25 M☉ — drawn to the same qualitative shape established for the Sun: ZAMS, sub-giant branch, Red Giant Branch, helium flash at the tip, Horizontal Branch, Asymptotic Giant Branch, and white dwarf cooling. Higher mass means brighter throughout, so the tracks are offset vertically for legibility here — but they all converge at the end, because the white dwarf core mass left behind is nearly the same regardless of where the star started.

2Different Starts, Same Climb

Main-sequence luminosity and lifetime vary enormously across this range — a 2 M☉ star burns far brighter and finishes far sooner than a 0.5 M☉ star. But once each leaves the main sequence, expands across the sub-giant branch, and begins climbing the Red Giant Branch on a hydrogen-burning shell around a growing helium core, they are all headed toward the same terminal luminosity: the RGB tip.

$$\log(L/L_\odot)\approx 3.45$$
The RGB-tip luminosity, essentially constant across this whole mass range — the foundation of the Tip-of-the-RGB (TRGB) standard candle.

3The Same Flash, Wherever You Start

The degenerate core's pressure doesn't depend on temperature, so when helium ignites at the RGB tip it runs away rather than self-regulating, briefly reaching an internal luminosity around $10^{11}\,L_\odot$ — all of it absorbed lifting degeneracy, none of it escaping. Because the ignition conditions are set by the same core mass every time, this flash looks physically identical for a 0.6 M☉ star and a 1.8 M☉ star alike.

$$P \propto \rho^{5/3}$$
Degenerate electron pressure is temperature-independent — the reason the flash runs away rather than stabilising, for any star in this category.

4Diverging Again — Horizontal Branch Color

After the flash, the identical cores redistribute onto different points of the Horizontal Branch, because the envelope mass sitting on top of that fixed core still differs star to star — set by initial mass and by however much was lost climbing the RGB. A thinner envelope means a hotter, bluer HB position; a thicker one means cooler and redder.

$$q = M_c/M_E$$
Higher $q$ (thinner envelope) → hotter, bluer HB position. Lower $q$ (thicker envelope) → cooler, redder HB position — the mechanism linking mass-loss history to HB color at fixed core mass.

5Double Fire Again — The AGB

Every star in this range eventually ignites a helium shell around its inert carbon-oxygen core while an old hydrogen shell fades above it, the two alternating in thermal pulses that dredge freshly made carbon, oxygen, and s-process elements to the surface. Dust-driven superwinds ($\dot M\sim10^{-4}\,M_\odot\,\text{yr}^{-1}$) then strip the envelope down to almost nothing — the same ending, just reached sooner for the more massive stars in the range.

6The Same Ending — Carbon-Oxygen White Dwarf

What's left in every case is a naked, degenerate carbon-oxygen core of roughly 0.5–0.6 M☉, held up by temperature-independent electron degeneracy pressure regardless of whether the progenitor started at 0.5 M☉ or 2 M☉. With the remnant mass this similar, the cooling tracks themselves converge onto essentially the same slope-4 line in the HR diagram.

$$L_{WD}(t) = L_0\left[1+\frac{5}{2}\frac{t}{\tau_0}\right]^{-7/5}$$
Mestel cooling law: fast fading while young, then progressively slower — the shared final chapter for this entire mass category.

Timing shifts with mass — a 2 M☉ star races through the main sequence in roughly a billion years where a 0.5 M☉ star takes tens of billions — but the relative shape of the story is shared. The bar below illustrates that shared shape, not any single mass's exact durations.

◆A Family of Tracks, One Shared Trick

Three representative masses spanning the category (2, 5, and 8 M☉) trace the same qualitative shape: a rise up the giant branch, a quiet ignition of helium with no flash, a blue loop that carries the star back and forth across the Cepheid instability strip, a second giant-branch ascent on the AGB, and finally a white dwarf cooling track. Higher mass tracks sit higher and bluer throughout.

1What Defines This Category

Between 2 and 8 M☉ a star is too massive to develop the electron-degenerate helium core that triggers a flash, yet too light to ignite carbon and march all the way to an iron core. Every star in this band shares one defining trait: helium ignites quietly. That single difference in core physics — flash or no flash — is the real dividing line between this category and the sun-like stars just below it, more than any difference in outward appearance.

2Quiet Helium Ignition — No Flash

Above roughly 2.2 M☉, the pre-ignition helium core never becomes electron-degenerate, so the 3α reaction switches on as a stable, thermoregulated burn at $T\sim10^8$ K and $\rho\sim10^4\,\text{g cm}^{-3}$ — no runaway, no invisible internal flash. Even so, ignition still ends the RGB ascent and drags the star onto the core-helium-burning region of the HRD, following (for a representative 5 M☉ model) a path in which the hydrogen shell's luminosity first dips as the expanding core pushes it to cooler layers, then climbs again as helium burning strengthens.

$$T_\text{ignite}\sim10^8\ \text{K}, \quad \rho_\text{ignite}\sim10^4\ \text{g cm}^{-3}$$
Conditions for stable, non-degenerate helium ignition — no thermostat failure, unlike the flash below 2.2 M☉.

3Building the Carbon-Oxygen Core

Helium burning proceeds via $3\alpha\to{}^{12}\text{C}$ followed by $^{12}\text{C}+\alpha\to{}^{16}\text{O}$; because the triple-alpha rate scales as $\rho^2Y^3$ while carbon capture scales only as $\rho Y$, carbon production dominates early (while helium is abundant) and oxygen production overtakes it as helium depletes. The result, common to the whole category, is a CO core in which oxygen slightly outmasses carbon by the end of core helium burning — though the exact ratio remains uncertain due to the poorly known $^{12}\text{C}+\alpha$ cross-section.

4The Blue Loop — A Tug-of-War in the Core

Once helium burning begins, the star traces a characteristic loop in the HRD driven by a two-stage competition. First, convection from the 3α process expands the helium core, pushing the hydrogen shell outward into cooler layers and weakening it; starved from below, the envelope contracts and the surface heats up — the star swings blueward. Later, as helium depletes and the core itself begins to contract, the rising 3α rate reverses the balance: the envelope re-expands and the surface cools — the star swings back redward, eventually rejoining the giant branch as the AGB.

5Blue Loops Meet the Cepheid Instability Strip

The instability strip is a near-vertical band in the HRD, roughly 6300–7100 K wide, where the opacity-driven κ-mechanism can sustain radial pulsation. Because the blue loop of a helium-burning intermediate-mass star crosses straight through this strip — and does so slowly, since the star spends a large fraction of its core-helium-burning lifetime near the loop's turning point — this category supplies the bulk of the classical Cepheids: Population I, He-burning supergiants whose period–luminosity relation anchors the first rung of the cosmic distance ladder.

6A Second Giant Branch — the AGB

When core helium is exhausted, the inert CO core contracts and the envelope re-expands toward the Hayashi track a second time: the Asymptotic Giant Branch. Two shells — helium below, hydrogen above — alternately reignite in thermal pulses, dredging carbon, oxygen, and s-process material to the surface, until dust-driven superwinds strip the envelope away almost entirely.

7The Upper Boundary — Mup ≈ 8 M☉

Whether the AGB is the end of the story depends on whether the contracting CO core becomes electron-degenerate. Below $M^\text{up}=8\,M_\odot$ it does, degeneracy halts further nuclear burning, and the star is confined to this category's fate; above it, carbon ignites stably and the star escapes into the massive-star channel entirely. As the course notes put it: "stars with 7–11 M☉ occupy a fuzzy transition zone where the outcome... depends sensitively on exact mass and uncertain physics" — so the top of this category blurs into genuine astrophysical uncertainty rather than a sharp line.

8Endpoint — CO or ONe White Dwarf

Every star that stays below Mup ends the same way the sun-like stars do: a planetary nebula exposes a degenerate core, held up by electron degeneracy pressure alone, which then cools along the Mestel track for billions of years. Near the very top of the range the ejected core can be carbon-oxygen or, closer to 8 M☉, an oxygen-neon-magnesium mixture from more advanced burning — but in both cases the outcome is a white dwarf, never a supernova.

◆How Long Does Each Phase Last?

As in every mass range, the main sequence dominates — though it is far shorter here (tens to hundreds of millions of years, not billions) because luminosity rises steeply with mass. This category is the quiet middle child of stellar evolution: too massive to flash, too light to explode. Its blue loops light up the sky as Cepheids, and its final act — a slow crystallizing white dwarf — is identical in kind to the Sun's, just built from a slightly larger and sometimes differently seasoned core.

◆A Brief Blue Life, a Long Red One, Then Silence

Three representative masses (9, 15, and ~22 M☉) all trace the same shape: a short, hot main sequence, a fast crossing to the red supergiant branch where the star spends nearly all of its remaining visible life, and then core collapse exactly where it sits — the advanced burning stages that precede collapse are too fast to move the star anywhere in this diagram.

1A Label With a Caveat

This category is conventionally bounded at 8–25 M☉, marking it off from the intermediate stars below (which stop at white dwarfs) and the very massive stars above (which form black holes). But the course notes' own core-collapse-to-neutron-star threshold is sharper and higher: "For stars with M > 11 M☉... if the initial mass is 11 M☉ < M < 25 M☉, the collapse produces a neutron star." The gap between the category's nominal 8 M☉ floor and the notes' actual 11 M☉ threshold is not a rounding error — it is real, unresolved physics, covered in §6.

2Building the Onion — Successive Nuclear Burning

Above the 11 M☉ threshold, the core is never degenerate enough to stop nuclear burning, so fusion proceeds all the way up the periodic table: H → He → C → Ne → O → Si → Fe, each stage igniting at higher temperature and burning dramatically faster than the last as neutrino losses (scaling roughly as $T^9$) drain energy directly out of the star rather than into supporting it. By the time silicon burns to iron, the interior has organized into a classic onion-shell structure, layers of ash nested by nuclear weight around an inert iron core of roughly 1.3–2.0 M☉.

$$t_{H} \sim 10^7\,\text{yr} \;\longrightarrow\; t_{Si} \sim 1\,\text{week}$$
The acceleration from hydrogen to silicon burning is a factor of roughly $10^9$ — driven mainly by neutrino losses, not by faster nuclear rates.

3Why Iron Cannot Be Saved

Iron sits at the peak of the nuclear binding-energy curve, so fusing it into anything heavier costs energy rather than releasing it — the core has no further fuel to draw on. Electron captures onto iron-group nuclei strip away the degeneracy pressure that had been holding the core up, while photodisintegration of iron ($^{56}\text{Fe}+\gamma\to13\,{}^4\text{He}+4n$) actively absorbs thermal energy at $T\sim5$–$10\times10^9$ K, and the URCA process removes still more electrons and energy through neutrino emission. Together these three effects drop the core's effective adiabatic index below the critical stability value of $4/3$, and collapse becomes unavoidable.

4Tens of Milliseconds to Nuclear Density

Once collapse begins it proceeds in near free-fall, and the core — roughly 1,000 km across — reaches nuclear density in a time of order tens of milliseconds: the fastest known stellar transition, an interior restructured irreversibly in less time than a human blink. When the inner core reaches nuclear density the strong force turns repulsive, the infall bounces, and the rebound launches an outward shock that stalls within the still-infalling outer layers — reviving that stalled shock via delayed neutrino heating is what actually turns collapse into a visible explosion.

$$\tau_d \approx 10^{-2}\ \text{s} = 10\ \text{ms}\quad\text{at}\;\rho\sim10^{10}\ \text{g cm}^{-3}$$
Free-fall collapse timescale once the adiabatic index drops below 4/3.

5The Neutron Star Left Behind

What survives is a sphere of degenerate neutrons roughly 10 km across, supported by neutron degeneracy pressure in direct analogy to the electron-degenerate white dwarf, but at densities up to $10^{15}\,\text{g cm}^{-3}$. Because nearly every neutron star forms from an iron core that was itself supported by degenerate electron pressure near the Chandrasekhar mass, observed neutron star masses cluster tightly around $\sim1.4\,M_\odot$ — this is not a coincidence but a direct inheritance from the collapse physics.

6The Fuzzy Zone — 7 to 11 M☉

The category's nominal 8 M☉ floor sits squarely inside a region the notes describe plainly: "stars with 7–11 M☉ occupy a fuzzy transition zone where the outcome... depends sensitively on exact mass and uncertain physics." In this band the core ignites carbon only mildly, in a degenerate ONeMg mixture, and three distinct fates are all physically plausible depending on exactly where a star sits and on uncertain mass-loss and convection prescriptions: the core cools into an O-Ne white dwarf, it grows past $M_{Ch}$ and undergoes an electron-capture supernova, or — nearer 11 M☉ — it behaves as a genuine Type II core-collapse supernova and produces a neutron star outright. This is not a modeling failure to be papered over; it is where stellar evolution theory is honestly uncertain.

Cross-reference — the compactness caveatEven inside the well-established 11–25 M☉ neutron-star band, the picture is not perfectly uniform: models using the compactness parameter $\xi_{2.5}$ show a mixed regime above roughly 18 M☉ where some progenitors still explode successfully (→ NS) while others collapse directly to a black hole with no visible supernova at all. The clean "11–25 → NS" rule is a good first-order threshold, not an exact one — see the Very Massive category for the full picture.

◆How Long Does Each Phase Last?

The main sequence again dominates, now compressed to a few million years. Everything after core hydrogen exhaustion — including every advanced burning stage from carbon to silicon — occupies less than a few thousand years combined, and collapse itself is over in tens of milliseconds. There is no flash, no AGB, no planetary nebula, and no white dwarf in this category's story.

This category is the classic core-collapse supernova engine of the textbooks — but its edges are genuinely blurred. The bottom overlaps a fuzzy zone that might make a white dwarf instead, and the top overlaps a mixed regime that might make a black hole instead. Only the broad middle, roughly 11–18 M☉, is unambiguously a neutron-star factory.

◆Stripped Bare Before It Falls

Three representative masses (30, 60, and 100 M☉) begin extremely hot and luminous, attempt a redward turn toward the red-supergiant branch, but never fully get there: ferocious radiation-driven winds strip the envelope down to the bare helium/carbon core, hooking the track back blueward as a Wolf-Rayet star before collapse. This blueward hook — absent from the neutron-star category's diagram — is the visible fingerprint of wind-stripping on the HRD.

1Beyond the Neutron Star's Limit

As the course notes state directly: "When the remnant left behind by a core-collapse supernova has a mass exceeding the Oppenheimer-Volkoff (OV) limit of approximately 2.5–3 M☉, no stable compact object configuration is possible... The remnant therefore has no equilibrium state and undergoes total gravitational collapse to form a black hole." Unlike the sharply-derived Chandrasekhar mass, the OV limit is poorly constrained — it depends on the equation of state of matter beyond nuclear saturation density, physics that is not yet fully resolved.

2The ~25 M☉ Progenitor Threshold

The notes tie this remnant-mass limit directly to a progenitor mass: "This OV mass limit corresponds to a progenitor star with an initial main-sequence mass of roughly 25 M☉." Above this ZAMS mass, the iron core that eventually collapses is simply too large for any leftover degenerate-neutron configuration to support — there is no version of "wait for the pressure to catch up" available, the way there was for the milder collapses that leave neutron stars behind.

$$R_s = \frac{2GM}{c^2} = 3\left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right)\ \text{km}$$
The Schwarzschild radius — the event-horizon scale for the resulting black hole, whatever its final mass turns out to be.

3Stripped by Its Own Winds — the Wolf-Rayet Stage

Before collapse, radiation-driven stellar winds — powered by absorption on metal ions, chiefly iron — progressively strip the star's outer layers: first the hydrogen envelope, then in the most extreme cases the helium layer beneath it, exposing the hot, compact Wolf-Rayet core seen in the HRD hook above. This same stripping is what produces the observational split among core-collapse supernovae: a star that keeps its hydrogen envelope is seen as Type II, one stripped of hydrogen but not helium as Type Ib, and one stripped of both as Type Ic — with Ib/Ic progenitors preferentially identified as Wolf-Rayet stars.

4Sometimes No Explosion at All — Failed Supernovae

Whether the collapse produces a visible explosion at all depends on the compactness parameter $\xi_{2.5}$, the ratio of the innermost 2.5 M☉ to the radius enclosing it at the moment of iron-core infall: above a threshold of roughly $\xi_{2.5}\approx0.2$, the core is too centrally concentrated for the neutrino-driven shock to reverse the infall, and the star undergoes a "failed supernova" — direct collapse to a black hole with no optical transient, the entire stellar mass simply vanishing into the remnant. Models place the majority of stars above roughly 26 M☉ in this failed-SN regime, with a genuinely mixed population between about 18 and 26 M☉ where either outcome is possible.

$$\xi_M = \frac{M/M_\odot}{R(M)/1000\ \text{km}}, \qquad \xi_{2.5}\gtrsim0.2 \Rightarrow \text{failed SN}$$
Higher compactness means denser, more tightly bound core material — harder for the shock to unbind, and more likely to collapse straight to a black hole.

5How Big Can the Black Hole Get? — The Metallicity Ceiling

It is tempting to assume the most massive progenitors simply make the most massive black holes, but the course notes are precise on this point, and it is worth stating carefully: this is a statement about remnant mass, not progenitor mass, and it depends critically on metallicity. Radiation-driven winds scale strongly with metal content, so at solar metallicity even the most massive stars lose enormous amounts of mass before they ever collapse: "Solar metallicity (Z = 2×10⁻²): no remnant exceeds 25 M☉ for any initial mass up to 150 M☉, across all SN models considered. At solar metallicity, massive BHs cannot form from single-star evolution." Only at low metallicity — where weaker winds preserve much more of the initial mass and a larger CO core — do remnant black holes readily exceed 25 M☉, up to the tens-of-solar-mass black holes LIGO/Virgo has detected merging.

6Endpoint — A Black Hole, Not an Ember

Unlike every category below it, this one leaves no cooling ember: no white dwarf, no neutron star surface, nothing that radiates away a store of leftover heat over billions of years. What remains is defined purely by its event horizon — a region from which, per the Schwarzschild geometry, not even light escapes — and, in the LIGO/Virgo era, is chiefly known through the gravitational waves it emits when it eventually merges with another compact object.

◆How Long Does Each Phase Last?

The main sequence is shorter still than the neutron-star category's — a few million years at most — and the wind-driven Wolf-Rayet stripping phase that follows is itself a significant fraction of the star's short remaining life, not an instantaneous afterthought. Collapse, when it comes, is again a matter of milliseconds, but for a large share of these stars it arrives with no visible explosion whatsoever.

This is the most extreme stellar fate covered in this course: a star massive enough to out-fight its own supernova, direct-collapsing into a black hole that may never announce itself with light at all — only, decades or eons later, with a ripple in spacetime when it finally merges with another remnant of the same kind.

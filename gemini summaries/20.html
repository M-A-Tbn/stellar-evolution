Pre-Main Sequence Evolution: From Molecular Cloud to ZAMS

An exhaustive study companion covering slide lecture series "20.PreMS_evolution.pdf" verbatim, tracing a proto-star's journey from a diffuse Jeans-unstable cloud through free-fall collapse, the Hayashi track, and the perfect-gas thermostat, to its arrival on the Zero Age Main Sequence.

01

The H-R Diagram as Phase Space: Tracks vs. Isochrones

Stellar evolution is the study of the macroscopic variation of a star's observable properties — luminosity, effective temperature, radius — as a function of time. The word "macroscopic" matters: we are not tracking individual nuclear reactions in isolation, but the global, observable state of the star as it changes over its lifetime. The natural tool for this is the Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) Diagram, a plot of luminosity against effective temperature.

Exam Trap: The Reversed Axis

A crucial orientation convention: the x-axis ($\log T_e$) increases to the left, so hot stars sit on the left and cool stars on the right — the opposite of the natural reading direction. Every evolutionary track discussed below must be read with this reversal in mind, or the direction of motion will be completely misinterpreted.

The H-R diagram acts as a phase space for stellar structure: any set of stellar structure equations, solved for a given mass and composition, maps to exactly one point $(L, T_e)$. A stellar model takes as input the four fundamental parameters $$\text{INPUT: } [M,\, X,\, Y,\, Z]$$ where $M$ is the total stellar mass, $X$ the hydrogen mass fraction, $Y$ the helium mass fraction, and $Z$ the metal mass fraction ($X+Y+Z=1$). These feed into the structure equations (hydrostatic equilibrium, mass continuity, energy generation, radiative/convective transport), which output exactly one point: $$\text{OUTPUT: } [L,\, T_e]$$

When time is introduced, the flow chart gains a feedback loop: the composition $X,Y,Z$ changes as nuclear reactions burn fuel, and at each step the code must also ask two structural questions — has the core become degenerate? (the equation of state changes qualitatively), and is any region convectively unstable by the Schwarzschild criterion (energy transport switches from radiative to convective form)? The sequence of points $(L,T_e)$ generated this way, each tagged with a time, is an evolutionary track — the complete life history of one star of fixed mass.

Tracks vs. Isochrones — Do Not Confuse Them

An evolutionary track is the path of a single star (fixed mass) through the H-R diagram over time — every point is a different age of the same star. An isochrone is a curve connecting points of equal age across stars of different masses. If a population forms at $t=0$, the isochrone at $t=t_0$ is built by taking the evolutionary track of each mass, marking the point tagged with age $t_0$, and joining those points.

This is why isochrones and evolutionary tracks look so different when plotted together — one is a time-snapshot across many masses, the other is the full time-history of one mass. In a real star cluster, the observer always sees an isochrone, never a set of evolutionary tracks.

Lines of constant radius appear as diagonals in the H-R diagram, from the Stefan-Boltzmann law $L = 4\pi R^2\sigma T_e^4$: moving upper-right along a track means increasing radius, moving lower-left means contraction. These constant-$R$ diagonals are the standard reference grid for reading whether a star is expanding or contracting at any evolutionary stage.

02

Three Timescales of Stellar Evolution

A star's evolution is driven by its core's continual tendency toward gravitational contraction. As the core contracts, density and temperature rise. This process passes through three distinct regimes, each governed by a different timescale:

Regime Governing Balance Duration ($1\,M_\odot$)
Dynamical (free-fall) $\tau_d$ Gravity alone; gas pressure negligible minutes – $10^2$ yr
Thermodynamic (Kelvin-Helmholtz) $\tau_{KH}$ Virialized; pressure balances gravity, slow radiative contraction $10^7$ yr
Thermonuclear $\tau_\text{nuc}$ Fusion balances gravity; sets the main-sequence lifetime $10^{10}$ yr

The path of increasing timescale as a star forms is: $\text{Gas cloud} \rightarrow \text{Proto-star} \rightarrow \text{Star}$. The transitions are triggered by physical thresholds — gas pressure balancing gravity ends the dynamical phase, and fusion balancing gravity ends the thermodynamic phase.

Dynamical Timescale and Free-Fall

When a cloud exceeds the Jeans mass and pressure cannot resist gravity, the hydrostatic-equilibrium equation gains a radial acceleration term: $$\frac{\partial P}{\partial r} + \frac{Gm\rho}{r^2} = \rho\,\alpha$$ During free fall, $\partial P/\partial r$ is negligible, so mass shells accelerate under gravity alone. From $r=\tfrac12 g t^2$ with $g=GM/r^2$, one derives: $$T_d = \sqrt{\frac{2r^3}{GM}}$$ Expressed via the mean density $\bar\rho = 3M/(4\pi r^3)$ — more useful for gas clouds, where density is better known than $r$ and $M$ separately: $$T_d = \frac{2600}{\sqrt{\bar\rho}}\,\text{s} \quad (\bar\rho \text{ in g cm}^{-3})$$

The Sun (Dense, Formed Object)
$T_d \approx 2.3\times10^3\,\text{s} \approx 38\,\text{min}$

If pressure support vanished instantly, the Sun would collapse in ~38 minutes.

Typical ISM Cloud ($\bar\rho\sim10^{-23}$)
$T_d \approx 10^{15}\,\text{s} \approx 10^7\,\text{yr}$

Even the "fast" dynamical timescale is 10 million years, because the density is so low.

As free-fall proceeds, compression raises density, adiabatic heating raises temperature, and ionisation dramatically raises opacity — together driving a substantial pressure increase. In the denser central regions this halts the collapse first, and the structure enters the virialized (Kelvin-Helmholtz) regime, discussed next.

03

The Virial Theorem, Negative Heat Capacity & the Kelvin-Helmholtz Timescale

In the virialized, quasi-static regime, the Virial Theorem holds: $$2K + \Omega = 0$$ where $K>0$ is the total thermal kinetic energy and $\Omega<0$ is the gravitational potential energy. Contracting the structure slightly ($d\Omega<0$) forces $dK = -d\Omega/2 > 0$ — the kinetic energy must increase. The total energy $U=K+\Omega$; substituting $K=-\Omega/2$ gives $U=\Omega/2<0$, so upon contraction: $$dK = +\frac{|d\Omega|}{2}\ (\text{heats up}) \qquad dU = +\frac{d\Omega}{2}<0\ (\text{radiates energy away})$$

Negative Heat Capacity — In Plain Language

Of the gravitational energy released by contraction, exactly half heats the gas and half is radiated as luminosity. The gas simultaneously emits energy and gets hotter. This is a rigorous consequence of the Virial Theorem, and it governs pre-MS evolution, the post-MS giant phase, and every other gravity-controlled contraction phase.

The Kelvin-Helmholtz Timescale

The luminosity of a virialized contracting proto-star is powered by gravitational energy release, $L=|dU/dt|=\tfrac12|d\Omega/dt|$. Integrating over the contraction lifetime, with $|\Omega|\approx GM^2/R$: $$t_{KH} = \frac{1}{2}\frac{GM^2}{LR}$$ This is the time a star of mass $M$, luminosity $L$, and radius $R$ can shine on gravitational contraction alone. For the Sun: $$t_{KH,\odot} \approx 5\times10^{14}\,\text{s} \approx 1.5\times10^7\,\text{yr}$$

Exam Trap: $t_{KH}$ Is Not the Main-Sequence Lifetime

Gravitational energy alone can power the Sun's present luminosity for only ~15 million years — yet geology and fossils place the Earth's age at ~4.5 billion years. This contradiction, identified by Kelvin and Helmholtz in the 19th century, was only resolved by the discovery of nuclear fusion. $t_{KH}$ is the time an unborn star spends descending to the main sequence — not its main-sequence lifetime.

04

The Thermonuclear Timescale, the Core's Staircase & the Degeneracy Fork

As Kelvin-Helmholtz contraction raises $T_c$, it eventually reaches the threshold for thermonuclear fusion — for hydrogen burning, $T_c\sim10^7\,\text{K}$. Once ignited, the released energy balances radiative losses and halts further contraction: the structure enters the thermonuclear timescale regime, $\sim10^{10}\,\text{yr}$ for $1\,M_\odot$. Physically, fusing four protons into one $^4$He nucleus (plus positrons and neutrinos) reduces the particle count by roughly a factor of 4; at fixed $T$, $P=nkT$ falls slightly, favouring gentle contraction — but on such a slow timescale that the star simply adjusts quasi-statically and appears stable. This is a long break in the core's tendency to contract.

The Staircase of Core Evolution

After each fuel reservoir is exhausted, the core contracts again on the Kelvin-Helmholtz timescale, heating until the next fuel ignites. Plotting $T_c$ against time produces a characteristic staircase: slanted segments ($\tau_G$, gravitational KH contraction, $T_c$ rising steeply) alternate with flat plateaus (stable nuclear burning, $T_c$ roughly constant). The successive burning phases, in order of increasing temperature, are $H\rightarrow He$, $He\rightarrow C,O$, $C\rightarrow Mg$, $O\rightarrow S$, and ultimately an Fe core for massive stars only.

This staircase describes a core that remains a perfect gas throughout. But after helium burning, conditions may drive the electrons into degeneracy: non-relativistic degeneracy gives $P\propto\rho^{5/3}$, relativistic gives $P\propto\rho^{4/3}$ — pressure that is nearly independent of temperature and far stronger than thermal pressure at the same density.

The Most Important Branching Point in Stellar Evolution

After $He\rightarrow C,O$ burning, each subsequent contraction phase must be checked against the degenerate boundary in $(\log\rho_c,\log T_c)$ space. For $M<8\,M_\odot$: the CO core goes degenerate, the staircase is permanently cut off, no further carbon ignition occurs — these stars end as white dwarfs. For $M>8\,M_\odot$: the core stays hot and massive enough to avoid degeneracy, proceeding through all burning stages to an iron core and ultimately a core-collapse supernova.

05

Cloud Collapse & the Jeans Criterion

Stars form from the gravitational collapse of interstellar gas clouds (Jeans, 1902). For a particle of mass $H$ at the periphery of a cloud of mass $M$ and radius $R$, gravitational binding energy is $GMH/R$ and thermal kinetic energy is $kT$. Gravity wins — the cloud collapses — when $$\frac{GMH}{R} \geq kT$$ If thermal energy dominates instead, the cloud disperses.

Deriving the Jeans Mass

Eliminating $R$ via $M=\tfrac43\pi R^3\rho$ and solving for $M$ in terms of the two observable ISM parameters, density $\rho$ and temperature $T$: $$M^{2/3} \geq \underbrace{\frac{k}{(4\pi/3)^{1/3}GH}}_{\equiv\, a}\cdot\frac{T}{\rho^{1/3}} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad M \geq a^{3/2}\frac{T^{3/2}}{\rho^{1/2}}$$ Numerically, with $H_2=2\times1.6\times10^{-24}\,\text{g}$, $G=6.67\times10^{-8}$, $k=1.4\times10^{-16}$:

$$M_J \geq 10^{23}\cdot T^{3/2}\cdot\rho^{-1/2}\,\text{g} \quad (T\text{ in K},\ \rho\text{ in g/cm}^3)$$

Clouds exceeding $M_J$ at their $(T,\rho)$ collapse; those below do not. The scaling $M_J\propto T^{3/2}\rho^{-1/2}$ says: hotter gas needs a more massive cloud to overcome thermal pressure (larger $M_J$); denser gas needs less mass to bind gravitationally (smaller $M_J$).

Numerically, for typical cold ISM ($\bar\rho\approx10^{-23}\,\text{g/cm}^3$, $T\approx10\,\text{K}$): $M_J\approx10^{35}\,\text{g}\approx100\,M_\odot$ — fifty times a solar-mass star. A single Jeans-mass clump must therefore fragment during collapse, and the fragmentation process determines the stellar mass spectrum. Individual solar-mass stars are not the natural product of collapse — a stellar aggregate (cluster or OB association) is; isolated field stars are escapees from such groups.

06

Collapse Triggers & the Initial Mass Function

The Jeans criterion identifies which clouds can collapse, not what triggers the collapse — the ISM can sit marginally stable for a long time. Identified physical triggers include:

  • Supernova shock fronts: an expanding blast wave compresses surrounding gas above the Jeans threshold, explaining the correlation of young clusters with SN remnants and sequential star-formation waves.
  • Cloud-cloud collisions: two ISM clouds on random trajectories collide, locally compressing gas past the threshold.
  • Spiral-arm compression: a spiral arm is a gravitational potential well; gas passing through is compressed, concentrating star formation along the arm.
  • Galaxy interactions: tidal forces during mergers drive massive gas inflows, triggering starbursts.

The Initial Mass Function (IMF)

Fragmentation during collapse sets the mass spectrum of the resulting stars, $\Psi(M)$. For $M\gtrsim1\,M_\odot$, observations follow the Salpeter (1955) power law: $$\Psi(M) = k\,M^{-s}, \quad s_\text{Salpeter}=2.35$$ A shallower slope ($s=1.5$) implies more high-mass stars; a steeper slope ($s=3.5$) implies more low-mass stars. This value is well measured locally and often (though not certainly) assumed universal.

Why the IMF Matters
  • Final fate: a star's endpoint (white dwarf, neutron star, black hole) is set entirely by initial mass — the IMF sets the ratio of compact-object types in a population.
  • Chemical enrichment: more massive stars evolve and return processed material faster; the IMF weights the enrichment timescale.
  • Core-collapse SNe rate: stars with $M\gtrsim10\,M_\odot$ end as CC-SNe; their rate is set directly by the IMF above $\log(M/M_\odot)\sim1$.
07

From Cloud to Protostar: Segments A → E

The proto-star's evolution begins at point A: a large, cool, diffuse cloud ($R\sim10^4\,R_\odot$, low $T_e$ of a few thousand K, and — because of its enormous radius — high $L$ despite the low temperature). In the H-R diagram this places point A in the upper right.

A–B: Free-Fall Collapse

Gravity dominates over pressure; the cloud falls essentially freely. The denser central regions collapse faster, reaching hydrostatic equilibrium first while the envelope is still falling — a differential collapse in which the inner core stabilises while the outer layers keep infalling. For $1\,M_\odot$, A–B takes ~100 years: a near-vertical drop in luminosity at roughly constant (low) $T_e$.

B–C: Shock & Luminosity Spike

The infalling envelope collides with the stabilised core, launching an outward shock that disperses surrounding gas and sharply reduces opacity $\kappa$. Since $\left.dT/dr\right|_\text{rad}\propto\kappa$, a sudden opacity drop lets radiation escape freely — luminosity spikes to a local maximum at point C in ~10 days for $1\,M_\odot$: too brief to observe.

C–E: Progressive Hydrostatic Equilibrium

After the shock passes, the proto-star establishes hydrostatic equilibrium progressively outward, layer by layer, until — at point E — the entire structure, centre to surface, is in hydrostatic equilibrium. This takes $\sim10^5$ yr for $1\,M_\odot$. At point E: $L\sim10^3\,L_\odot$, $R\sim10^2\,R_\odot$, $T_e\sim2500\,\text{K}$ — a large, puffed-up, very cool but luminous object at the extreme right of the H-R diagram.

Point E's Special Significance

Point E is the first moment the whole proto-star is in hydrostatic equilibrium — making it the entry point onto the Hayashi track, the physically fundamental locus described in the next section.

08

The Hayashi Track & the Forbidden Zone

For fixed composition, Hayashi tracks form a family of nearly-vertical lines in the H-R diagram — one per mass — representing models that are simultaneously in hydrostatic equilibrium and completely convective. Different masses sit at slightly different (higher-mass = slightly hotter) positions, but all are nearly vertical.

Physical Origin: Kramers Opacity & the Schwarzschild Criterion

The Kramers opacity law, $\kappa\propto\rho/T^{3.5}$, makes $\kappa$ very large at the low temperatures on the cool side of the H-R diagram, driving a steep radiative gradient: $$\left.\frac{dT}{dr}\right|_\text{rad} = -\frac{3\kappa\rho}{4\pi r^2}\frac{L(r)}{4acT^3}$$ Convective instability sets in when the Schwarzschild criterion $\nabla_\text{rad}>\nabla_\text{ad}$ (with $\nabla=d\log T/d\log P$) is satisfied. At Hayashi-regime temperatures, high $\kappa$ forces $\nabla_\text{rad}\gg\nabla_\text{ad}$ throughout the star, making the entire structure convective — the defining feature of a Hayashi-track model.

As the star contracts and heats down the Hayashi track, $\kappa\propto T^{-3.5}$ falls, and eventually $\nabla_\text{rad}$ drops below $\nabla_\text{ad}$ in the centre — the core turns radiative and the model leaves the Hayashi track (moves leftward).

The Hayashi Theorem — Universal, Not Just Pre-MS

At fixed mass and composition, there is a forbidden region to the right (cooler side) of the Hayashi track: no stable hydrostatic model can exist there. Three possible states at the same luminosity: left of the track = stable, partially convective (radiative core); on the track = stable, fully convective; right of the track = no stable solution exists at all, not merely "unlikely."

This constraint is universal — it applies at every evolutionary stage, not only pre-MS. When a post-MS star cools onto the red giant branch, it is again bounded by its own Hayashi track on the cool side.

09

Evolution Along the Hayashi Track & Departure

Once at point E, the proto-star descends its Hayashi track — luminosity falling, $T_e$ roughly constant — powered by gravitational contraction on the Kelvin-Helmholtz timescale, since the core is not yet hot enough for fusion. For $1\,M_\odot$, this descent takes $\sim10^5$–$10^6$ yr. The Virial Theorem operates continuously: half the released energy heats the interior, half is radiated; the falling luminosity reflects that a more tightly-bound, more slowly-contracting star releases energy at a diminishing rate.

Molecular Dissociation as an Energy Sink

As the core heats, a significant share of the released gravitational energy is absorbed by dissociation of $H_2\rightarrow2H$ and subsequent ionisation of H and He. These endothermic processes temporarily drop the effective adiabatic index below $5/3$, letting contraction proceed without a matching rapid temperature rise — an important brake on the early Hayashi descent.

Departure: Building the Radiative Core

As $T_c$ keeps rising, $\kappa\propto T^{-3.5}$ keeps falling in the core, reducing $\nabla_\text{rad}$ until the Schwarzschild criterion fails there — the core becomes radiative and the star leaves the Hayashi track, bending sharply toward higher $T_e$ in the H-R diagram. The convective envelope progressively retreats outward as the radiative core grows.

10

Mass-Dependent Departure Points & Partial Ignition

The point where a pre-MS track bends leftward off the Hayashi track — first radiative-core development — depends critically on mass, because more massive proto-stars have much higher Kelvin-Helmholtz luminosities ($L_{KH}\propto M^2/R$) and so contract and heat their cores far faster:

Point 1: $M>5\,M_\odot$

Departs early and high on the track — barely touches the Hayashi track before heading left.

Point 2: $1.25 \lt M \lt 3\,M_\odot$

Departs at intermediate luminosity, partway down the track.

Point 3: $\sim1\,M_\odot$

Departs at low luminosity, deep into the Hayashi descent.

Point 2 in Detail: First Partial Nuclear Ignition

For $1.25 \lt M \lt 3\,M_\odot$, Point 2 marks both the departure from the Hayashi track and the onset of partial pp-chain and CNO reactions — not yet self-sustaining cycles: $$^2\text{H} + {}^1\text{H} \rightarrow {}^3\text{He} + \gamma \qquad {}^{12}\text{C} + {}^1\text{H} \rightarrow {}^{13}\text{N} + \gamma \rightarrow {}^{13}\text{C} + e^+ + \nu \rightarrow {}^{14}\text{N} + \gamma$$ These contribute additional energy ($L_\varepsilon\nearrow$) alongside gravitational luminosity. Both radiative-core formation and partial ignition share the same root cause — rising $T_c$ simultaneously lowers $\kappa$ and eases Coulomb-barrier penetration.

Point 5: Nuclear Energy Takes Over

Point 5 marks the moment thermonuclear output begins to dominate over gravitational contraction. The extra energy causes the core to slightly expand (a perfect-gas thermostat response — detailed in Section 11), which temporarily decreases both $\rho_c$ and $T_c$, while $L_g/L$ begins its dramatic fall toward zero.

11

The Final Approach: The Perfect-Gas Thermostat & the ZAMS

The entire pre-MS path is marked by a continuous rise in $\rho_c$ and $T_c$ — the direct consequence of the Virial Theorem's negative heat capacity. As partial reactions activate, $L_g/L$ begins falling from $\approx1$ toward zero.

Closing the pp Chain: $^3$He+$^3$He

The last reaction needed to complete the pp chain is $$^3\text{He} + {}^3\text{He} \rightarrow {}^4\text{He} + 2p$$ which requires a higher $T_c$ than earlier pp steps because it involves two $Z=2$ nuclei and a correspondingly larger Coulomb barrier. $^3$He accumulates in the core until this temperature is finally reached — the ZAMS is defined as the moment this reaction fully activates, closing the chain $4p\rightarrow{}^4\text{He}+2e^++2\nu+\text{energy}$ and providing sustained thermonuclear power.

The Perfect-Gas Thermostat

When $^3$He+$^3$He ignites, the extra energy input causes the core to expand slightly — decreasing both $\rho_c$ and $T_c$ as the gas does work against gravity. In the $\log\rho_c$ and $\log T_c$ vs. $\log t$ diagrams this appears as a small but identifiable dip; $L_g/L$ even goes briefly negative (the core absorbs energy rather than releasing it) before the star stabilises at $L_g/L=0$.

This is the same negative-feedback mechanism that stabilises the entire main sequence: extra nuclear output → core expands → cools → reaction rate falls back down; a deficit → core contracts → heats → reaction rate rises back up.

Exam Trap: The Pre-ZAMS Dip Is Not "Contraction Resuming"

Just before the ZAMS, $\rho_c$, $T_c$, and $L_g/L$ all briefly dip. It is tempting to misread this as gravitational contraction reasserting itself — it is the exact opposite: the thermostat responding to new nuclear energy input by expanding and cooling the core.

Defining the ZAMS

At the ZAMS: $L_\text{nuc}=L_\text{surface}$ (thermal equilibrium), hydrostatic equilibrium holds everywhere, and there is no net contraction ($L_g/L=0$) — the star enters the thermonuclear timescale regime for the first time. For fixed composition, each mass maps to exactly one $(L,T_e)$ point on the ZAMS — a one-parameter family in mass alone, which is why the ZAMS is such a tight, clean sequence in the H-R diagram.

12

Pre-MS Timescales & the Mass-Dependence Scaling Law

The pre-MS phase lasts $10^4$ to $10^7$ yr — a small fraction of any main-sequence lifetime — with a strong inverse mass dependence:

Mass ($M_\odot$)Pre-MS duration
0.5$\sim10^8$ yr
1.0$\sim10^7$ yr
5.0$\sim10^5$ yr
15.0$\sim10^4$ yr

The approximate scaling formula:

$$t_\text{PMS} \sim 4\times10^7\left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right)^2\frac{R_\odot}{R}\frac{L_\odot}{L}\,\text{yr}$$
Exam Trap: The $M^2$ Term Is Misleading

Naive reasoning from the $M^2$ factor alone suggests a longer pre-MS for massive stars. The opposite is true: massive proto-stars are so much more luminous and larger that the $L^{-1}$ and $R^{-1}$ factors dominate completely, making pre-MS evolution far shorter for high mass. A $10\,M_\odot$ proto-star ($L\sim10^4\,L_\odot$, $R\sim10\,R_\odot$) finishes in $\sim4\times10^4$ yr — 1000× faster than the Sun.

Consequence: in a young star-forming region, massive stars ($M\gtrsim10\,M_\odot$) are already on the main sequence burning hydrogen while solar-mass stars in the same cluster are still descending the Hayashi track — a young cluster's H-R diagram shows both simultaneously.

13

Summary: The Complete Pre-MS Evolutionary Path

Three overarching principles govern every stage of pre-MS evolution:

  1. Virial Theorem / negative heat capacity: every gravitational contraction phase heats the interior while radiating half the released energy, driving the inexorable core-temperature rise that eventually ignites fusion.
  2. The Hayashi constraint: the forbidden zone to the right of the Hayashi track is physically inaccessible; the proto-star must descend vertically (fully convective) before moving leftward (as the radiative core grows) — true at all evolutionary stages, not only pre-MS.
  3. The perfect-gas thermostat: a perfect-gas core expands and cools in response to excess energy generation, and contracts and heats in response to a deficit — the negative feedback that stabilises nuclear burning from the ZAMS onward.

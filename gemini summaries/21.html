Main Sequence Evolution: Structure, Burning, and the Road Off the ZAMS

An exhaustive study companion covering slide lecture series "21.MS_evolution.pdf" verbatim, detailing the Zero Age Main Sequence, the pp chain/CNO cycle dichotomy, mass-dependent stellar structure, and the growth of the isothermal helium core toward the Schönberg-Chandrasekhar limit.

01

The Zero Age Main Sequence (ZAMS)

The Zero Age Main Sequence (ZAMS) is a reference locus in the Hertzsprung-Russell (HR) diagram connecting stars of different masses at the exact instant they ignite hydrogen burning in their cores. "Zero age" refers literally to time zero of a star's nuclear-burning life: a star is placed on the ZAMS the moment its core reaches the temperature required to sustain hydrogen-to-helium fusion.

It is essential to understand what the ZAMS is not: it is not an evolutionary track. A track shows how a single star of fixed mass moves through the HR diagram over time. The ZAMS, by contrast, is a snapshot locus that simultaneously shows stars of wildly different masses, all at time zero of their nuclear lives. Once evolution begins, each star departs from its ZAMS position along its own individual track.

Exam Trap: ZAMS ≠ Evolutionary Track

This is one of the most common conflations in the subject. The ZAMS is a snapshot across masses at $t=0$; an evolutionary track is the time-history of one single mass. Never describe "moving along the ZAMS" — stars move away from it, along tracks.

A key physical property of every ZAMS star is chemical homogeneity: the composition is uniform throughout the entire volume. This arises because, during the pre-main-sequence (pre-MS) phase, deep convection thoroughly mixed the stellar material. When the star arrives on the ZAMS there is no abundance gradient from centre to surface.

The ZAMS is the only location in the HR diagram where a firm, straightforward mass-luminosity relation can be established, precisely because all ZAMS stars are freshly ignited and have not yet accumulated the complications of chemical evolution or structural change.

The ZAMS spans effective temperatures from roughly $T_{eff}\approx2700$ K (low-mass end) to $T_{eff}\approx53{,}000$ K (high-mass end) — a factor of $\sim$20 — while mass varies from $0.08\,M_\odot$ to $90\,M_\odot$, a factor of over 1000. This enormous mass range compresses into only a factor of $\sim$20 in surface temperature, illustrating how strongly stellar structure moderates the response to mass.

02

Mass Cut-Off Limits: Brown Dwarfs to Eddington Instability

The Lower Cut-Off: $M_{low}\approx0.08\,M_\odot$

Below this critical mass, a forming object never reaches the central temperature required to ignite the full pp chain:

$$M_{crit} = 0.08\,M_\odot \approx 0.1\,M_\odot$$

Objects with $M<M_{crit}$ are not stars in the astrophysical sense — they are brown dwarfs, "failed stars" whose cores never sustain a self-regulating thermonuclear furnace. They simply radiate away gravitational potential energy and cool over time, lying along the Hayashi (pre-MS) track without ever converging onto the true ZAMS.

The Upper Cut-Off: $M_{up}\sim90\,M_\odot$

At the other extreme, very massive stars become structurally unstable. This limit is not an absolute, rigorous cut-off — it depends on metallicity — but it captures a real physical message: forming a stable star with $M\gtrsim100\,M_\odot$ is extremely difficult, because radiation pressure becomes comparable to gravity, threatening hydrostatic equilibrium in the outer layers (the Eddington luminosity, Section 3).

03

The Eddington Luminosity: A Derivation from First Principles

In the outer envelopes of very hot, luminous stars, radiation pressure can dominate over gas pressure. The pressure gradient is then driven by the radiative flux $F_{rad}=L/4\pi r^2$:

$$\frac{dP}{dr} = -\frac{\kappa\rho}{c}F_{rad}$$

Hydrostatic equilibrium simultaneously requires the pressure gradient to balance gravity:

$$\frac{dP}{dr} = -\frac{GM\rho}{r^2}$$

Setting these equal, at the point where radiation force exactly counterbalances gravity, both $\rho$ and $r^2$ cancel:

$$L_{ed} = \frac{4\pi GMc}{\kappa}$$

This is the maximum luminosity a star of mass $M$ can radiate while maintaining hydrostatic equilibrium at opacity $\kappa$. If $L>L_{ed}$, radiation pressure exceeds gravity in the outer regions and mass loss can be driven.

Numerical Evaluation and the $90\,M_\odot$ Connection

For hot, massive stars the dominant opacity source is electron (Thomson) scattering, $\kappa=0.2(1+X)$. With $X=0.7$: $\kappa=0.34\ \text{cm}^2\text{g}^{-1}$. Substituting CGS constants:

$$\frac{L_{ed}}{L_\odot} \approx 3.8\times10^4\,\frac{M}{M_\odot}$$

For a $90\,M_\odot$ star this gives $L_{ed}\approx3.5\times10^6\,L_\odot$. Crucially, the observed/theoretical luminosity of a $90\,M_\odot$ ZAMS star is only about a factor of 3 below $L_{ed}$ — a near-coincidence explaining why forming stars above $\sim90\,M_\odot$ at solar metallicity is so difficult: their own luminosity sits close enough to the Eddington limit that radiative forces destabilize the structure.

Exam Trap: The $90\,M_\odot$ Limit Is Not a Hard Wall

Do not quote $90\,M_\odot$ as a rigorous universal ceiling. It is metallicity-dependent (lower $Z$ → lower opacity → the star can push closer to $L_{ed}$ at higher mass) and stems entirely from proximity to $L_{ed}$, not from a separate hard physical law.

04

Hydrogen Burning Modes: the pp Chain vs. the CNO Cycle

Every star on the ZAMS is a core hydrogen-burning star, via one of two thermonuclear chains with different activation temperatures:

pp Chain (Proton-Proton)

Ignites at $T_c\approx1.4\times10^7$ K. Dominant in low-mass stars including the Sun.

$$\epsilon_{pp}\propto\rho X^2 T_6^5$$
CNO Cycle (Carbon-Nitrogen-Oxygen)

Ignites at $T_c\approx1.8\times10^7$ K. Requires slightly higher $T$, but overwhelmingly dominant at higher masses.

$$\epsilon_{CNO}\propto\rho X Z_{CNO}T_6^{15}$$

Because core temperature $T_c$ rises with stellar mass, there is a transition mass above which CNO takes over, at a surprisingly low value:

$$M_{transition}\approx1.2\,M_\odot$$

At $1\,M_\odot$, $T_c=1.4\times10^7$ K and pp dominates; at $15\,M_\odot$, $T_c=3.4\times10^7$ K and CNO dominates entirely. In the Sun, $\epsilon_{pp}/\epsilon_{CNO}\sim100$ — the pp chain supplies about 99% of solar luminosity.

The exponent $n\sim15$ for CNO versus $n\sim5$ for pp is the central fact driving all subsequent structural differences. Although core temperatures differ only modestly between low- and high-mass ZAMS stars (both a few $\times10^7$ K), the CNO cycle's extreme temperature sensitivity ($T_6^{15}$) makes $\epsilon_{CNO}$ enormously larger than $\epsilon_{pp}$ even for modest $T$ increases. In $M>1.2\,M_\odot$ stars, $\sim$90% of the luminosity is generated within the innermost 10% of stellar mass, versus 70% within the innermost 20% in the Sun.

Exam Trap: It's the Exponent, Not the Temperature

The transition mass $\approx1.2\,M_\odot$ looks deceptively low given how close $1.4\times10^7$ K and $1.8\times10^7$ K are. What matters is not the absolute core temperature but the $T^{15}$ vs. $T^5$ exponent — a small $T$ increase produces a disproportionate $\epsilon_{CNO}$ jump. Never justify the transition by "temperature difference" alone.

05

Why CNO Burning Forces a Convective Core

The concentrated energy production of the CNO cycle directly causes central convection in massive stars. Convection is triggered when the radiative gradient exceeds the adiabatic gradient:

$$\nabla_{rad} > \nabla_{ad}, \qquad \left.\frac{dT}{dr}\right|_{rad} = -\frac{3\kappa\rho}{4acT^3}\frac{L_r}{4\pi r^2}$$

When $\epsilon_{CNO}$ is large due to its steep temperature dependence, $L_r$ rises very steeply near the centre. Combined with the concentrated nature of the energy production (a large $L_r/r^2$ term), $\nabla_{rad}$ exceeds $\nabla_{ad}$ in the core, triggering convection:

$$\text{H-burning via CNO} \implies \text{convective core}$$

In contrast, the pp chain distributes energy production over a larger volume, so $L_r$ rises more gradually and the core remains radiative. The type of hydrogen burning — determined entirely by stellar mass — therefore determines whether the core is convective or radiative, and this single branch point cascades through nearly every property discussed in later sections.

06

Stellar Structure Along the ZAMS: Three Regimes

The internal structure of a ZAMS star — specifically where convection occurs — depends on mass. Two transition masses define three structural regimes, arising from two distinct physical mechanisms:

Fig. 1: The three ZAMS structural regimes, defined by two transition masses.

$M>1.2\,M_\odot$: Convective Core, Radiative Envelope

For a $5\,M_\odot$ star, the innermost $\sim$10% by radius is the H-burning/convective region, driven by the intense concentrated CNO flux; the outer 70% is a radiative envelope. This convective core homogenizes the composition within it, enlarging the effective fuel reservoir and producing flat-topped H-abundance profiles (Section 12).

$0.3\,M_\odot<M<1.2\,M_\odot$: Radiative Core, Convective Envelope

For a $1\,M_\odot$ star, the pp-burning core ($\sim$20% by radius) is radiative, while the outer $\sim$30% is convective. Two effects cooperate to produce this outer convection:

  1. Low surface temperatures raise opacity via the Kramers law, $\kappa\propto T^{-3.5}$, elevating $\nabla_{rad}$.
  2. Partial ionization zones near the surface depress $\nabla_{ad}=1-1/\gamma$ from $\sim$0.4 toward $\sim$0.1, since ionization absorbs energy that would otherwise raise $c_P,c_V$ toward $\gamma\to1$.

Both effects together make the outer layers of the Sun (and all stars in this mass range) convective.

$M<0.3\,M_\odot$: Fully Convective

This is an extreme version of the convective-envelope mechanism operating throughout the entire star: uniformly low temperature keeps opacity high and $\nabla_{ad}$ suppressed everywhere. The Hayashi (pre-MS) track for these stars nearly coincides with the ZAMS, since the convective structure barely changes upon H-ignition.

07

Core Temperature and the Mass-Luminosity Relation

If all stars burned only via the pp chain, core temperature would need to rise steeply with mass to sustain higher luminosities. The CNO cycle's enormously stronger temperature dependence lets the same (or larger) luminosity be produced at much lower core temperatures: a $1.5\,M_\odot$ star has $T_c\approx1.81\times10^7$ K, while a $7\,M_\odot$ star (a $\sim$5× mass increase) has only $T_c\approx2.91\times10^7$ K — a factor of $\sim$1.6.

A crucial secondary consequence: as mass increases, central density decreases. From the ZAMS data table: $\rho_c\approx402.5\ \text{g cm}^{-3}$ at $0.1\,M_\odot$, but only $\rho_c\approx13.5\ \text{g cm}^{-3}$ at $7.0\,M_\odot$. High-mass stars have significantly less dense cores than low-mass stars — a fact that will govern whether the post-MS core ever becomes electron-degenerate (Section 15).

Exam Trap: Higher Mass ≠ Higher Central Density

It is tempting to assume a more massive, more luminous, hotter star also has a denser core. The opposite is true: $\rho_c$ falls with increasing mass because CNO burning limits $T_c$ growth. Low central density is precisely why high-mass cores avoid electron degeneracy ($T/\rho^{2/3}<D$ is much harder to satisfy at low $\rho$) — connect this trend directly to the degeneracy condition when reasoning about post-MS fate.

The Broken Power Law

The different burning processes modify the mass-luminosity relation into three distinct regimes:

Regime Mass Range Burning Mode $L\propto M^n$
Upper main sequence $M\gtrsim1.2\,M_\odot$ CNO $n\approx3.6$ (shallower)
Lower main sequence $M\lesssim1.2\,M_\odot$ pp $n\approx4$–$4.5$ (steeper)
Very low mass $M\ll1\,M_\odot$ pp $n\approx2.6$ (shallowest)

The shallower upper-MS slope reflects the CNO cycle limiting core-temperature growth: each added unit of mass buys a smaller fractional luminosity gain than it would under pure pp burning. Four distinct effects, all traceable to the CNO cycle's $T^{15}$ sensitivity, follow from this single mechanism: (1) sets convective cores above $1.2\,M_\odot$; (2) limits $T_c$ growth with mass; (3) reduces the mass-luminosity exponent; (4) shapes the post-MS "hook" signature (Section 12).

Exam Trap: There Is No Single Mass-Luminosity Exponent

Do not quote "$L\propto M^4$" as a universal law. It is a broken power law with three distinct regimes above — the exponent depends on which burning mode and mass range applies.

08

Metallicity and Helium Abundance Effects on the ZAMS

Metallicity ($Z\nearrow$, $Y$ fixed)

Higher $Z$ raises opacity (more bound-free/bound-bound absorbers), steepening $\nabla_{rad}$, so the star must be fainter and cooler to maintain hydrostatic equilibrium. The ZAMS shifts down and to the right; fainter, cooler stars radiate more slowly and so have longer MS lifetimes.

Lower $Z$ shifts the ZAMS up and to the left; at very low $Z$ ($Z=0.0001$), scarce CNO catalysts suppress CNO burning, so pp dominates to higher masses, requiring hotter cores — low-metallicity stars are systematically hotter-cored at fixed mass.

At fixed population age, higher-$Z$ populations (fainter, longer-lived) have a larger turnoff mass: at 13 Gyr, $Z=0.0001$ gives $M_{TO}=0.77\,M_\odot$ vs. $Z=0.02$ giving $M_{TO}=0.85\,M_\odot$.

Helium Abundance ($Y\nearrow$, $Z$ fixed)

Helium acts through two channels: (1) reduced opacity as $X=1-Y-Z$ falls, and (2) increased mean molecular weight $\mu$ (He nuclei are heavier per electron), making the gas more compact and hotter. Since $L\approx\mu^7$, even modest He enhancements drive large luminosity increases — a brighter, hotter star.

Since He-enhanced stars burn faster, they have shorter lifetimes, so at fixed age a He-rich population has a smaller turnoff mass: at 13 Gyr, $Z=0.002$, $Y=0.24$ gives $M_{TO}=0.79\,M_\odot$ vs. $Y=0.40$ giving only $M_{TO}=0.59\,M_\odot$.

Exam Trap: "Brighter/Hotter" Is Not One Mechanism

Higher $Z$ and higher $Y$ both look like they push "brighter/hotter" in some sense, but they act through entirely different physics and have opposite effects on turnoff mass at fixed population age: higher $Z\Rightarrow$ longer lifetime $\Rightarrow$ larger $M_{TO}$; higher $Y\Rightarrow$ shorter lifetime $\Rightarrow$ smaller $M_{TO}$. Never conflate the two.

The Main Sequence as an Evolutionary Phase

As an evolutionary phase, the MS spans core hydrogen burning from H-ignition (Point 1, the ZAMS) to core H-exhaustion (Point 2, defined as $X_c<0.05$). The set of all Point-2 positions across mass tracks forms the "terminal age main sequence" (TAMS), running roughly parallel to the ZAMS.

Why the MS Is the Longest Phase

Core H-burning is the most energetically efficient thermonuclear reaction operating on the largest available fuel reservoir. MS lifetimes at solar metallicity:

10

Solar Evolution During the Main Sequence & the Role of $\mu$

Although the MS is the most quiescent phase, properties change slowly. Over the Sun's $\sim4.6$ Gyr lifetime: luminosity rose from $\sim0.7\,L_\odot$ to today's $1.0\,L_\odot$ ($\sim$30% increase); radius increased modestly; $T_{eff}$ rose from $\sim5650$ K to $\sim5780$ K. The luminosity profile plateaus at $L_r=L_\odot$ by $r/R_\odot\approx0.25$ — no energy is produced in the outer 75–80% of the Sun by radius.

The Driving Mechanism: Rising Mean Molecular Weight

As four H nuclei combine into one He nucleus, the number of free particles drops, raising $\mu$ in a fully ionized plasma:

$$\mu = \frac{1}{2X+\tfrac34Y+\tfrac12Z}, \qquad P=\frac{k\rho T}{\mu H}$$

Since $P=k\rho T/\mu H$, rising $\mu$ at fixed $T$ lowers pressure, initiating a chain of consequences:

$$\mu\nearrow \implies P\searrow \implies \text{core contracts} \implies \rho\nearrow,\,T\nearrow \implies \epsilon\nearrow,\,L\nearrow$$

The star is virialized, so contraction converts gravitational potential energy into thermal energy, raising $T_c$ and burning efficiency — extremely slowly (the thermonuclear timescale), but inexorably throughout the entire MS.

A notable feature is the $^3$He abundance peak: the pp chain produces $^3$He via $H^2+H^1\to{^3\text{He}}+\gamma$, but the follow-on reaction $^3\text{He}+{^3\text{He}}\to{^4\text{He}}+H^1+H^1$ is slow at moderate temperature, causing a visible bump in the abundance profile — a direct observational signature of the incomplete pp chain at those depths.

11

Track Morphology During Segment 1–2: Low- vs. High-Mass

In both mass regimes, rising $T$ dominates over falling $X$, so $\epsilon\nearrow$ and $L\nearrow$ during the MS. What differs is where the burning happens, and this geometry decides the direction the star moves in the HR diagram.

Low Mass ($M<1.2\,M_\odot$): pp Chain

The pp chain burns over a large volume ($\sim$20–30% by radius), so $\mu$-driven core contraction affects a significant mass fraction and partially counteracts envelope expansion. $R$ increases only modestly; since $L$ still grows faster than $R^2$ can compensate, $T_{eff}$ rises slightly. The track moves up and to the left (hotter).

High Mass ($M>1.2\,M_\odot$): CNO Cycle

CNO burning is confined to $\sim$10% by radius, so $\mu$-driven contraction is localized and essentially unopposed by any counteracting envelope contraction. The envelope expands substantially; since $R^2$ now grows faster than $L$, $T_{eff}$ falls. The track moves up and to the right (cooler) — visible as the rightward slope of upper-MS tracks in the HR diagram.

The fundamental explanation is the different spatial extent of the burning region (20–30% vs. $\sim$10% of the radius) — not any difference in total luminosity or temperature per se.

Exam Trap: The Sign of $\Delta T_{eff}$ Flips With Mass

Do not assume both mass regimes redden or both bluen during the MS. Low-mass tracks shift hotter, high-mass tracks shift cooler — the sign flips because burning-region geometry determines whether core contraction can counteract envelope expansion. And remember: increasing $R$ does not automatically mean cooling — only if $R^2$ outpaces $L$'s growth. In low-mass stars $L$ outpaces $R^2$, so $T_{eff}$ still rises even as $R$ grows.

12

Segment 2–3: H-Profile Shape and the Turnoff Hook

Point 2 marks core H-exhaustion ($X_c<0.05$). What happens next depends entirely on the H-abundance profile left behind — which in turn depends on whether the core was radiative (low mass) or convective (high mass).

Low Mass: Smooth Gradient, Seamless Transition

The pp chain burns hydrogen in a smooth, diffuse region with no sharp mixing boundary, so $X$ decreases monotonically from surface to centre. When the centre first reaches $X\approx0$, the adjacent shell already has fuel and is nearly at ignition temperature — H-shell burning ignites without pause. Points 2 and 3 effectively coincide, and the track shows a smooth, continuous curve off the MS with no hook.

High Mass: Step Profile, Gravitational Contraction, and the Hook

Continuous convective mixing keeps the core's H-abundance flat at all times, producing a step-shaped profile: $X\approx$ const inside the convective boundary, then a sharp jump to the initial abundance. The convective boundary retreats inward over time as the He-rich mixture's lower opacity shrinks $\nabla_{rad}$ below $\nabla_{ad}$ (via $\kappa_{FF}\propto10^{22}(X+Y)(1+X)\rho/T^{3.5}$). At Point 2, the entire convective core reaches $X=0$ simultaneously.

This creates a structural crisis: the core is too cool to ignite helium ($T\sim10^8$ K required, far above $T_c\sim$ few$\times10^7$ K), and the overlying shell is also too cool to immediately ignite. With no nuclear energy source anywhere, the star contracts under gravity on the Kelvin-Helmholtz timescale, radiating entirely from gravitational contraction until the shell heats enough to ignite at Point 3.

Fig. 2: Schematic HR-diagram turnoff — smooth low-mass track vs. the convective-core "hook".

Observational confirmation: the Color-Magnitude Diagram of globular cluster NGC 1978 (HST/ACS, age $\sim2$ Gyr, $M_{TO}\approx1.5\,M_\odot$) shows a prominent turnoff hook — one of the clearest observational validations of the convective-core scenario in intermediate-mass stars.

Exam Trap: The Hook Tracks Convection, Not "Bigness"

The hook is diagnostic of a convective core during the MS, not of high mass in some vague sense — it appears only for $M\gtrsim1.2\,M_\odot$ and is most visible in the $2.2$–$6\,M_\odot$ range (Section 15). Do not describe it as a generic "massive star feature" without tying it to the underlying convective mixing.

13

The Growing Isothermal Helium Core

From Point 3 onward, every star has a thermonuclearly inactive helium core: $\epsilon=0$, $L(r)=0$ within it. Because there is no energy source, the radiative temperature gradient vanishes:

$$\left.\frac{dT}{dr}\right|_{rad} = -\frac{3\kappa\rho}{4acT^3}\frac{L(r)}{4\pi r^2} = 0$$

A zero gradient means the core is isothermal — uniform temperature throughout, surrounded by an actively burning thick H-shell that continuously deposits fresh He onto the core surface, growing its mass over time.

Exam Trap: Isothermal ≠ Unsupported

"Isothermal" only means $dT/dr=0$ — it does not mean the core lacks pressure support. Hydrostatic equilibrium is still satisfied via a density gradient rather than a temperature gradient: $$-\frac{GM\rho}{r^2} = \frac{dP}{dr} = \frac{kT}{\mu H}\frac{d\rho}{dr}$$ Higher central density provides the outward pressure gradient needed at fixed $T$. This balance has a physical maximum — the Schönberg-Chandrasekhar limit (Section 14).

The key diagnostic is the core mass fraction $q=M_c/M_{tot}$, which grows continuously as the shell deposits mass. As $q$ increases, the core must supply ever more pressure to support the overlying envelope — a demand it eventually cannot meet.

14

The Schönberg-Chandrasekhar Mass Limit

Derived analytically by Schönberg and Chandrasekhar in 1942, the SC limit is the maximum mass an isothermal, ideal-gas core can support against the overlying envelope while in hydrostatic equilibrium. It reflects a competition between thermal pressure (supporting the core) and the core's own self-gravity (working against that support) — as the core grows, its self-gravity increases and the maximum pressure it can supply falls.

Two Equivalent Formulations

Mass-Fraction Form
$$q\le q_0 = 0.37\left(\frac{\mu_e}{\mu_{ic}}\right)^2 \approx 0.08$$

With $\mu_e\approx0.62$ (H-rich envelope), $\mu_{ic}\approx1.33$ (He-rich core).

Absolute-Mass Form
$$M_c \le M_{sc} = 0.10\,M_{tot}$$

The commonly quoted "10% rule" — a practical rounding of the compositional result.

Derivation Sketch

  1. Integrate hydrostatic equilibrium over the isothermal core mass, yielding a virial-like relation, $4\pi R_{ic}^3P_{ic}-2K_{ic}=U_{ic}$; maximizing $P_{ic}$ over $R_{ic}$ gives $P_{ic}^{max}\propto1/M_{ic}^2$ — a more massive core is inherently less able to support itself.
  2. Integrate hydrostatic equilibrium over the envelope (using $M_{ic}^2\ll M^2$) to estimate the pressure the envelope exerts on the core: $P_{env}\propto1/M^2$.
  3. Equate $P_{ic}^{max}=P_{env}$ and solve for $M_{ic}/M$; the coefficient scales as $(\mu_{env}/\mu_{ic})^2$.
$$\frac{M_{ic}}{M} \approx 0.7\left(\frac{\mu_{env}}{\mu_{ic}}\right)^2 \quad\text{(course estimate; accepted value: }0.37\text{)}$$

The SC limit scales as $(\mu_{env}/\mu_{ic})^2$; since the He-rich core has higher $\mu$ than the H-rich envelope, $q_0<0.37$. The limit is fundamentally a consequence of the chemical contrast between the He-rich core (product of nuclear burning) and the H-rich envelope — if the two had identical composition, there would be no chemically distinct "core" to speak of.

Exam Trap: Two Numbers, Not Interchangeable

$q_0\approx0.08$ is the compositionally-derived mass fraction; $M_c\le0.10\,M_{tot}$ is the commonly-quoted rounded practical form — know both exist and which is being asked for. Also: the textbook one-zone derivation above gives coefficient 0.7, not the accepted 0.37 — the discrepancy comes from approximations in estimating $P_{env}$ and $R$; the $(\mu_{env}/\mu_{ic})^2$ scaling is the exact, examinable part, not the coefficient itself.

15

Post-MS Core Fate: Three Mass Regimes & Electron Degeneracy

Whether the growing He core stays ideal gas or becomes electron-degenerate before hitting the SC limit is the pivotal question determining the entire subsequent evolutionary path:

RegimeMass RangeCore Behavior
1 — No isothermal core $M>6\,M_\odot$ Contraction begins immediately at H-exhaustion; core stays ideal-gas throughout; direct He ignition, no developed RGB.
2 — Temporary isothermal core $2.2\,M_\odot<M<6\,M_\odot$ Core grows in ideal-gas conditions until it hits the SC limit ($M_c\approx0.10M$), then contracts, heats, and ignites He; small hook in the track.
3 — Long-lasting degenerate core $M<2.2\,M_\odot$ Core crosses the degeneracy line before reaching the SC limit; supported by $P\propto\rho^{5/3}$, grows far beyond the SC limit without heating; long RGB ascent, delayed He ignition (helium flash).

The Electron Degeneracy Condition

Degeneracy occurs when all states below the Fermi energy are filled. Requiring thermal energy to fall below the Fermi energy:

$$\frac32kT < \epsilon_F = \frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}\left[3\pi^2\left(\frac{Z}{A}\right)\frac{\rho}{m_H}\right]^{2/3}$$

rearranges to the simple degeneracy criterion:

$$\frac{T}{\rho^{2/3}} < D \approx 10^5\ ^\circ\text{K cm}^2\text{g}^{-2/3}$$

In the $\log\rho$-$\log T$ plane, the degeneracy boundary is $\log T=\tfrac23\log\rho+5.11$; below and to the right of this line, matter is degenerate.

Fig. 3: The $\log\rho$-$\log T$ diagram — high-mass cores stay in the ideal-gas "passageway"; low-mass cores cross into degeneracy.

The different core trajectories trace directly back to the very different ZAMS central densities from Section 7 ($\rho_c\sim13$ g cm$^{-3}$ at $7\,M_\odot$ vs. $\rho_c\sim400$ g cm$^{-3}$ at $0.1\,M_\odot$): low-mass stars start much closer to the degeneracy boundary.

Exam Trap: Two Different Limits Separate the Three Regimes

Regimes 1/2 are separated from Regime 3 by the degeneracy boundary, while Regimes 1 and 2 are separated by whether the core ever needs to obey the Schönberg-Chandrasekhar limit at all. These are two distinct physical limits, not the same limit applied twice at different masses — always identify which boundary is crossed first for a given mass.

Every aspect of main-sequence and immediate post-MS evolution follows from one physical chain: mass determines the burning mechanism; the burning mechanism determines everything else.

Mass > $1.2\,M_\odot$

CNO dominates → concentrated energy → convective core → flat H-profile → hook at Points 2–3 → lower central density (CNO limits $T_c$ growth) → avoids degeneracy → direct He ignition, no flash.

Mass < $1.2\,M_\odot$

pp dominates → diffuse energy → radiative core → smooth H-profile → no hook, seamless shell ignition → higher central density → SC limit never reached before degeneracy → degenerate core → long RGB → helium flash.

The four effects of CNO burning (structure, core temperature, mass-luminosity slope, post-MS morphology) are not independent phenomena — they all trace back to the same root cause: the extreme temperature sensitivity ($T^{15}$) of the CNO cycle. This single fact is the key to understanding all of main-sequence stellar evolution.

Question 1

What best describes the Zero Age Main Sequence (ZAMS)?

Correct! The ZAMS is a $t=0$ snapshot across masses, not an evolutionary track.

Question 2

Why is a $\sim100\,M_\odot$ star so difficult to form stably at solar metallicity?

Correct! A $90\,M_\odot$ ZAMS star's luminosity is only a factor of ~3 below $L_{ed}$.

Question 3

What is the true reason the pp/CNO transition mass ($\approx1.2\,M_\odot$) is so low despite similar core temperatures?

Correct! It's the exponent, not the absolute temperature, that matters.

Question 4

How does central density $\rho_c$ change as ZAMS stellar mass increases?

Correct! Higher mass means lower central density, which is why high-mass cores avoid degeneracy.

Question 5

Higher helium abundance $Y$ (at fixed $Z$) has what effect on the turnoff mass of a population at fixed age?

Correct! Higher $Y$ and higher $Z$ push turnoff mass in opposite directions.

Question 6

Approximately how does main-sequence lifetime scale with mass?

Correct! Lifetime falls steeply with mass since $t_{MS}\propto M/L\propto M^{-3}$.

Question 7

During Segment 1–2, why does a low-mass star's track shift up and to the left (hotter) while a high-mass star's track shifts up and to the right (cooler)?

Correct! It's the geometry of the burning region, not the size of the luminosity increase, that flips the sign.

Question 8

Why does a high-mass star's evolutionary track show a "hook" at the main-sequence turnoff, while a low-mass star's does not?

Correct! The step-function H-profile of a convective core forces a Kelvin-Helmholtz contraction phase, producing the hook.

Question 9

What does "isothermal" mean for the growing helium core, and how is it still held up against gravity?

Correct! Isothermal only removes the temperature gradient; density gradient still supplies pressure support.

Question 10

What does the Schönberg-Chandrasekhar limit represent?

Correct! (That's the Chandrasekhar limit, a different concept, for degenerate cores.)

Question 11

Why do stars below $\sim2.2\,M_\odot$ develop a long-lasting degenerate core and a well-developed Red Giant Branch?

Correct! Crossing the degeneracy boundary first is what produces the long RGB ascent and eventual helium flash.

Question 12

What single physical fact underlies all four major effects of CNO burning (structure, $T_c$-mass relation, mass-luminosity slope, and post-MS "hook")?

Correct! The $T^{15}$ sensitivity is the single root cause of all four downstream effects.

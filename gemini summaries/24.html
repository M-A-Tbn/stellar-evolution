01

Classes of Variable Stars & Structural Properties

Stellar pulsations represent transient, non-hydrostatic phases in a star's evolutionary path where the star exhibits periodic changes in its external radius, effective surface temperature, and total integrated luminosity. The archetype variable star, $\delta$ Cephei, was discovered by John Goodricke in 1784, showing a rigorous periodic cycle of 5 days, 8 hours, and 48 minutes.

Pulsating variable stars are classified based on their spectral properties, evolutionary status, chemical composition (Population I vs. II), and structural oscillation profiles:

Variable Class Period Range Population Type Oscillation Mode
Classical Cepheids 1 -- 50 days Population I (Metal-Rich) Radial (Fundamental)
W Virginis Stars 2 -- 45 days Population II (Metal-Poor) Radial (Fundamental)
RR Lyrae Stars 1.5 -- 24 hours Population II (Old Systems) Radial (Fund. & 1st Overtone)
$\delta$ Scuti Stars 1 -- 3 hours Population I (Main Sequence) Radial & Non-Radial
$\beta$ Cephei Stars 3 -- 7 hours Population I (Blue Supergiants) Radial & Non-Radial
ZZ Ceti Stars 100 -- 1000 seconds Population I (White Dwarfs) Non-Radial Gravitational
Long Period Variables (LPVs/Miras) 100 -- 700 days Populations I & II (AGB Stars) Radial (Complex Shells)
02

Henrietta Leavitt's Discovery & Apparent-Absolute Scaling

The real historical turning point for variable star studies was conducted in the early 1900s by astronomer Henrietta Leavitt. Analyzing photographic plates taken at multiple epochs of the Small Magellanic Cloud (SMC)—an irregular dwarf satellite galaxy located at a distance of $d \approx 60\text{ kpc}$ from the Sun—she cataloged and derived period parameters for over 2,000 variable stars.

The Single-Distance Distance Disadvantage Lever

Because the physical depth of the Small Magellanic Cloud is tiny compared to its massive cosmological distance from the Solar System, Leavitt realized that all variable stars in her sample were located at approximately the same distance from Earth.

Therefore, any measured differences in apparent magnitude ($m_V$) mapped directly to differences in absolute magnitude ($M_V$). Since more luminous Cepheids consistently exhibited longer oscillation cycles, Leavitt discovered the fundamental Period-Luminosity (P-L) Relation:

$$M_V = -2.8 \log P_{\text{days}} - 1.43$$

(Empirically calibrated for Classical Cepheids by Sandage & Tammann, Ap. J., 151, 531, 1968)

By measuring a simple temporal parameter—the period ($P$) in days—astrophysicists can solve for a star's intrinsic luminosity. This relationship is the critical first rung of the **Cosmic Distance Ladder**, allowing us to compute distances across local Group galaxies and map the expanding Universe.

03

Radial Pulsations, Driving Variables, & Phase Lag

Harlow Shapley demonstrated in 1914 that variable star light curves are driven by physical **radial pulsations**—periodic expansions and contractions of the star's outer layers—rather than eclipsing binary stars.

Luminosity Driver: Effective Temperature vs. Radius

Stellar luminosity is parameterized by the Stefan-Boltzmann area-temperature relation: $$L = 4\pi R^2 \sigma T_{\text{eff}}^4$$ During a typical Cepheid cycle, the radius variation ($\Delta R/R_0$) is modest, usually capped around **10%**. However, the effective surface temperature ($T_{\text{eff}}$) oscillates sharply by **1000--1500 K** (shifting spectral classifications between F5 and G2).

Because luminosity scales with the fourth power of temperature ($T^4$) but only the second power of radius ($R^2$), temperature changes dominate the luminosity variation.

The Phase Lag Phenomenon

If the pulsation cycle were perfectly adiabatic, the maximum luminosity would coincide with the minimum radius ($R_{\text{min}}$) where density, pressure, and temperature peak.

However, observations show a distinct delay: the maximum luminosity occurs when the star is already expanding, shortly after it has passed $R_{\text{min}}$. This is known as the **Phase Lag**, and it is caused by the behavior of the stellar ionization zones as they store and release energy.

04

The Instability Strip Boundaries

Pulsating stars do not populate the entire H-R diagram. Instead, they are confined to a narrow, nearly vertical region known as the **Instability Strip** ($T_{\text{eff}} \approx 6000\text{--}7000\text{ K}$, width $\Delta T \approx 600\text{--}1000\text{ K}$).

The physical boundaries of this strip are determined by the location of the star's ionization zones:

The Blue Edge Boundary (Hot Limit)

In hot stars ($T_{\text{eff}} > 7500\text{ K}$), the Helium II ionization zone lies too close to the surface:

$M_{\text{above, HeII}} \to 0$

Because there is not enough mass in the overlying envelope to absorb and store the radiation, the ionization zone cannot drive global pulsations, setting a sharp hot limit.

The Red Edge Boundary (Cool Limit)

As the surface temperature drops below $6000\text{ K}$, the envelope becomes cool enough to trigger **convection**:

$\nabla_{\text{rad}} > \nabla_{\text{ad}} \implies \text{Convection}$

Because convective energy transport is highly turbulent, it efficiently disperses temperature and pressure variations, dampening the coherent waves needed to sustain pulsations.

05

Stellar Carnot Engines: Work Integrals & Thermodynamics

To sustain pulsations against dissipation, a layer of stellar gas must act as a **heat engine**, converting thermal energy into mechanical work over each cycle.

The Carnot Work Equation Derivation

Consider a stellar layer of mass $\Delta m$ that undergoes a complete, reversible cycle. Over a closed path, the net change in internal energy ($U$) and entropy ($S$) must be zero: $$\oint dU = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \oint dS = 0$$

Applying the First Law of Thermodynamics ($dU = dQ - dW$, where $dW = P dV$ is the mechanical work performed on the surrounding layers): $$\oint dU = \oint dQ - \oint dW = 0 \implies W = \oint dQ = \oint \left(\frac{dQ}{dt}\right) dt$$

We can model the instantaneous temperature of the layer as a small perturbation ($\delta T$) around its equilibrium value ($T_0$): $$T(t) = T_0 + \delta T(t) \quad \text{where } \delta T \ll T_0$$

Using a binomial expansion for $1/T$: $$\frac{1}{T(t)} = \frac{1}{T_0 + \delta T} = \frac{T_0 - \delta T}{T_0^2 - \delta T^2} \approx \frac{1}{T_0} \left(1 - \frac{\delta T}{T_0}\right)$$

Substituting this into the entropy equation ($\oint \frac{dQ}{T} = 0$): $$0 = \oint \frac{1}{T} \left(\frac{dQ}{dt}\right) dt \approx \frac{1}{T_0} \oint \left(1 - \frac{\delta T}{T_0}\right) \frac{dQ}{dt} dt$$ $$\implies \frac{1}{T_0} \oint \left(\frac{dQ}{dt}\right) dt = \frac{1}{T_0^2} \oint \delta T \left(\frac{dQ}{dt}\right) dt$$

Multiplying both sides by $T_0$ yields the expression for the net work ($W$) performed by the layer:

$$W = \oint \left(\frac{dQ}{dt}\right) dt = \frac{1}{T_0} \oint \left(\frac{dQ}{dt}\right) \delta T dt$$
The Driving Condition

For a stellar layer to do positive work ($W > 0$) and drive the pulsation, the integrand must be positive: $$\delta T \left(\frac{dQ}{dt}\right) > 0$$

This requires the layer to absorb heat ($\frac{dQ}{dt} > 0$) when it is hot ($\delta T > 0$, at maximum compression) and release heat ($\frac{dQ}{dt} < 0$) when it is cool ($\delta T < 0$, at maximum expansion).

06

The physical Engine: The $\kappa$ and $\gamma$ Mechanisms

In normal stellar layers, radiative energy transport acts as a dampener. Under standard conditions, opacity drops when the temperature rises: $$\kappa_{\text{Kramer}} \propto \rho T^{-3.5}$$ During compression, the temperature increases, causing the opacity to drop. This allows radiative energy to escape easily, cooling the gas and dampening any oscillations.

The Ionization Valve

This behavior shifts in regions of **partial ionization**. Under these conditions, the gas acts as a **thermal valve**:

The $\kappa$-Mechanism (Opacity Valve)

During compression, the temperature rise increases the ionization fraction of the gas rather than its thermal energy. This increased ionization raises the free-free and bound-free opacity ($\delta \kappa > 0$), trapping heat inside the layer.

The $\gamma$-Mechanism (Heat Capacity)

Because some of the compression energy goes into ionizing atoms rather than increasing kinetic motion, the temperature rise ($\delta T$) is smaller than expected. This lower temperature creates a steep thermal gradient between the zone and the deeper, hotter layers, causing heat to flow *into* the layer and further drive the pulsation.

The Ionization Engine Zones

  • Hydrogen Ionization Zone ($HI \to HII$): Located near the surface where $T \approx 1.5 \times 10^4\text{ K}$. This zone behaves like a sponge, absorbing heat and delaying the escape of radiation to create the observed **Phase Lag**.
  • Helium II Ionization Zone ($HeII \to HeIII$): Located deeper in the envelope where $T \approx 4 \times 10^4\text{ K}$. This zone contains enough mass to act as the primary **engine** driving the pulsations.
07

Analytical Proof of the Period-Density Relation

Because stellar pulsations behave like **standing sound waves** trapped within a resonant cavity, the oscillation period ($\Pi$) is proportional to the time required for a sound wave to travel across the star's diameter: $$\Pi \approx 2 \int_{0}^{R} \frac{dr}{v_s}$$

1. Deriving Sound Velocity ($v_s$) from Adiabatic Gas Laws

The sound speed in a gas depends on its compressibility (the Bulk Modulus, $B$) and its inertia (density, $\rho$): $$v_s = \sqrt{\frac{B}{\rho}} \quad \text{where } B = -V \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial V}\right)_{\text{ad}}$$

For an adiabatic process ($dQ = 0$): $$dU = -P dV$$ Since $U = c_v T$, the change in internal energy is $dU = c_v dT$. Substituting this into the first law yields: $$c_v dT + P dV = 0 \quad \text{--- (Eq 2)}$$

From the perfect gas equation of state ($PV = n R T$), differentiating both sides gives: $$P dV + V dP = n R dT \implies dT = \frac{P dV + V dP}{nR}$$

Substituting $dT$ back into Equation 2, and using the relations $nR = c_p - c_v$ and $\gamma = c_p/c_v$: $$c_v \left(\frac{P dV + V dP}{c_p - c_v}\right) + P dV = 0 \implies c_v (P dV + V dP) + (c_p - c_v) P dV = 0$$ $$c_v V dP + c_p P dV = 0 \implies \frac{dP}{P} + \gamma \frac{dV}{V} = 0 \implies P V^\gamma = \text{const}$$

By differentiating $P = \text{const} \cdot V^{-\gamma}$: $$\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial V}\right)_{\text{ad}} = -\gamma \frac{P}{V} \implies B = -V \left(-\gamma \frac{P}{V}\right) = \gamma P$$

This yields the classic expression for the speed of sound: $$v_s = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P}{\rho}}$$

2. Integrating Across the Stellar Radius

To find the pressure profile, we integrate the equation of hydrostatic equilibrium assuming a uniform average density $\rho$: $$\frac{dP}{dr} = -\frac{G M_r \rho}{r^2} = -\frac{G \left(\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \rho\right) \rho}{r^2} = -\frac{4}{3}\pi G \rho^2 r$$ Integrating from $r$ to the surface radius $R$ (where surface pressure $P(R) = 0$): $$P(r) = \frac{2}{3} \pi G \rho^2 (R^2 - r^2)$$

Substituting this pressure profile into the sound speed equation: $$v_s(r) = \sqrt{\frac{\gamma P}{\rho}} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}\pi \gamma G \rho (R^2 - r^2)}$$

Now, we substitute $v_s(r)$ back into our period integral: $$\Pi \approx 2 \int_{0}^{R} \frac{dr}{\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}\pi \gamma G \rho R^2 \left(1 - \frac{r^2}{R^2}\right)}} = 2 \sqrt{\frac{3}{2\pi \gamma G \rho}} \frac{1}{R} \int_{0}^{R} \frac{dr}{\sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{r}{R}\right)^2}}$$

By substituting $x = r/R$ (where $dr = R dx$): $$\Pi \approx 2 \sqrt{\frac{3}{2\pi \gamma G \rho}} \int_{0}^{1} \frac{dx}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} = 2 \sqrt{\frac{3}{2\pi \gamma G \rho}} \left[\arcsin(x)\right]_{0}^{1}$$ Since $\arcsin(1) - \arcsin(0) = \pi/2$, we obtain:

$$\Pi \approx \sqrt{\frac{3\pi}{2 \gamma G \rho}} \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{\bar{\rho}}}$$

This proves that the pulsation period is inversely proportional to the square root of the star's average density. The observed Period-Luminosity relation is simply the projection of this Period-Density relation onto the H-R diagram, where highly luminous giant stars have much lower densities and therefore exhibit longer pulsation periods.

08

The One-Zone Stellar Toy Model Proof

To confirm the Period-Density relation, we can model the star's envelope as a single, thin shell of mass $m$ at radius $R$, enclosing a central core of mass $M$ that exerts a pressure $P$.

The Non-Equilibrium Equation of Motion

The movement of the outer shell is governed by the balance of gravity and pressure forces: $$m \frac{d^2 R}{dt^2} = -\frac{G M m}{R^2} + 4\pi R^2 P \quad \text{--- (Eq 3)}$$

At equilibrium ($R = R_0$, $P = P_0$):

$$\frac{G M m}{R_0^2} = 4\pi R_0^2 P_0 \implies \frac{GM}{R_0^3} = \frac{4\pi P_0}{\rho_0} \quad (\text{since } m_s \text{ is massless})$$

Linearization of the Equations

We introduce small perturbations around the equilibrium state:

$$R = R_0 + \delta R \quad \text{and} \quad P = P_0 + \delta P \quad \text{where } \delta R \ll R_0, \quad \delta P \ll P_0$$

Substituting these back into Equation (3):

$$m \frac{d^2}{dt^2}(R_0 + \delta R) = -G\frac{Mm}{(R_0 + \delta R)^2} + 4\pi (R_0 + \delta R)^2 (P_0 + \delta P)$$

We simplify the gravity term using a binomial expansion, neglecting terms of order $(\delta R/R_0)^2$ and higher:

$$\frac{1}{(R_0 + \delta R)^2} = \frac{1}{R_0^2 \left(1 + \frac{\delta R}{R_0}\right)^2} \approx \frac{1}{R_0^2} \left(1 - 2\frac{\delta R}{R_0}\right)$$

Similarly, expanding the pressure term and neglecting second-order perturbations like $\delta R \delta P$ and $\delta R^2$:

$$(R_0 + \delta R)^2 (P_0 + \delta P) \approx R_0^2 P_0 + 2 R_0 P_0 \delta R + R_0^2 \delta P$$

Substituting these approximations back into the equation of motion:

$$m \frac{d^2(\delta R)}{dt^2} \approx -\frac{G M m}{R_0^2} + \frac{2 G M m}{R_0^3}\delta R + 4\pi R_0^2 P_0 + 8\pi R_0 P_0 \delta R + 4\pi R_0^2 \delta P$$

Using the equilibrium relation, the zero-order terms cancel out. This simplifies the equation to:

$$m \frac{d^2(\delta R)}{dt^2} = 2 G \frac{Mm \delta R}{R_0^3} + 8\pi R_0 P_0 \delta R + 4\pi R_0^2 \delta P \quad \text{--- (Equation A)}$$

Substituting the Adiabatic Index

For an adiabatic process in a spherical volume ($V = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3$):

$$P V^\gamma = \text{const} \implies P R^{3\gamma} = \text{const}$$

Taking the logarithm and differentiating yields:

$$\frac{\delta P}{P_0} + 3\gamma \frac{\delta R}{R_0} = 0 \implies \delta P = -3\gamma \frac{P_0}{R_0} \delta R$$

Substituting $\delta P$ into Equation (A) and using $G \frac{Mm}{R_0^3} = 4\pi R_0 P_0$:

$$m \frac{d^2(\delta R)}{dt^2} = \left( 2 \frac{G M m}{R_0^3} + 2 \frac{G M m}{R_0^3} - 3\gamma \frac{G M m}{R_{0}^3} \right) \delta R$$

Simplifying the equation allows us to cancel the shell mass $m$, leaving:

$$\frac{d^2(\delta R)}{dt^2} = -(3\gamma - 4) \frac{GM}{R_0^3} \delta R$$
The Dynamic Stability Criterion

This differential equation describes **simple harmonic motion** of the form $\ddot{X} = -\omega^2 X$, where: $$\omega^2 = (3\gamma - 4)\frac{GM}{R_0^3}$$

  • Case I: $\gamma < 4/3$ ($\omega^2 < 0$): The solution has the form $\delta R(t) \propto e^{\omega t}$, which grows exponentially over time. This makes the star dynamically unstable, leading to collapse or disruption.
  • Case II: $\gamma > 4/3$ ($\omega^2 > 0$): The solution is a stable, periodic oscillation: $\delta R(t) = A \sin(\omega t)$.

For a standard monoatomic gas ($\gamma = 5/3$), the system is highly stable against collapse, oscillating with a period of: $$\Pi = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = \frac{2\pi}{\sqrt{\frac{4}{3}\pi G \rho_0 (3\gamma - 4)}}$$

Exam Trap: $\Gamma_1 = 4/3$ Is Not Just a Toy-Model Curiosity

Do not file the $\gamma > 4/3$ result away as a one-zone-model footnote. The same threshold governs real dynamical stability whenever the average adiabatic index $\Gamma_1$ of a star's envelope is pushed toward $4/3$ by physical processes other than a simple monoatomic ideal gas:

  • Radiation-pressure-dominated massive stars: as $\beta = P_{\text{gas}}/P_{\text{total}} \to 0$, the effective $\Gamma_1 \to 4/3$ from above, so extremely luminous stars sit close to the stability boundary "for free" — this is why very massive stars are pulsationally and dynamically marginal even without invoking partial ionization.
  • Pair-instability in very massive cores: at $T \sim 10^9\,\text{K}$, photon pair production ($\gamma\gamma \to e^+e^-$) silently converts thermal/radiation energy into rest mass, softening the equation of state so that $\Gamma_1$ drops below $4/3$. The star then sits in Case I above ($\omega^2 < 0$): $\delta R(t) \propto e^{\omega t}$ growth — i.e. catastrophic dynamical collapse, governed by the exact same mathematics as this one-zone model, not a separate mechanism.

Exam-trap wording to remember: "$\Gamma_1 > 4/3$ everywhere in the star" is the general dynamical-stability requirement — the one-zone $\gamma = 5/3$ monoatomic case is just the safest possible example of it, not the definition of it.

Derivation of the Period-Luminosity-Color (PLC) Relation

To find the absolute magnitude ($M_V$) of a pulsating star, we must convert the Period-Density relation ($\Pi \propto \rho^{-1/2}$) into observable quantities.

1. Expressing Density via Luminosity and Temperature

Starting with the definition of average density ($\rho \propto M R^{-3}$):

$$\log P = -\frac{1}{2} \log \rho + C = -\frac{1}{2}\log M + \frac{3}{2}\log R + C$$

Using the Stefan-Boltzmann equation ($L \propto R^2 T^4 \implies R \propto L^{1/2} T^{-2}$):

$$\log P = -\frac{1}{2} \log M + \frac{3}{2}\left(\frac{1}{2}\log L - 2 \log T_{\text{eff}}\right) + C$$ $$\log P = -\frac{1}{2} \log M + \frac{3}{4}\log L - 3 \log T_{\text{eff}} + C$$

Expressing this relation in solar units yields:

$$\log P = -\frac{1}{2} \log\left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right) - 3\log\left(\frac{T_{\text{eff}}}{T_{\odot}}\right) - 0.3 \left(M_{\text{bol}} - M_{\text{bol}}^\odot\right) - 1.294$$

2. Removing Stellar Mass using ZAMS Scaling

Stellar masses cannot be measured directly for individual distant stars. However, we can use mass-luminosity scaling relations from the ZAMS: $$M_{\text{bol}} = 3.96 - 8.22 \log\left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right)$$ Because massive stars ($5\text{--}10 M_\odot$) cross the instability strip during Helium core burning at luminosities roughly **1 magnitude brighter** than their ZAMS values, we can write: $$M_{\text{bol}}^{\text{He-C}} \approx 2.96 - 8.22 \log\left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right) \implies -\frac{1}{2}\log\left(\frac{M}{M_\odot}\right) \approx 0.06 \left(M_{\text{bol}} - 3\right)$$

Substituting this mass relation back into the period equation (with $M_{\text{bol}}^\odot = 4.75$):

$$\log P = -3 \log\left(\frac{T_{\text{eff}}}{T_{\odot}}\right) - 0.24 M_{\text{bol}} - 0.049$$

3. Converting to Observable Magnitudes and Colors

We use standard calibrations to convert bolometric magnitude and effective temperature to the observable $V$-band magnitude and $(B-V)$ color index:

$$M_{\text{bol}} = M_V + 0.145 - 0.322(B-V)$$
$$\log T_{\text{eff}} = 3.886 - 0.175(B-V)$$

Substituting these relations into the period equation yields the **Period-Luminosity-Color (PLC)** relation:

$$M_V = -3.53 \log P_{\text{days}} - 2.13 + 2.13(B-V)$$

RR Lyrae Subtypes & Metallicity Dependencies

In old stellar populations (globular clusters and galactic halos), low-mass helium-burning stars populate the Horizontal Branch. Those crossing the instability strip are observed as RR Lyrae stars.

Pulsational Subtypes: RRab vs. RRc

RRab Variables (Fundamental Mode)

These cooler stars oscillate in the fundamental radial mode. They are characterized by larger pulsation amplitudes (**0.8--1.6 mag**) and longer periods:

$$P \approx 0.4\text{ to }0.8\text{ days}$$
RRc Variables (First Overtone)

These hotter stars oscillate in the first overtone radial mode. They exhibit smaller amplitudes (**0.5--0.8 mag**) and shorter periods:

$$P \approx 0.25\text{ to }0.4\text{ days}$$

The Metallicity-Luminosity Scaling Relation

Because RR Lyrae stars are old stars with similar core masses ($\approx 0.5 M_\odot$), their absolute magnitude is relatively constant, making them excellent standard candles. However, their luminosity depends on metallicity ($Z$), which affects envelope opacity:

$$M_V = 0.32 [Fe/H] + 1.06$$

Metal-rich stars start their evolution from the ZAMS with larger masses. Because they reach the Horizontal Branch with a thicker envelope (lower fractional core mass $q = M_c/M_E$), they populate the cooler, redder side of the instability strip, often bypassing the variable region entirely.

The "Second Parameter" Problem

Metallicity alone does not fully determine whether a cluster hosts RR Lyrae. Two globular clusters with nearly identical $[Fe/H]$ can show dramatically different Horizontal Branch morphologies — one entirely blue, one entirely red, one crossing the strip and rich in RR Lyrae. Since $[Fe/H]$ is fixed between the two, something else — a "second parameter" — must be shaping the HB.

Candidate second parameters debated in the literature include:

  • Cluster age: older clusters have lower-mass stars leaving the Main Sequence, which land on a bluer part of the HB.
  • Helium abundance ($Y$): higher He content lowers the envelope mass at the He-flash, again pushing stars blueward.
  • Mass loss on the RGB: more efficient mass loss thins the envelope further, shifting the HB blue independent of composition.

This is a purely evolutionary/compositional effect — it governs where on the HB a star lands, not the pulsation driving mechanism itself. Whether that star then happens to fall inside the instability strip (and is observed as an RR Lyrae) is a downstream consequence of this "second parameter," not a separate physical process.

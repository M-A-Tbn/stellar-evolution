01

Helium Ignition Mass Cutoffs & Degeneracy Cutoffs

Helium core ignition represents a critical phase shift in stellar astrophysics. The conditions required to trigger the central triple-alpha ($3\alpha$) reaction depend strictly on the initial stellar mass $M$.

Unlike Hydrogen burning, which occurs gradually along the Main Sequence, the ignition of Helium is heavily affected by whether the Helium ash core reaches degenerate or perfect gas conditions first. The evolutionary cutoffs are parameterized as follows:

Low Mass Regime ($M < 0.5 M_\odot$)

These stars do not possess the gravitational potential energy needed to heat their degenerate Helium cores to the critical threshold:

$T_c < 8 \times 10^7\text{ K}$

Because their cores never reach the ignition temperature of the $3\alpha$ reaction, they bypass subsequent burning phases entirely. They end their evolution as degenerate, non-burning **Helium White Dwarfs (He-WD)**.

Intermediate Low Mass ($0.5 M_\odot < M < 2.2 M_\odot$)

The degenerate core reaches a critical threshold mass of $M_{\text{core}} \approx 0.5 M_\odot$ at the Red Giant Branch (RGB) tip.

$M_{\text{core}} \approx 0.5 M_\odot \implies \text{Helium Flash}$

This degenerate state decouples thermal pressure from temperature, triggering an explosive runaway known as the **Helium Flash**. Once degeneracy is lifted, they settle down to burn Helium stably, ending as **Carbon-Oxygen White Dwarfs (CO-WD)**.

For stars with initial masses in the range $2.2 M_\odot < M < 8 M_\odot$, the Helium core remains non-degenerate upon Hydrogen exhaustion. Helium is ignited gradually and stably under perfect gas conditions. Despite these different ignition paths, both ranges ($0.5 M_\odot < M < 8 M_\odot$) eventually end their lives as degenerate **Carbon-Oxygen White Dwarfs (CO-WDs)**.

02

Physical Mechanics of the Triple-Alpha Reaction

Helium burning always occurs through the **triple-alpha ($3\alpha$) process**, regardless of the star's initial mass. This nucleosynthetic chain must overcome a severe stability bottleneck because no stable isotope of atomic mass $A=5$ or $A=8$ exists in nature.

The Triple-Alpha Sequence

The reaction proceeds through a rapid, two-step resonance capture process:

$$\text{Step 1: } \quad {^4\text{He}} + {^4\text{He}} \rightleftharpoons {^8\text{Be}} \quad (\Delta E \approx 92 \text{ keV})$$
$$\text{Step 2: } \quad {^8\text{Be}} + {^4\text{He}} \rightarrow {^{12}\text{C}} + \gamma \quad (\text{via the Hoyle State } 7.65 \text{ MeV})$$
Nuclear Kinetic Bottleneck

The primary obstacle is the extremely short lifetime of Beryllium-8 ($^{8}\text{Be}$): $$\tau_{{^8\text{Be}}} \approx 10^{-17} \text{ seconds}$$ Because $^{8}\text{Be}$ decays back into two alpha particles almost instantly, a tiny, steady-state abundance of Beryllium-8 must be maintained in thermal equilibrium. This requires very high central densities ($\rho_c \sim 10^4\text{ to }10^5 \text{ g cm}^{-3}$) and temperatures ($T \ge 10^8\text{ K}$) for a third alpha particle to collide with Beryllium-8 before it decays.

Because of this multi-body collision process, the energy generation rate of the triple-alpha process ($\epsilon_{3\alpha}$) is extremely sensitive to the temperature: $$\epsilon_{3\alpha} \propto \rho^2 Y^3 T^{25}$$ This incredibly high temperature sensitivity ($\propto T^{25}$) drives both core convection and the explosive nature of the runaway core Helium Flash in degenerate stars.

03

Helium Ignition: Core Expansion & Shell De-energization

When Helium is ignited in the core (at Point 6, the RGB Tip), it has a profound effect on the surrounding layers. Regardless of whether ignition occurs via a Helium Flash (degenerate cores) or gradually (non-degenerate cores), the result is the same: **the core expands dramatically, and the star's overall luminosity drops**.

The Thermodynamics of Expansion

The massive energy injection from the triple-alpha process heats the core:

  • In low-mass stars, the flash lifts core degeneracy. Once degeneracy is removed, the core behaves as a perfect gas again.
  • In massive stars, perfect gas rules apply from the start. Convection carries the extra energy, expanding the core to restore thermal equilibrium.
The Core-Shell Coupling Principle

As the Helium core expands, its boundary moves outward in radius. This pushes the surrounding, very thin Hydrogen-burning shell into cooler, lower-density regions.

Because the Hydrogen shell's energy generation rate is highly temperature-sensitive, this expansion cools the shell, reducing its efficiency and energy output. As a direct result, the star's overall luminosity ($L$) drops well below the high value it reached at the RGB Tip.

Parameter At the RGB Tip (Flash Ignition) On the Horizontal Branch (Stable)
Core Density ($\rho_c$) $\approx 10^6 \text{ g cm}^{-3}$ (Degenerate) $\approx 10^4 \text{ g cm}^{-3}$ (Ideal Gas)
Core Temperature ($T_c$) $\approx 8 \times 10^7 \text{ K}$ $\approx 10^8 \text{ K}$
Stellar Luminosity ($L$) $L \approx 10^3\text{ to }10^{3.5} L_\odot$ (Peak) $L \approx 50 \text{ to } 100 L_\odot$ (Reduced)
04

The Zero Age Horizontal Branch (ZAHB) Starting Line

Once core de-degeneracy is complete and Helium burning becomes stable, the star settles onto the **Zero Age Horizontal Branch (ZAHB)**.

Just as the Zero Age Main Sequence (ZAMS) marks the starting line where stars of different masses begin stable core Hydrogen burning, the ZAHB represents the starting line where stars begin **stable core Helium burning** in non-degenerate conditions.

The Structuring Mass Parameter $q$

While a star's position on the ZAMS is determined entirely by its initial mass, its position on the ZAHB depends on a more complex structural parameter, the **fractional core mass ($q$)**: $$q = \frac{M_c}{M_E}$$ where:

  • $M_c$ is the Helium core mass (which is highly uniform at $M_c \approx 0.5 M_\odot$ for all stars that undergo the Helium Flash).
  • $M_E$ is the **evolutionary mass** of the star at the moment of stable core Helium ignition: $$M_E = M_{\text{ZAMS}} - M_{\text{ML}}$$ where $M_{\text{ML}}$ represents the mass lost via stellar winds while ascending the Red Giant Branch.

Because $M_c$ is essentially constant ($\approx 0.5 M_\odot$) for all low-mass stars, **the value of $q$ is determined entirely by the evolutionary mass $M_E$**. This relationship shapes the observed temperature distribution of stars across the Horizontal Branch.

05

Horizontal Branch Morphology & Second Parameter Dilemmas

The most striking characteristic of Helium-burning stars is the wide range of surface temperatures ($T_{\text{eff}}$) they can reach, which shapes the Horizontal Branch in the Color-Magnitude Diagram (CMD).

The Envelope Mass Effect

With a constant core mass of $M_c \approx 0.5 M_\odot$, the mass of the overlying Hydrogen envelope determines the star's surface temperature:

  • Large Envelope ($M_E \ge 0.7 M_\odot \implies q \le 0.7$): The star has a thick, extended envelope that cushions the core's heat, resulting in lower surface temperatures. The star remains red, populating the **Red HB / Red Clump**.
  • Small Envelope ($M_E \approx 0.51 M_\odot \implies q \ge 0.98$): The star has a very thin Hydrogen envelope, exposing the hot core. This shifts the star to high surface temperatures, populating the **Blue HB** or **Extreme HB (EHB)**.

Extreme HB (EHB) Stars & Galactic "UV Upturn"

Extreme HB stars have lost almost their entire Hydrogen envelope ($M_E \approx M_c \approx 0.5 M_\odot$). Because their envelopes are too thin to support Hydrogen shell burning, they bypass the Asymptotic Giant Branch phase entirely, a phenomenon known as AGB Manqué.

These hot, blue stars release significant ultraviolet radiation. In old elliptical galaxies and galactic bulges—which contain very old stellar populations with no active star formation—EHB stars are the primary source of the observed excess UV emission, known as the UV Upturn (Greggio & Renzini 1990).

The Parameter Matrix of HB Morphology

The distribution of stars along the Horizontal Branch depends on several factors that affect the envelope mass and the Main Sequence Turn-Off (MS-TO) mass:

Parameter Shift Physical Effect on Star HB Morphology Output
Increasing Age Decreases MS-TO mass → smaller envelope at the RGB tip BLUE HB
Increasing Metallicity Increases envelope opacity (Kramers' Law) → keeps envelope expanded RED HB
Increasing Helium ($Y$) Decreases MS-TO mass → smaller envelope at the RGB tip BLUE HB
Increasing Mass Loss Directly reduces envelope mass ($M_E \approx M_{\text{core}}$) BLUE HB

Note: The conflict between these factors—such as clusters with identical ages and metallicities displaying vastly different HB morphologies (e.g., NGC 6388 vs 47 Tuc/NGC 104)—is known as the **Second Parameter Problem**.

06

Core Convection & The Self-Driving Opacity Loop

During stable Helium burning, the high temperature sensitivity of the triple-alpha process ($\epsilon_{3\alpha} \propto T^{25}$) drives the creation of a **convective core**.

However, the behavior of this convective core is very different from that of Hydrogen-burning stars:

Hydrogen-Burning Core (Decreases)

The transformation of Hydrogen to Helium reduces the number of particles and the opacity of the core:

$$\kappa_{\text{FF}} \propto (1+X) \implies X \downarrow \implies \kappa \downarrow$$

As opacity drops, radiative heat transport becomes more efficient. The radiative gradient ($\nabla_{\text{rad}}$) decreases, causing the **convective core to shrink over time**.

Helium-Burning Core (Increases)

The conversion of Helium to Carbon and Oxygen increases core opacity due to heavier, highly charged ions:

$$\kappa_{\text{FF}} \propto Z^2 \implies Z \uparrow \implies \kappa \uparrow$$

The heavier nuclei increase free-free opacity, making radiative transport less efficient. This increases the radiative gradient ($\nabla_{\text{rad}} \uparrow$), causing the **convective core to expand outward over time** (the self-driving convective loop).

Convective Boundary Overshooting

According to the standard **Schwarzschild criterion** ($\nabla_{\text{rad}} = \nabla_{\text{ad}}$), convection stops where the acceleration of convective gas cells drops to zero.

However, because these gas cells have non-zero momentum when they reach this boundary, they overshoot into the surrounding stable radiative zone. This **convective overshooting ($\Lambda_{\text{ov}}$)** mixes fresh Helium fuel into the core, extending the Helium-burning lifetime of the star.

07

The Thermodynamics of Semiconvection

The complex interplay between opacity, density, and temperature near the convective core boundary can prevent a clean division between convective and radiative zones. This gives rise to a partially mixed region known as the **semiconvective zone**.

The Development of Semiconvection

As the core converts Helium to Carbon and Oxygen, the central radiative gradient ($\nabla_{\text{rad}}$) rises. However, $\nabla_{\text{rad}}$ is not monotonic; it exhibits a minimum near the outer edge of the core.

The Physical Sequence of Semiconvection

  • Overshooting carries carbon- and oxygen-rich material outward, increasing opacity and raising the radiative gradient in the outer layers.
  • As opacity drops in the outer regions due to the influx of fresh Helium, the radiative gradient falls to match the adiabatic gradient ($\nabla_{\text{rad}} \approx \nabla_{\text{ad}}$).
  • This splits the convective region into two separate zones: a **fully convective inner core** and a **partially mixed outer semiconvective region** that is not completely mixed.

Accounting for this semiconvective region in stellar models is essential: it introduces fresh Helium fuel into the core over time, creating a more pronounced loop toward hotter surface temperatures in the HR diagram and significantly extending the core Helium-burning lifetime.

08

The Asymptotic Giant Branch (AGB) Double-Shell Structure

Once Helium is completely depleted in the convective core, the thermonuclear reactions die out. The core, now composed of Carbon and Oxygen (with $^{16}\text{O}$ being more abundant than $^{12}\text{C}$ due to alpha captures, $^{12}\text{C} + \alpha \rightarrow {^{16}\text{O}} + \gamma$), begins to contract.

This contraction heats the surrounding layers, initiating the **Double-Shell Burning Phase** on the **Asymptotic Giant Branch (AGB)**.

Fig 3: Double-shell burning structure of an AGB star.

The Transition Mass $M^{\text{up}} \approx 8 M_\odot$

The subsequent evolution of the star is determined by whether the newly formed Carbon-Oxygen core becomes degenerate:

  • $M < M^{\text{up}} \approx 8 M_\odot$: The core becomes degenerate. Degeneracy pressure supports the core, halting further contraction and temperature rises. The star is limited to the AGB phase and eventually ejects its envelope, ending as a **Carbon-Oxygen White Dwarf**.
  • $M > M^{\text{up}} \approx 8 M_\odot$: The core remains non-degenerate. It continues to contract and heat up, eventually igniting Carbon, Neon, Oxygen, and Silicon burning, culminating in an Iron core and a **Type II Core-Collapse Supernova**.
The Second Dredge-Up (Early-AGB Mixing Event)

Immediately after core Helium exhaustion — before the star enters the Thermally-Pulsing AGB phase described in Section 9 — the outer convective envelope deepens once more as the star ascends the Hayashi Track for a second time. This is the Second Dredge-Up, distinct from both the First Dredge-Up (which occurs on the RGB) and the Third Dredge-Up (which occurs repeatedly during thermal pulses).

Unlike the universal First Dredge-Up, the Second Dredge-Up only penetrates deep enough to reach CN-processed material in stars with $M > 4$ to $5\, M_\odot$. In these intermediate/high-mass AGB stars, it carries the products of earlier H-shell burning (elevated $^{4}\text{He}$ and $^{14}\text{N}$) to the surface, further altering surface composition ahead of the s-process and HBB pollution discussed in Section 10.

Do not conflate this event with the Third Dredge-Up: the Second Dredge-Up is a single, one-time mixing episode marking entry onto the E-AGB, while the Third Dredge-Up recurs after every individual thermal pulse.

09

Thin-Shell Instabilities & The Physics of Thermal Pulses

The advanced AGB phase (the **Thermally-Pulsing AGB**, or **TP-AGB**) is characterized by the alternate ignition and extinction of the Hydrogen- and Helium-burning shells.

The Math of Thin-Shell Instability

In a stable, non-degenerate star, an increase in temperature increases pressure, causing the star to expand and cool back to equilibrium.

However, inside a very thin burning shell (of thickness $s$ located at radius $r$), this regulation mechanism can fail. By differentiating the gas state equation:

$$\frac{dP}{P} = \alpha \frac{d\rho}{\rho} + \beta \frac{dT}{T}$$

For a thin shell where $s \ll r$, the change in pressure during expansion is related to the change in density by:

$$\frac{dP}{P} = \left(\frac{4s}{r}\right) \frac{d\rho}{\rho}$$

Substituting this back into the state equation yields:

$$\frac{d\rho}{\rho} \left(\frac{4s}{r} - \alpha\right) = \beta \frac{dT}{T}$$
Thermonuclear Runaway Case

If the shell is extremely thin such that: $$\frac{4s}{r} < \alpha \approx 1 \implies \left(\frac{4s}{r} - \alpha\right) < 0$$

Under these conditions, a decrease in density ($d\rho < 0$, expansion) mathematically forces an increase in temperature ($dT > 0$). This positive feedback loop triggers a thermonuclear runaway, resulting in a **Thermal Pulse** ($L_{\text{intershell}} \sim 10^6\text{ to }10^7 L_\odot$).

Interpulse Period (Low-Mass Core, $0.6\,M_\odot$) $\approx 2\times10^5$ yr
Interpulse Period (High-Mass Core, $5\,M_\odot$) $\approx 10^3$ yr

The quiescent interval between successive thermal pulses shrinks sharply with increasing core mass — more massive AGB cores burn through their intershell Helium reservoir, and therefore cycle through pulses, far more rapidly.

The Third Dredge-Up & Carbon Stars

The energy released during a thermal pulse expands the entire intershell region, cooling and turning off the Hydrogen-burning shell. Convection then develops in the intershell zone, mixing the products of Helium burning (mainly $^{12}\text{C}$) outward.

As the outer convective envelope expands and cools, it penetrates deep into the intershell region. This is the **Third Dredge-Up**, which carries Carbon ($^{12}\text{C}$) directly to the stellar surface. Repeating this process can enrich the surface in Carbon until its abundance exceeds that of Oxygen ($C/O > 1$), forming a **Carbon Star**.

10

The s-Process & Hot Bottom Burning (HBB) Nucleosynthesis

AGB stars are important sites of stellar nucleosynthesis, producing heavy elements via **slow neutron capture (the s-process)** and altering light element abundances via **Hot Bottom Burning (HBB)**.

1. The s-Process & Neutron Sources

The s-process forms heavy elements (such as Barium, Zirconium, and Yttrium) through the capture of free neutrons by iron seed nuclei. Because free neutrons are unstable and decay in about 15 minutes, they must be produced **locally and simultaneously** in the Helium-burning layers. Two reaction chains serve as neutron sources:

  • The $^{22}\text{Ne}(\alpha, n)^{25}\text{Mg}$ Source: Occurs in massive AGB stars ($M > 3 M_\odot$) where temperatures reach $T \ge 3.5 \times 10^8\text{ K}$: $${^{14}\text{N}}(\alpha, \gamma){^{18}\text{F}}(\beta^+){^{18}\text{O}}(\alpha, \gamma){^{22}\text{Ne}}(\alpha, n){^{25}\text{Mg}}$$
  • The $^{13}\text{C}(\alpha, n)^{16}\text{O}$ Source: Operates at lower temperatures ($T \approx 9 \times 10^7\text{ K}$) in lower-mass AGB stars. This source requires a small amount of Hydrogen to be mixed into the Carbon-rich core boundary, initiating: $${^{12}\text{C}}(p, \gamma){^{13}\text{N}}(\beta^+){^{13}\text{C}}(\alpha, n){^{16}\text{O}}$$
Exam Trap: The $^{14}\text{N}$ Neutron Poison

Not every free neutron produced by the sources above goes on to build heavy s-process elements. $^{14}\text{N}$ — left over from earlier CNO-cycle H-burning — has an unusually large neutron-capture cross-section:

$${^{14}\text{N}}(n,\gamma){^{15}\text{N}}$$

Because $^{14}\text{N}$ competes directly with the Fe-group seed nuclei for available neutrons, it is termed a neutron poison: its presence suppresses s-process efficiency unless it is first destroyed or converted away. This is precisely why the two neutron sources are not interchangeable — the $^{22}\text{Ne}(\alpha,n)^{25}\text{Mg}$ source is effective specifically because it operates in a He-rich, $^{14}\text{N}$-depleted zone (the $^{14}\text{N}$ having already been consumed to make the $^{22}\text{Ne}$ itself), while the $^{13}\text{C}(\alpha,n)^{16}\text{O}$ pocket must be kept physically separate from any residual $^{14}\text{N}$ to remain efficient.

2. Hot Bottom Burning (HBB)

In massive AGB stars ($M \ge 6\text{ to }7 M_\odot$), the base of the outer convective envelope reaches temperatures of:

$$T_{\text{base}} \ge 8 \times 10^7\text{ K}$$

At this temperature, Hydrogen fusion (the CNO cycle) occurs directly at the base of the convective envelope. This has several important effects:

  • No Carbon Star Formation: Newly dredged-up Carbon is rapidly converted to Nitrogen ($^{12}\text{C} \rightarrow {^{14}\text{N}}$), preventing the star from becoming a Carbon star.
  • The Cameron-Fowler Lithium Mechanism: Helium-3 is converted to Beryllium-7, which is quickly transported by convective currents to cooler outer layers ($T < 2 \times 10^6\text{ K}$) before it can capture a proton. There, it undergoes electron capture to form Lithium-7: $${^3\text{He}}(\alpha, \gamma){^7\text{Be}}(e^-, \nu){^7\text{Li}}$$ This explains the anomalously high Lithium abundances observed in bright AGB stars (Long Period Variables).
  • High-Temperature Proton-Capture Cycles: HBB initiates the Ne-Na and Mg-Al cycles:
    Ne-Na Cycle ($T > 4\times 10^7$ K):

    ${^{20}\text{Ne}} \rightarrow {^{23}\text{Na}}$ (O-Na anticorrelation)

    Mg-Al Cycle ($T > 7\times 10^7$ K):

    ${^{24}\text{Mg}} \rightarrow {^{27}\text{Al}}$ (Mg-Al anticorrelation)

Active Recall & Focus Quiz

Validate your retention of the physical concepts before your exam.

Question 1

What is the final evolutionary fate of stars with initial masses under $0.5 M_\odot$?

Correct! Because these low-mass stars cannot compress their cores enough to heat them to $T_c \approx 8 \times 10^7\text{ K}$, Helium never ignites.

Question 2

Why is Beryllium-8 a bottleneck in the triple-alpha reaction chain?

Correct! The extremely short decay lifetime of $^{8}\text{Be}$ means that a high collision rate (high density and temperature) is required to build a steady-state abundance of Beryllium-8.

Question 3

What structural change causes a star's luminosity to decrease after Helium core ignition?

Correct! The expansion of the core cools and de-energizes the Hydrogen-burning shell, which reduces the star's overall luminosity.

Question 4

How does a smaller Hydrogen envelope mass affect a star's position on the ZAHB?

Correct! A smaller Hydrogen envelope exposes more of the hot Helium core, shifting the star to higher effective temperatures.

Question 5

Why does the size of the convective core increase during core Helium burning?

Correct! Carbon-Oxygen ions have higher effective charges ($Z^2$) than Helium ions, which increases core opacity and drives convection.

Question 6

What defines a semiconvective region in a Helium-burning stellar core?

Correct! Semiconvection is a partially mixed transition layer where the radiative gradient matches the adiabatic gradient ($\nabla_{\text{rad}} \approx \nabla_{\text{ad}}$).

Question 7

What is the critical boundary mass M^up separating WD final states from core collapse supernovae?

Correct! At $M^{\text{up}} \approx 8 M_\odot$, Carbon-Oxygen cores remain non-degenerate and contract to ignite Carbon and subsequent heavier elements.

Question 8

Why does an extremely thin burning shell undergo thermonuclear runaway even if the gas is non-degenerate?

Correct! When $4s/r < \alpha$, expansion is geometrically restricted, meaning expansion cannot regulate temperature and triggers a thermonuclear runaway.

Question 9

How does a star become a Carbon Star (C/O > 1) along the AGB?

Correct! This is the Third Dredge-Up, which dredges up Carbon-12 produced by Helium shell flashes to the stellar surface.

Question 10

What is the Cameron-Fowler mechanism in Hot Bottom Burning stars?

Correct! Rapid convective transport is required to move Beryllium-7 to cooler layers ($T < 2 \times 10^6\text{ K}$) before it can capture a proton, allowing stable Lithium-7 to form.
